# Review: CausalBound — Verified Worst-Case Systemic Risk Bounds via Decomposed Causal Polytope Inference with Adversarial Search

**Reviewer:** Yuan Cheng (Probabilistic Modeling & Stochastic Processes)
**Score: 8/10**

---

CausalBound's probabilistic architecture is impressively rigorous in its treatment of causal polytope LP formulations. The decision to optimize over the full set of joint distributions consistent with a structural causal model's DAG factorization—rather than committing to a single parametric family—represents a meaningful advance over the point-estimate paradigm of DebtRank and its extensions. The column generation scheme for the causal polytope LP is well-motivated: for subgraphs with n ≤ 50 binary variables and treewidth ≤ 12, the pricing subproblem decomposes along junction-tree cliques, keeping the active column count polynomial in n for fixed treewidth. This is a sound application of the Balke-Pearl (1997) partial identification framework scaled to network settings via Mauá-de Campos credal network LP techniques. The PAC-optimal convergence guarantee for the causal UCB variant—requiring T = O(|Σ_eff| / ε² · log(|Σ_eff|/δ)) rollouts—is correctly derived from standard bandit concentration arguments, and the use of d-separation to prune the effective action space from ~10,000 to ~1,000 interface configurations is a clever exploitation of graphical model structure that I have not seen applied to MCTS before.

However, several probabilistic modeling choices warrant scrutiny. The adaptive discretization of continuous financial instrument payoffs (CDS, IRS, equity options) into finite-cardinality CPDs with k = 4–6 states per variable is a significant approximation whose tail-behavior implications are insufficiently analyzed. Systemic risk is driven precisely by tail events—the 99.9th percentile of contagion loss—and coarse discretization can systematically underestimate tail probability mass. The problem statement acknowledges this ("tail-preserving quantization") but does not provide formal guarantees that the discretization error in the tails is bounded. For CDS protection legs, which are discontinuous functions of default time, the piecewise-constant approximation at k = 5 could introduce discretization artifacts that propagate through the junction tree and compound across subgraphs. I would want to see a formal discretization error bound: something like |E_exact[Loss] - E_discrete[Loss]| ≤ O(k^{-α}) for some rate α depending on the smoothness class of the CPDs, with explicit treatment of the discontinuous CDS case. The Monte Carlo ground truth with 10M samples provides an empirical check, but 10M samples may be insufficient to accurately estimate the 99.9th percentile of heavy-tailed loss distributions—the standard error of the 99.9th percentile quantile estimator at 10M samples is still non-trivial for fat-tailed distributions with tail index α < 3.

The Bound Composition Theorem is the mathematical centerpiece, and its probabilistic content deserves careful evaluation. The claim that the composition gap Δ ≤ O(k · L · s · ε) when the contagion function has Lipschitz constant L across separator variables of size s with discretization granularity ε is plausible but requires assumptions about the joint distribution's behavior at separator boundaries that may not hold for heavy-tailed financial distributions. Specifically, the Lipschitz condition on contagion loss as a function of separator variables assumes a smoothness that margin spirals and fire-sale externalities—which exhibit threshold effects and positive feedback loops—may violate. The composition theorem should explicitly characterize what happens when the Lipschitz condition fails: does the gap degrade gracefully (e.g., O(k · s · ε^{1/2}) for Hölder-continuous losses) or does it become vacuous? The stated target of bound ratio ≤ 1.5 for tw ≤ 12 is ambitious; achieving this would demonstrate that the decomposition cost is practically manageable, but without formal discretization error bounds and composition gap analysis for non-Lipschitz losses, the tightness claim rests primarily on empirical validation against Monte Carlo, which is necessary but not sufficient for a system whose value proposition is *provable* bounds.
