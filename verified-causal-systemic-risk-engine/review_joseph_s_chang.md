# Review: CausalBound — Verified Worst-Case Systemic Risk Bounds via Decomposed Causal Polytope Inference with Adversarial Search

**Reviewer:** Joseph S. Chang (Automated Reasoning & Proof Theory)
**Score: 8/10**

---

CausalBound's approach to proof-theoretic soundness is among the most carefully designed I have seen in a systems-oriented research proposal. The streaming SMT co-routine's incremental assertion protocol—where each junction-tree message-passing step generates a QF_LRA assertion that is pushed onto Z3's/CVC5's assertion stack and checked incrementally—mirrors the structure of proof-carrying computation. The soundness theorem (New Mathematics #4) formalizes the key property: if every prefix context C_t = C_{t-1} ∧ a_t is satisfiable, then the final inference result is correct with respect to the stated SCM. This is structurally a cut-elimination argument in disguise—the incremental assertions correspond to lemmas in a proof, and the soundness theorem states that the proof composed from these lemmas is valid if each lemma is individually valid in context. The analogy to Gentzen's Hauptsatz is imperfect (SMT satisfiability is not exactly cut-free provability), but the logical structure is sound. The use of QF_LRA—a decidable theory with well-understood proof theory and efficient decision procedures based on the simplex method—keeps the trusted computing base (TCB) small: the soundness of the certificates depends only on the correctness of the SMT solver's QF_LRA theory solver, not on the correctness of the junction-tree implementation, the LP solver, or the MCTS engine.

The TCB analysis deserves careful examination. The system's formal guarantees depend on a chain of trust: (1) the SMT solver correctly implements QF_LRA satisfiability checking, (2) the assertion encoding faithfully represents the inference claims, (3) the causal polytope LP correctly encodes the set of distributions consistent with the SCM. Components (1) and (3) are well-understood—Z3 and CVC5 have been extensively tested and used in safety-critical applications, and LP formulations of causal polytopes follow established techniques from Balke-Pearl. Component (2)—the assertion encoding—is the critical link and the primary source of TCB risk. The encoding must translate numerical potential table operations (floating-point arithmetic in the junction-tree engine) into exact real-arithmetic assertions (QF_LRA uses rationals or algebraic reals). This translation introduces a potential soundness gap: if the junction-tree engine computes in IEEE 754 double precision but the assertions claim exact real-arithmetic properties, the proof certifies the exact computation, not the floating-point approximation. The problem statement's SIMD vectorization (AVX-512) exacerbates this—SIMD operations may use fused multiply-add (FMA) instructions that produce different rounding than scalar operations. To close this gap, the assertion encoding should either use interval arithmetic (encoding each assertion as a bound on the true value given bounded rounding error) or the junction-tree engine should use rational arithmetic (exact but slower). The problem statement does not address this floating-point/exact-arithmetic gap, which is a standard concern in formally verified numerical computation.

The Bound Composition Theorem (New Mathematics #2) is the most significant proof-theoretic contribution, and its logical structure is worth analyzing. The theorem composes local bounds into global bounds, which is a form of compositional reasoning analogous to assume-guarantee reasoning in concurrent verification (Jones 1983, Misra-Chandy 1981). The key question is whether the composition is *tight*: the composition gap Δ = |B| - |B*| measures the price of decomposition, analogous to the completeness gap in abstract interpretation. The stated bound Δ ≤ O(k · L · s · ε) under Lipschitz conditions is a concrete quantitative result, but the conditions under which Δ → 0 are more interesting from a proof-theoretic perspective. If the contagion function decomposes exactly along separator sets (each subgraph's contribution to total loss depends only on its internal variables and the separator values), then Δ = 0—the decomposition is exact. This is the analogue of "no interference" in concurrent verification. The interesting case is when contagion exhibits cross-boundary effects beyond the separator variables (analogous to interference), and the theorem must characterize how the composition gap grows with the degree of cross-boundary coupling. This is genuinely novel mathematics with clear proof-theoretic structure, and I regard it as the strongest intellectual contribution of the proposal.
