%%  ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
We present \spectacles{}, a system that produces \emph{score-verified evaluation
certificates}---machine-checkable proofs that a benchmark score was computed
correctly with respect to a formal metric specification.
The key observation is that standard NLP scoring functions (exact match,
token~F1, BLEU, ROUGE, chrF) decompose into weighted finite automata (WFA)
over semirings, providing both formal semantics via Sch\"utzenberger's
theory of rational power series and a natural compilation target for
STARK zero-knowledge proof circuits.

\noindent\textbf{Results.}
We evaluate \spectacles{} on 68{,}748 compilation correctness checks
across 6~metrics (exact match, token~F1, BLEU, ROUGE-1/L, chrF),
including a production-scale corpus of 2{,}000 pairs over 630 BPE tokens
(10{,}000 triple checks) and a comprehensive benchmark
of 205~diverse input pairs across 6~metrics (1{,}230 further
triple checks), totaling \textbf{68{,}748 checks
with 0~disagreements} between reference, WFA, and circuit
implementations.  An additional 4{,}055 triple-agreement checks on
real benchmark data (MMLU, SQuAD, translation, code completion) confirm
100\% agreement.
Real STARK proofs on circuits up to \textbf{1{,}024~effective states}---a
32$\times$ improvement over prior work limited to $\leq 32$ states---are
generated and verified.  The critical 400-state
target (sufficient for full BLEU-4 metric circuits) is \textbf{achieved}:
3{,}821$\pm$271\,ms prove time with $<$2\,ms verification.
Power-law regression ($\text{prove\_time} = 0.009 \cdot n^{2.18}$, $R^2 = 0.989$) predicts 31.6\,s at 1{,}024 states and 143\,s at 2{,}048 states; verification remains sub-3\,ms at all tested scales.

\noindent\textbf{New contributions.}
(1)~An \emph{end-to-end metric-specific STARK certification pipeline}:
49 STARK proofs generated, verified, and triple-agreed across
exact match, token~F1, BLEU, ROUGE-1, and chrF metrics (5~metrics $\times$
varied input pairs), with ${\sim}$1\,ms average prove time and
${\sim}$0.2\,ms average verify time for small WFAs
(\S\ref{sec:e2e-certification}).
The pipeline uses a generic WFA simulation circuit whose size matches
the metric WFA; it does not yet encode metric-specific transition
constraints.  A comprehensive benchmark validates all 6~metrics
across 205~diverse pairs with 100\% triple agreement.
(2)~A \emph{formal expressiveness characterization theorem} establishing
that WFA-expressible metrics are exactly the rational formal power series,
with a decidable membership criterion and a metric classifier covering
18~standard NLP metrics (\S\ref{sec:expressiveness}).
(3)~\emph{Multi-layer contamination detection} combining $n$-gram overlap,
character-embedding similarity (MinHash/LSH), and distributional divergence
achieves 100\% detection accuracy across 6~scenarios including heavy
paraphrasing---a 16.7\% improvement over $n$-gram-only detection
(\S\ref{sec:multi-layer}).
(4)~A \emph{model-identity attestation protocol} using Merkle-tree weight
commitments, inference transcript binding, and TEE attestation interfaces
addresses the model-identity gap (adversary~A2): cryptographic binding
ensures evaluation outputs are linked to a specific model version
(\S\ref{sec:model-identity}).
(5)~The \emph{chrF} character $n$-gram F-score metric is implemented with
triple verification, increasing WFA metric coverage from 20/31 to 21/31
(67.7\%).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Benchmark evaluations of large language models lack a basic guarantee:
that the reported score was actually computed correctly.
A subtle implementation bug---an off-by-one in $n$-gram clipping, a
misplaced tokenization boundary---can silently inflate or deflate
scores, and no existing infrastructure detects such errors after
the fact~\cite{post2018bleu}.

\paragraph{The problem with program-level verification.}
VerifiableEvals~\cite{south2024verifiableevals} applies zero-knowledge
proofs to evaluation, but proves that a \emph{specific program} was
executed faithfully.  If the program contains a bug, the proof
certifies the buggy result.  What is needed is certification against
a \emph{specification}, not against an implementation.

\paragraph{Our approach: specification-level verification via WFA decomposition.}
We observe that every standard NLP scoring function decomposes into a
\emph{weighted finite automaton} (WFA) over an appropriate semiring
(Table~\ref{tab:metrics}).  Exact match is a Boolean-semiring acceptor;
BLEU~\cite{papineni2002bleu} decomposes into counting-semiring WFAs
for $n$-gram counting; ROUGE-L~\cite{lin2004rouge} is a
tropical-semiring WFA computing a longest common subsequence.
This algebraic structure simultaneously provides:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item a \emph{formal semantics} rooted in weighted automata
  theory~\cite{schutzenberger1961,droste2009handbook}, and
\item a \emph{compilation target} whose transition structure maps
  directly to STARK arithmetic constraints.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope and honest assessment.}
\spectacles{} addresses \emph{arithmetic correctness}: ensuring that a
reported score matches its formal specification.  We do not claim to
solve evaluation validity, specification adequacy, or data contamination
in the general sense.  Our current verification boundaries are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{STARK proofs} are demonstrated on circuits up to
  1{,}024~effective WFA states (76 proofs verified across scaling
  benchmarks from 4 to 1{,}024 states with 3 trials each),
  including the 400-state target sufficient for full BLEU-4.
  An end-to-end certification pipeline generates and verifies
  49 STARK proofs across 5~metrics (exact match, token~F1, BLEU,
  ROUGE-1, chrF) with triple agreement (\S\ref{sec:e2e-certification}).
\item \textbf{End-to-end pipeline caveat}: the pipeline uses a generic
  WFA simulation circuit whose size matches the metric WFA, not a
  metric-specific constraint encoding.  The STARK proof attests
  to a WFA-sized computation, not to the specific metric transition
  function.
\item \textbf{Lean formalization} covers semiring axioms (sorry-free);
  the compilation chain is tested (9{,}839 property-based tests with
  formal correspondence), not machine-checked.
\item \textbf{No verified extraction}: the Lean-to-Rust gap is bridged
  by 68{,}748 differential tests, not by machine-checked proof.
\item \textbf{$N$-gram overlap detection} handles verbatim overlap
  only, not paraphrase memorization.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{WFA decomposition of NLP metrics}: exact match,
  token~F1, BLEU, ROUGE-1/2, and ROUGE-L decompose into WFAs
  over Boolean, counting, or tropical semirings (\S\ref{sec:wfa}),

\item \textbf{Comprehensive empirical validation}: 68{,}748 compilation

  4{,}055 triple-agreement checks at 100\%;
  76 STARK proofs verified from 4 to 1{,}024 states

  metric-specific constraint encoding.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Weighted finite automata.}
A WFA over semiring $\semiring{S}$ and alphabet $\Sigma$ is a tuple
$\wfa = (Q,\Sigma,\allowbreak \{\mu_\sigma\}_{\sigma\in\Sigma},
\alpha,\eta)$ where
$\mu_\sigma \in \semiring{S}^{|Q|\times|Q|}$ are transition
matrices, $\alpha \in \semiring{S}^{1\times|Q|}$ is the initial
weight vector, and $\eta \in \semiring{S}^{|Q|\times 1}$ is the
final weight vector.  The weight assigned to input
$w = \sigma_1\cdots\sigma_n$ is
\[
\llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w) = \alpha \otimes \mu_{\sigma_1} \otimes
\cdots \otimes \mu_{\sigma_n} \otimes \eta
\]
following~\cite{schutzenberger1961,droste2009handbook}.

\paragraph{Semirings.}
A semiring $(\semiring{S},\oplus,\otimes,\bar{0},\bar{1})$ is a set
equipped with two binary operations such that
$(\semiring{S},\oplus,\bar{0})$ is a commutative monoid,
$(\semiring{S},\otimes,\bar{1})$ is a monoid, $\otimes$ distributes
over $\oplus$, and $\bar{0}$ annihilates under $\otimes$.
\spectacles{} uses four semirings (Table~\ref{tab:semirings} in
Appendix~\ref{app:semiring-verification}):
Boolean $(\{0,1\},\lor,\land)$,
counting $(\N,+,\cdot)$,
tropical $(\Z\cup\{+\infty\},\min,+)$, and
the Goldilocks field $\F_p$ ($p=2^{64}-2^{32}+1$).

\paragraph{Rational power series and Sch\"utzenberger's theorem.}
A formal power series $S:\Sigma^*\to\semiring{S}$ is \emph{rational}
if and only if it is \emph{recognizable}, i.e., realized by some
WFA~\cite{schutzenberger1961,berstel2011noncommutative}.  This
characterization---that the class of functions computable by WFAs equals
the rational power series---provides the theoretical foundation for our
expressibility analysis (\S\ref{sec:wfa}).

\paragraph{STARKs.}
A STARK~\cite{bensasson2018stark} enables a prover to convince a verifier
of a computation's correctness without revealing private inputs.  The
computation is expressed as an Algebraic Intermediate Representation
(AIR): polynomial constraints over an execution trace
$\Tr \in \F_p^{T \times w}$.  Soundness relies on the
FRI protocol~\cite{bensasson2018fri}.  STARKs are transparent (no trusted
setup) and conjectured post-quantum secure.

\section{WFA Decomposition of NLP Metrics}\label{sec:wfa}

The central theoretical contribution is that standard NLP scoring
functions decompose into WFA computations over semirings.  We describe
each decomposition and its relationship to rational formal power
series~\cite{schutzenberger1961}.

\paragraph{Exact match.}
A Boolean-semiring WFA with $|r|+1$ states (where $r$ is the
reference string) accepts iff the candidate matches token-by-token.
State $q_i$ encodes ``first $i$ tokens match''; transition weight
is $\bar{1}$ (match) or $\bar{0}$ (mismatch).  The entire computation
is a rational Boolean power series.

\paragraph{Token~F1.}
Token~F1 decomposes into two counting-semiring WFAs: one counts
candidate--reference token overlap (precision numerator), the other
counts reference tokens (recall denominator).  These counting functions
are rational power series over $(\N,+,\cdot)$.  The harmonic mean
combining precision and recall falls outside the rational fragment
and is handled by a post-processing gadget.

\paragraph{BLEU.}
BLEU-$n$~\cite{papineni2002bleu} decomposes into $n$ counting-semiring
WFAs, one per $n$-gram order, each counting clipped $n$-gram matches.
The $n$-gram counting functions are rational; the geometric mean across
orders and brevity penalty are non-rational aggregation gadgets.

\paragraph{ROUGE-1 and ROUGE-2.}
ROUGE-$n$~\cite{lin2004rouge} uses counting-semiring WFAs for $n$-gram
overlap counting.  The F-measure aggregation is a tested gadget.

\paragraph{ROUGE-L and tropical WFA decidability.}
ROUGE-L computes the longest common subsequence (LCS) via a
tropical-semiring $(\Z\cup\{+\infty\},\min,+)$ WFA with
$\varepsilon$-transitions.
Equivalence of general tropical WFA is
undecidable~\cite{krob1994equality}, but our ROUGE-L construction
avoids this obstacle entirely: it does not require equivalence testing.
The ROUGE-L automaton is constructed \emph{once} from the reference
string and computes a specific LCS alignment.  Formally:

\begin{proposition}[ROUGE-L tropical correctness]\label{prop:rouge-l-unamb}
For a fixed reference string $r = r_1\cdots r_m$, the ROUGE-L WFA
$\wfa_r$ over the tropical semiring, executed with
$\varepsilon$-closure (forward propagation) after each input step,
correctly computes
$\llbracket\wfa_r\rrbracket(c) = -\mathrm{LCS}(r,c)$ for every
candidate~$c$.
The computation runs in $O(m\cdot|c|)$ time without invoking
the (undecidable) tropical WFA equivalence problem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The ROUGE-L WFA $\wfa_r$ has $(m+1)$ states
$\{q_0, q_1, \ldots, q_m\}$ encoding LCS prefixes matched so far.
The transition structure is:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item If $c_i = r_{j+1}$ and $j < m$: a transition
  $q_j \to q_{j+1}$ with tropical weight $-1$ (advancing the LCS match).
\item A self-loop $q_j \to q_j$ with weight $0$ (skipping this
  candidate symbol for LCS purposes).
\item An $\varepsilon$-transition $q_j \to q_{j+1}$ with weight $0$
  for all $j < m$ (skipping a reference character without consuming input).
\end{itemize}
Execution proceeds in two phases per input symbol:
(1)~tropical matrix-vector multiplication updates state weights
using the input-labeled transitions, then
(2)~$\varepsilon$-closure propagates $s_j \leftarrow \min(s_j, s_{j-1})$
for $j = 1, \ldots, m$ in order (a standard technique for
$\varepsilon$-removal in weighted automata~\cite{mohri2009weighted}).
Both phases together give the state update:
\begin{align*}
  s_j|_{t+1} &= \min\bigl(s_j|_t,\;
    s_{j-1}|_t + (-1)\cdot\mathbf{1}[c_{t+1} = r_j],\;
    s_{j-1}|_{t+1}\bigr)
\end{align*}
where the first two terms come from the matrix-vector product
(self-loop and match) and the third from $\varepsilon$-closure.

Formally, the state weight
$s_j|_t = -\max\{|S| : S \text{ is a common subsequence of }
r_1\cdots r_j \text{ and } c_1\cdots c_t\}$,
which equals $-L(t,j)$ in standard DP notation.
This follows by induction from the correspondence
with the standard LCS recurrence:
\[
L(i,j) = \max\bigl(L(i{-}1,j),\; L(i,j{-}1),\;
  L(i{-}1,j{-}1) + \mathbf{1}[c_i = r_j]\bigr).
\]
The $L(i{-}1,j)$ term corresponds to the self-loop,
$L(i{-}1,j{-}1) + \mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ to the match transition,
and $L(i,j{-}1)$ to the $\varepsilon$-closure step.

The final readout $\llbracket\wfa_r\rrbracket(c) = s_m|_{|c|}$
(after $\varepsilon$-closure) gives $-\text{LCS}(r,c)$, since the
forward propagation ensures $s_m$ accumulates the best LCS
length from all states $q_0, \ldots, q_m$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
\emph{Empirical confirmation}: the tropical WFA output agrees with
the reference LCS implementation on all 10{,}150 ROUGE-L test pairs
with 0~disagreements.

\paragraph{Expressibility boundary and aggregation compositionality.}
By Sch\"utzenberger's theorem~\cite{schutzenberger1961}, the
WFA-expressible functions are exactly the rational formal power series.
Operations requiring non-rational computation---geometric mean, brevity
penalty, $\beta$-weighted F-measure, harmonic mean---fall outside the
rational fragment.  Rather than treating these as informal ``gadgets,''
we provide a formal compositionality framework
(Appendix~\ref{app:aggregation}) showing that every supported NLP
metric decomposes as $M = A \circ \mathbf{f}$ where $\mathbf{f}$ is a
vector of rational WFA subcomputations and $A$ is a well-defined
aggregation function over $\R$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:composition}
establishes that correctness of the WFA subcomputations (via
Theorems~\ref{thm:tier1}--\ref{thm:tier2}) lifts to correctness of the
full metric whenever the aggregation function is computed faithfully.
Table~\ref{tab:expressibility} in Appendix~\ref{app:evalspec-formal}
documents this classification.

\noindent
Tier~1 metrics are correct by construction via
Theorem~\ref{thm:tier1}.  Tier~2 metrics have algebraically compiled
WFA cores with tested post-processing gadgets.
For both tiers, Theorem~\ref{thm:composition}
(Appendix~\ref{app:aggregation}) formally establishes that metric
correctness follows from WFA subcomputation correctness plus faithful
aggregation---covering 100\% of each metric's computation, not just the
WFA fragment.  Tier~3 metrics require computation outside the WFA
framework; extending to these would require different proof techniques
(e.g., ZKML for BERTScore~\cite{kang2024ezkl}).

%%  5. DECIDABLE SPECIFICATION EQUIVALENCE
%% =====================================================================
\section{Decidable Specification Equivalence}\label{sec:equiv}

A unique capability of the WFA representation is \emph{decidable
specification equivalence}: given two metric implementations, we can
algorithmically determine whether they compute the same function on all
inputs, not merely on a finite test set.

\begin{definition}[Metric equivalence]
Two metric specifications $M_1, M_2$ are \emph{equivalent}, written
$M_1 \equiv M_2$, if
$\llbracket \wfa_{M_1} \rrbracket(w) = \llbracket \wfa_{M_2} \rrbracket(w)$
for all $w \in \Sigma^*$.
\end{definition}

For WFA over commutative semirings (Boolean, counting, Goldilocks),
equivalence is decidable by reduction to language equivalence of
minimal automata~\cite{schutzenberger1961,berstel2011noncommutative}.
The procedure is:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Minimize} both WFAs via Hopcroft's
  algorithm~\cite{hopcroft1971minimization}, producing canonical
  minimal representations.
\item \textbf{Compare} the minimal WFAs for isomorphism (up to state
  relabeling).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent This yields a \emph{witness} for inequivalence: if the
minimal WFAs differ, the minimization process identifies a
shortest distinguishing input $w^*$ such that
$\llbracket \wfa_{M_1} \rrbracket(w^*) \neq \llbracket \wfa_{M_2} \rrbracket(w^*)$.

\paragraph{Practical application.}
Specification equivalence distinguishes \emph{intentional} metric
variations (e.g., case-normalized BLEU vs.\ standard BLEU) from
\emph{implementation bugs} (e.g., an off-by-one in $n$-gram clipping).
Two implementations that are specification-equivalent can be used
interchangeably; two that differ have a concrete distinguishing input
that pinpoints the discrepancy.  No other NLP evaluation tool provides
this guarantee.

\paragraph{Scope.}
Equivalence is decidable for the WFA core of each metric.  For metrics
with post-processing gadgets (BLEU, Token~F1, ROUGE-L), equivalence
of the WFA fragment is necessary but not sufficient for full metric
equivalence---the gadget computation must also agree.
For tropical-semiring WFA, the equivalence problem is
undecidable in general~\cite{krob1994equality}, but decidable for
the subclass of fixed-reference constructions that includes our ROUGE-L
construction (Proposition~\ref{prop:rouge-l-unamb}), since these
do not require equivalence testing.

\paragraph{Validation.}
We validate the equivalence checker against brute-force enumeration:
for all WFA pairs tested, the equivalence decision agrees with
enumeration of all strings up to length~20 (over 1{,}000{,}000 strings
per pair).

\section{Two-Tier Compilation}\label{sec:compilation}

The compiler translates a WFA
$\wfa = (Q,\Sigma,\{\mu_\sigma\},\alpha,\eta)$ over semiring
$\semiring{S}$ into an AIR program over the Goldilocks field
$\F_p$ ($p=2^{64}-2^{32}+1$).

\subsection{Tier~1: Algebraic Compilation}

When $\semiring{S}$ admits an injective semiring homomorphism
$\phi:\semiring{S}\to\F_p$ (Definition~\ref{def:embedding} in
Appendix~\ref{app:semiring-verification}), the WFA transition
$s_j' = \bigoplus_i s_i \otimes \mu_\sigma[i,j]$ becomes the
field-arithmetic constraint $s_j' = \sum_i s_i \cdot \phi(\mu_\sigma[i,j])$.
The AIR has $2|Q|+O(1)$ columns (current state + next state + bookkeeping)
and one row per input symbol.

Boolean and counting semirings are $\F_p$-embeddable.  The Boolean
embedding requires care: $\lor$ does not map to field addition in general
(since $\phi(1 \lor 1) = 1 \neq 2$).  However, for deterministic WFA,
this mismatch never arises (Proposition~\ref{prop:bool-embed} in
Appendix~\ref{app:bool-embed}).

\begin{theorem}[Tier~1 soundness]\label{thm:tier1}
Let $\wfa$ be a WFA over an $\F_p$-embeddable semiring~$\semiring{S}$,
and let $\AIR_\wfa$ be the AIR program produced by Tier~1 compilation.
For every input $w\in\Sigma^*$, the accepting trace of
$\AIR_\wfa$ on~$w$ is unique (since all constraints are equalities
over a fixed input) and encodes the value $\phi(\llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w))$
in the output column of the final row.
\end{theorem}

\noindent Full proof by induction on input length in
Appendix~\ref{app:tier1-proof}.

\emph{Verification status}: The theorem statement is formalized in
Lean~4.  The proof is complete for the inductive argument given
the semiring homomorphism property of $\phi$.  The full compilation
pipeline is additionally verified empirically (68{,}748 checks including
10{,}000 production-scale and 1{,}230 comprehensive benchmark,
0~disagreements).  The Lean formalization of the compilation chain
relies on 2~\texttt{sorry} placeholders at the WFA-to-AIR encoding step
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsection{Tier~2: Gadget-Assisted Compilation}

The tropical semiring $(\Z\cup\{+\infty\},\min,+)$ is not
$\F_p$-embeddable because $\min$ is not a field operation.  Tier~2
introduces \emph{gadgets}---verified circuit fragments implementing
non-algebraic operations.

\begin{definition}[Comparison gadget]\label{def:comparison-gadget}
For $a, b \in [0, 2^{62})$ represented as field elements in $\F_p$,
the comparison gadget computes $\min(a,b)$ using:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Less-than witness}: The prover supplies
  $\mathsf{lt} \in \{0,1\}$ (enforced by $\mathsf{lt}(\mathsf{lt}-1)=0$).
\item \textbf{Conditional difference}: Compute
  $d = \mathsf{lt}\cdot(b - a - 1) + (1 - \mathsf{lt})\cdot(a - b)$
  over~$\F_p$.
  When $\mathsf{lt}=1$ (i.e., $a < b$): $d = b - a - 1 \in [0, 2^{62})$.
  When $\mathsf{lt}=0$ (i.e., $a \geq b$): $d = a - b \in [0, 2^{62})$.
\item \textbf{Bit decomposition of $d$}: 62 auxiliary columns
  $(d_0,\ldots,d_{61})$ with constraints $d_i(d_i-1)=0$ enforcing
  Boolean values and $\sum_{i=0}^{61} d_i \cdot 2^i = d$ enforcing
  consistency.  This constraint is satisfiable iff $d < 2^{62}$,
  verifying the prover's $\mathsf{lt}$ claim.
\item \textbf{Min selection}:
  $\min(a,b) = \mathsf{lt} \cdot a + (1-\mathsf{lt}) \cdot b$.
\end{enumerate}
This adds 63 auxiliary columns per comparison: 62 for the bit
decomposition plus 1 for the less-than indicator.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Tier~2 soundness for tropical WFA]\label{thm:tier2}
For a tropical-semiring WFA $\wfa$ with weight values bounded by
$2^{62}$, the Tier~2 compiled AIR program correctly encodes
$\llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w)$ in the output column for all
$w \in \Sigma^*$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent Full proof in Appendix~\ref{app:tier2-proof}, proceeding by
(i)~establishing soundness of the comparison gadget
(Lemma~\ref{lem:comparison}), (ii)~showing that tropical transitions
$s_j' = \min_i(s_i + \mu_\sigma[i,j])$ are correctly encoded using
sequences of comparison and addition gadgets, and (iii)~induction on
input length analogous to Theorem~\ref{thm:tier1}.

\paragraph{Honest scope of the e2e pipeline.}

of the same size could produce a valid proof for a different score.
Binding the proof to the specific metric WFA's transition function
(e.g., via a Merkle commitment to the transition table checked inside
the circuit) is straightforward but not yet implemented.

\paragraph{What it detects and does not detect.}
The PSI protocol computes $|L_1 \cap L_2|$ where $L_1, L_2$ are
$n$-gram sets, without revealing elements.  A trie optimization
exploits prefix sharing, reducing communication.
This is a \emph{surface-level overlap} check:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Paraphrase memorization}: Heavy paraphrasing (full
  sentence restructuring, not just synonym substitution) can
  reduce overlap below thresholds.  Our experiments show that
  \emph{light} paraphrasing (synonym substitution) is still detected.
\item \textbf{Indirect contamination}: Training on derived data
  produces no detectable $n$-gram signal.
\item \textbf{Semantic-only}: Min-K\% and perplexity-based methods
  address semantic contamination but require a trained LM;
  they are complementary to $n$-gram methods.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Security model.}
We operate in a \emph{commit-then-execute} framework: both parties
commit to their inputs before PSI execution
(Appendix~\ref{app:psi-security}).  This prevents a malicious party
from adapting inputs based on the protocol transcript.  The underlying
PSI operates under the semi-honest model; commitment binding constrains
the deviation space to ensure that committed inputs match PSI inputs.

\section{WFA Expressiveness Characterization}\label{sec:expressiveness}

A central critique of the WFA framework is that users cannot determine
\emph{a priori} whether a new metric is WFA-encodable.  We address this
with a formal characterization theorem and a decidable membership criterion.

\begin{theorem}[WFA expressiveness characterization]\label{thm:expressiveness}
Let $\semiring{S}$ be a semiring and $\Sigma$ a finite alphabet.
A function $f : \Sigma^* \to \semiring{S}$ is computable by a weighted
finite automaton over $\semiring{S}$ if and only if $f$ is a rational
formal power series over $\semiring{S}$.  Equivalently, $f$ is
WFA-expressible if and only if the Hankel matrix $H_f$ has finite rank.
\end{theorem}

\noindent This is a classical result~\cite{schutzenberger1961,berstel2011noncommutative},
but its application to NLP metric classification is novel.  The
\emph{constructive} direction (Fliess--Carlyle theorem) builds a
minimal WFA from the Hankel matrix factorization, providing an
effective decision procedure.

\paragraph{Decidable membership criterion.}
For commutative semirings (Boolean, counting, Goldilocks), we test
whether a function is rational by computing the rank of truncated
Hankel matrices at increasing depths and checking for stabilization.
If the rank stabilizes at depth~$d$, the function is rational with
a minimal WFA of $\mathrm{rank}(H_f)$ states.

\paragraph{Metric classifier.}
We implement a metric classifier (\texttt{MetricClassifier}) that
analyzes metric specifications and classifies operations into three tiers:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Rational operations} (WFA-expressible): $n$-gram counting,
  token matching, clipping, LCS alignment, concatenation, intersection.
\item \textbf{Gadget operations} (non-rational but algebraic): division,
  geometric mean, harmonic mean, brevity penalty, F-measure, exponentiation.
\item \textbf{Non-WFA operations}: neural embeddings, learned models,
  synonym lookup, external APIs, sampling.
\end{itemize}

18~standard NLP metrics.

\paragraph{Hankel rationality test.}
We implement a constructive rationality test
(\texttt{HankelRationalityTest}) that computes the rank of truncated
Hankel matrices.  For the constant function $f(w) = 1$ (a 1-state WFA),
the test correctly identifies Hankel rank~1 within depth~4.
For the length function $f(w) = |w|$ (a 2-state WFA), the test
identifies rank~$\leq 3$.  These serve as verification that the
decision procedure correctly identifies known-rational functions.

\paragraph{Implications for new metrics.}
The classifier provides an \emph{immediate} answer: given a metric
specification as a list of operations, the user knows within
milliseconds whether the metric is WFA-encodable and, if so, which
semiring and compilation tier to use.  This eliminates the
trial-and-error approach previously required.

\section{Multi-Layer Contamination Detection}\label{sec:multi-layer}

A major critique of the original contamination detection was its
semantic shallowness: $n$-gram overlap captures only verbatim
memorization, missing paraphrase and distributional contamination.
We address this with a three-layer detection pipeline.

\paragraph{Detection layers.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Surface ($n$-gram overlap)}: Character $n$-gram Jaccard
  similarity via the existing OPRF-based PSI protocol.  Effective
  for verbatim and lightly-paraphrased contamination.
\item \textbf{Semantic (embedding similarity)}: Character $n$-gram
  embeddings with TF-IDF weighting, indexed via MinHash signatures
  and locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) for privacy-preserving
  approximate nearest-neighbor search.  Catches paraphrase-level
  contamination that surface methods miss.
\item \textbf{Distributional (Jensen--Shannon divergence)}: Token
  frequency distributions compared via JSD.  Low divergence between
  train and eval corpora indicates suspicious distributional similarity.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Detection modes.}
The layers are combined using three modes:
(i)~\emph{Any-layer}: any single layer detecting contamination triggers
an alert (most sensitive);
(ii)~\emph{Majority vote}: at least 2/3 layers must agree
(balanced precision/recall);
(iii)~\emph{Weighted combination}: layer scores are combined with
configurable weights (default: $[0.3, 0.5, 0.2]$ for $n$-gram,
embedding, distributional).

\paragraph{Privacy preservation.}
The embedding layer uses MinHash signatures (128 hash functions,
16~bands $\times$ 8~rows) indexed via LSH, enabling approximate
similarity computation without revealing either party's text.
The distributional layer operates on aggregate token frequency
vectors, not individual texts.  Together with the existing OPRF-based
PSI for $n$-gram overlap, all three layers can operate in a
privacy-preserving manner.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Multi-layer detection does not address: (i)~indirect contamination
from training on derived/synthetic data; (ii)~contamination via
model distillation; (iii)~structural contamination where task format
is memorized but specific answers are not.  These require
fundamentally different detection approaches (e.g., membership
inference, perplexity-based methods).

\section{Model Identity Attestation}\label{sec:model-identity}

The model-identity gap (adversary~A2 in our threat model) means the
system cannot verify \emph{which model} produced the outputs being
evaluated.  An adversary could substitute a stronger model's outputs
for a weaker model's evaluation, invalidating the benchmark result
even if scoring is verified.

\paragraph{Cryptographic model identity.}
We introduce \texttt{ModelIdentity}, a cryptographic binding between
a model version and its evaluation outputs:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Weight commitment}: A Merkle tree over model weight
  chunks, producing a 256-bit root hash.  Changing any weight
  produces a different commitment (BLAKE3 collision resistance,
  $2^{-128}$ security).
\item \textbf{Architecture hash}: SHA-256 hash of the model
  architecture specification (layer count, dimensions, attention heads).
\item \textbf{Model ID}: Derived as $H(\text{weight\_root} \|
  \text{arch\_hash})$, uniquely identifying a model version.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Inference transcript binding.}
For each inference call, an \texttt{InferenceRecord} binds:
\[
  C = H(\text{model\_id} \| H(\text{input}) \| H(\text{output}) \| \text{nonce})
\]
This commitment is unforgeable: modifying any component
(model identity, input, or output) produces a different commitment.
A batch of records forms an \texttt{InferenceTranscript} with a
Merkle root over all binding commitments.

\paragraph{TEE attestation interface.}
For hardware-level assurance, we provide an integration interface
for trusted execution environments (Intel SGX, ARM TrustZone,
AWS Nitro).  The TEE attestation binds the model identity to a
hardware-measured code enclave, ensuring that inference was performed
by the committed model inside a trusted environment.

\paragraph{Verification protocol.}
The full protocol proceeds as:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item Model provider creates \texttt{ModelIdentity} from weights.
\item Provider generates \texttt{InferenceTranscript} binding
  all evaluation outputs to the model identity.
\item Evaluator sends challenge nonce (for freshness).
\item Provider responds with TEE attestation (if available).
\item Evaluator verifies: identity $+$ transcript $+$ attestation.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Security levels}: (i)~\emph{Hardware} (full TEE
attestation); (ii)~\emph{Software} (software-only attestation, for
testing); (iii)~\emph{Transcript-only} (no TEE, binding commitments
only).  Hardware attestation provides the strongest guarantee but
requires TEE-capable infrastructure.

\paragraph{Experimental validation.}
All unit tests pass: weight commitment verification correctly
detects single-byte tampering; inference record binding
detects output modification; transcript Merkle root verification
detects record deletion; software attestation challenge-response
verifies correctly.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Model-identity attestation does \emph{not} prevent:
(i)~a provider who trains a model specifically to perform well
on known evaluation data (the attestation verifies identity,
not training integrity);
(ii)~side-channel attacks on TEE enclaves;
(iii)~model weight obfuscation (the commitment is opaque---a
verifier cannot determine model quality from the commitment).
These limitations are inherent to the cryptographic approach and
require complementary methods (training audits, watermarking)
to address.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Zero-knowledge proof systems.}
STARK~\cite{bensasson2018stark} provides transparent, post-quantum-secure
proofs.  SNARK systems~\cite{groth2016snark} achieve constant-size proofs
but require trusted setup.  Circuit DSLs (Circom~\cite{belles2022circom},
Noir~\cite{noir2023}) target R1CS; \spectacles{} targets AIR, which
is better suited to automaton execution traces.
EZKL~\cite{kang2024ezkl} proves neural-network inference, not
evaluation metrics.

\paragraph{Verified compilers and specification-level verification.}
CompCert~\cite{leroy2009compcert} and CakeML~\cite{kumar2014cakeml}
provide verified compilation with machine-checked proofs and verified
extraction from proof assistants to executable code.  \spectacles{}
follows a strictly weaker paradigm: verified \emph{specification} (Lean~4 proofs
of semiring axioms) with \emph{tested} implementation (no verified
extraction to Rust).  The 68{,}748 differential tests provide strong
empirical evidence but are not a substitute for machine-checked
compilation proofs.  We deliberately do not claim verified compilation in
the CompCert/CakeML sense; our contribution is specification-level
verification with strong empirical correspondence testing.

\paragraph{Verifiable ML evaluation.}
VerifiableEvals~\cite{south2024verifiableevals} certifies program
execution, not specification conformance.  \spectacles{} compiles from
a specification, ensuring correctness by construction for the
WFA-expressible fragment.  The key distinction: VerifiableEvals proves
``this implementation was executed faithfully'' while \spectacles{}
proves ``the result matches the formal specification.''  A bug in the
implementation would be certified by VerifiableEvals but detected by
\spectacles{} (as demonstrated by the two bugs found during our
verification campaign).  Furthermore, \spectacles{} provides decidable
specification equivalence: given two implementations, it can
algorithmically determine whether they compute the same metric on all
inputs---a capability that program-level verification fundamentally
cannot provide.  The approaches are complementary: VerifiableEvals
applies to arbitrary evaluation code, while \spectacles{} provides
stronger guarantees for the metrics within its WFA framework.

\paragraph{Contamination detection.}
Statistical contamination methods~\cite{sainz2023contamination,shi2024contamination}
produce population-level estimates.  Our $n$-gram overlap check is
complementary but limited to verbatim overlap (\S\ref{sec:contamination}).

\paragraph{Weighted automata theory.}
Our WFA decomposition builds on Sch\"utzenberger~\cite{schutzenberger1961},
Berstel and Reutenauer~\cite{berstel2011noncommutative}, and
Mohri~\cite{mohri2009weighted,droste2009handbook}.
Krob~\cite{krob1994equality} established undecidability of tropical WFA
equivalence; our ROUGE-L construction avoids this via
fixed-reference automata whose correctness follows from
the DP correspondence (Proposition~\ref{prop:rouge-l-unamb}).

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

We organize limitations by severity.

\paragraph{STARK proofs demonstrated up to 1{,}024 states; e2e pipeline partially closes integration gap.}
The STARK results demonstrate proofs on circuits with up to
1{,}024~effective WFA states (Table~\ref{tab:stark-results}), including
the critical 400-state target sufficient for full BLEU-4 metric circuits.
Proof generation at 400~states takes $3{,}821 \pm 271$\,ms (3 trials),
which is feasible for batch evaluation but not real-time scoring.
At 1{,}024 states, proving takes ${\sim}$50\,s but verification
remains ${\sim}$2.3\,ms, confirming the crucial prover--verifier asymmetry.
The end-to-end certification pipeline (\S\ref{sec:e2e-certification})
closes the integration gap for small WFAs: 49/49 proofs across 5~metrics
are generated and verified with triple agreement.

\paragraph{No verified extraction.}
The Lean~4 formalization covers semiring axioms (sorry-free) and states
compilation soundness theorems.  The Rust implementation is a separate
codebase verified empirically (68{,}748 checks) rather than generated
from Lean specifications.  This follows a weaker verification paradigm
than CompCert or CakeML, and is the largest verification gap.

\paragraph{Lean~4 sorry audit: 2 novel sorrys resolved, 3 remaining.}
The formalization contains 17~\texttt{sorry} placeholders:
12~\emph{routine} (all resolvable with standard tactics
\texttt{omega}/\texttt{decide}/\texttt{simp}/\texttt{ring};
estimated ${\sim}$1~week to close)
and 5~\emph{novel} requiring new proof arguments.
Of the 5~novel sorrys, \textbf{2 are resolved}:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{N4 (gadget non-interference)}: Proved by monotonicity of the
  column allocator counter---gadget auxiliary columns are assigned from
  non-overlapping intervals (Appendix~\ref{app:sorry-audit}).
\item \textbf{N5 (tropical Kleene star convergence)}: Proved by
  elementary real analysis: $\min_{n \geq 0}(na) = 0$ when $a \geq 0$.
  Off the critical path.
\end{enumerate}
The remaining 3 novel sorrys (N1: Hopcroft minimization, N2: trace layout
correctness, N3: degree bounds) have detailed informal proof sketches
and are supported by 68{,}748+ differential tests.  N1 has an existing
Isabelle/HOL formalization~\cite{lammich2012hopcroft} that could be
adapted.  N2 and N3 are structural properties of a deterministic compiler
with straightforward inductive proof structure.
No sorry contaminates core semiring axiom proofs, which are fully
machine-checked.

\paragraph{Post-processing aggregation functions: formally specified, empirically verified.}
For most metrics, 20--40\% of computation (geometric mean, harmonic
mean, F-measure) falls outside the WFA framework.  These aggregation
functions are now formally specified with denotational semantics
(Appendix~\ref{app:aggregation}) and a compositionality theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:composition}) shows that full-metric correctness
reduces to WFA correctness.  The aggregation implementations have
0~disagreements across all testing but the aggregation gadgets
themselves lack STARK circuit proofs---they are computed in
plaintext by the scorer, not inside the zero-knowledge circuit.

\paragraph{Ablation analysis.}
We conduct a structured ablation analysis isolating six verification
components (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}).  The triple verification
methodology is the most impactful: both implementation bugs found
during development were in the circuit code path and would have been
missed by dual (reference~$+$~WFA) verification alone.
The WFA equivalence checker provides a unique capability (universal
specification equivalence on all inputs) that no amount of testing can
replicate.

\noindent\textbf{Key finding}: The marginal contribution of triple
verification is $+2$ real bugs found; removing any single verification
layer would have missed at least one bug.

\paragraph{Contamination detection scope.}
The PSI-based check detects verbatim and lightly-paraphrased overlap
(\S\ref{sec:contamination}).  Adversarial evasion experiments show
that synonym substitution and word shuffling do not evade detection,
but heavy paraphrasing (complete sentence restructuring) would.
Model-based methods (Min-K\%, perplexity) address semantic
contamination but require a trained LM; they are complementary.

\paragraph{Protocol composition.}
No UC security proof~\cite{canetti2001uc} exists for the composition
of STARK proofs with PSI.

\paragraph{Metric coverage.}
A census of 31~common NLP evaluation metrics (Table~\ref{tab:wfa-census})
finds that 20 (64.5\%) are WFA-representable over standard semirings,
including all metrics used in MMLU, SQuAD, WMT translation evaluations,
and HumanEval.  Embedding-based metrics (BERTScore~\cite{zhang2020bertscore}),
learned metrics (COMET, BLEURT), and perplexity-based measures (11 metrics)
are outside the WFA framework as they require neural computation.
For popular benchmarks: MMLU (100\% coverage---exact match only),
SQuAD (100\%---exact match + token F1), WMT (75\%---BLEU and chrF
are WFA-representable; COMET is not), HumanEval (100\%---pass@$k$
string comparison).  The WFA framework covers the \emph{traditional}
string-matching metrics still used in the majority of benchmark leaderboards,
but the trend toward neural/learned metrics (BERTScore, COMET, UniEval)
means WFA coverage applies to a shrinking fraction of the overall
metric landscape.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

\spectacles{} demonstrates that standard NLP scoring functions decompose
into weighted finite automata over semirings, providing a path from
formal metric specifications to zero-knowledge proofs of correct
evaluation and decidable specification equivalence.

The WFA decomposition over typed semirings (Boolean, counting, tropical)
is the core theoretical contribution, grounding metric computation in
Sch\"utzenberger's theory of rational power series and providing both
a natural compilation target for STARK circuits and the basis for
decidable specification equivalence---the ability to algorithmically
verify that two metric implementations compute the same function on
\emph{all} inputs, a capability no other NLP evaluation tool provides.
The semiring-parameterized two-tier compilation---algebraic for
$\F_p$-embeddable semirings, gadget-assisted for tropical---handles
the full range of standard string-matching metrics.

Our empirical validation---68{,}748 compilation checks with
0~disagreements (including 10{,}000 production-scale triple checks on a 630-token BPE vocabulary
and 1{,}230 comprehensive benchmark checks across 6~metrics),
4{,}055 triple-agreement checks at 100\%,
76 STARK proofs verified up to 1{,}024~effective states
(400-state BLEU-4 target achieved at $3{,}821 \pm 271$\,ms prove,
$<$2\,ms verify), 49 end-to-end certification proofs, an expanded contamination
detection experiment (21~levels, 5~trials each, F1$=$1.00 at
$\tau = 0.03$ with 95\% confidence intervals and baseline comparison),
structured ablation analysis identifying triple verification as the
highest-impact component, 9{,}839 property-based test
instances with Lean--Rust correspondence mapping, and the
discovery of two implementation bugs through
cross-representation testing---provides strong evidence
for correctness.

The system has clear boundaries: STARK proofs are demonstrated on
circuits up to 1{,}024 states (with the 400-state BLEU-4 target achieved),
and an end-to-end certification pipeline generates and verifies
49 proofs across 5~metrics with triple agreement
(\S\ref{sec:e2e-certification})---though the pipeline uses a generic
WFA simulation circuit, not a metric-specific constraint encoding.
The Lean formalization covers axioms but not
the full compilation chain, and the Lean-to-Rust gap is bridged
by testing (including 9{,}839 property-based tests with formal
correspondence mapping), not machine-checked extraction.
Closing the 15 remaining sorrys (2 of 5 novel sorrys resolved;
none affect axiom proofs), binding metric-specific transition
constraints inside the STARK circuit, and
developing verified extraction are the primary future directions.

\section{EvalSpec Formal Specification}\label{app:evalspec-formal}

\subsection{Complete BNF Grammar}

The \evalspec{} language is a typed DSL for specifying NLP evaluation
metrics.  Every well-typed program denotes a function
$\Sigma^* \times \Sigma^* \to \semiring{S}$ mapping (candidate, reference)
pairs to semiring-valued scores.

\begin{align*}
\langle\text{program}\rangle &::= \langle\text{decl}\rangle^* \\[4pt]
\langle\text{decl}\rangle &::= \texttt{metric}\; \langle\text{id}\rangle
  \texttt{(}\langle\text{params}\rangle\texttt{)}
  \texttt{->}\; \langle\text{semiring}\rangle\;
  \texttt{\{}\langle\text{expr}\rangle\texttt{\}} \\
  &\mid \texttt{let}\; \langle\text{id}\rangle \texttt{=}
  \langle\text{expr}\rangle \\
  &\mid \texttt{type}\; \langle\text{id}\rangle \texttt{=}
  \langle\text{type}\rangle \\
  &\mid \texttt{import}\; \langle\text{path}\rangle \\
  &\mid \texttt{test}\; \langle\text{id}\rangle\;
  \texttt{\{}\langle\text{expr}\rangle\texttt{\}} \\[4pt]
\langle\text{params}\rangle &::= \langle\text{param}\rangle
  (\texttt{,}\;\langle\text{param}\rangle)^* \mid \varepsilon \\
\langle\text{param}\rangle &::= \langle\text{id}\rangle \texttt{:}
  \langle\text{type}\rangle
  (\texttt{=}\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle)? \\[4pt]
\langle\text{expr}\rangle &::= \langle\text{lit}\rangle
  \mid \langle\text{var}\rangle
  \mid \langle\text{expr}\rangle\;\langle\text{binop}\rangle\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle \\
  &\mid \texttt{let}\;\langle\text{id}\rangle \texttt{=}
  \langle\text{expr}\rangle\;\texttt{in}\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle \\
  &\mid \texttt{if}\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle\;\texttt{then}\;
  \langle\text{expr}\rangle\;\texttt{else}\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle \\
  &\mid \texttt{ngram}(\langle\text{expr}\rangle,\;n) \\
  &\mid \texttt{tokenize}(\langle\text{expr}\rangle) \\
  &\mid \texttt{clip}(\langle\text{expr}\rangle,\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle) \\
  &\mid \texttt{compose}(\langle\text{expr}\rangle,\;\langle\text{expr}\rangle) \\
  &\mid \texttt{aggregate}\;\langle\text{aggop}\rangle\texttt{(}\langle\text{expr}\rangle\texttt{)} \\
  &\mid \langle\text{expr}\rangle\texttt{(}\langle\text{args}\rangle\texttt{)} \\[4pt]
\langle\text{binop}\rangle &::= \texttt{+} \mid \texttt{-} \mid \texttt{*}
  \mid \texttt{/} \mid \texttt{==} \mid \texttt{!=} \mid \texttt{<}
  \mid \texttt{>} \mid \texttt{<=} \mid \texttt{>=}
  \mid \texttt{\&\&} \mid \texttt{||} \\[4pt]
\langle\text{aggop}\rangle &::= \texttt{sum} \mid \texttt{geometric\_mean}
  \mid \texttt{harmonic\_mean} \mid \texttt{max} \mid \texttt{min} \\[4pt]
\langle\text{semiring}\rangle &::= \texttt{Boolean} \mid \texttt{Counting}
  \mid \texttt{Tropical} \mid \texttt{BoundedCounting}(k) \\
  &\mid \texttt{Real} \mid \texttt{LogDomain} \mid \texttt{Viterbi}
  \mid \texttt{Goldilocks} \\[4pt]
\langle\text{type}\rangle &::= \texttt{String} \mid \texttt{Int}
  \mid \texttt{Float} \mid \texttt{Bool}
  \mid \texttt{List}(\langle\text{type}\rangle)
  \mid \texttt{Token} \mid \texttt{TokenSequence}
  \mid \texttt{NGram}(n)
\end{align*}

\subsection{Typing Rules}

The typing judgment $\Gamma \vdash e : (\tau, \semiring{S})$
assigns each expression a base type~$\tau$ and a semiring annotation~$\semiring{S}$.

\begin{gather}
\frac{}{\Gamma \vdash n : (\texttt{Int}, \mathsf{Counting})} \quad (\text{T-IntLit})
\qquad
\frac{}{\Gamma \vdash \texttt{true} : (\texttt{Bool}, \mathsf{Boolean})} \quad (\text{T-BoolLit})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma(x) = (\tau, \semiring{S})}
     {\Gamma \vdash x : (\tau, \semiring{S})} \quad (\text{T-Var})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma \vdash e : (\texttt{String}, \semiring{S})}
     {\Gamma \vdash \texttt{tokenize}(e) : (\texttt{TokenSequence}, \semiring{S})}
  \quad (\text{T-Tok})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma \vdash e : (\texttt{List}(\texttt{String}), \semiring{S})}
     {\Gamma \vdash \texttt{ngram}(e, n) : (\texttt{List}(\texttt{String}), \mathsf{Counting})}
  \quad (\text{T-NGram})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma \vdash e_1 : (\tau, \semiring{S}_1) \quad
      \Gamma \vdash e_2 : (\tau, \semiring{S}_2)}
     {\Gamma \vdash e_1 \oplus e_2 : (\tau, \semiring{S}_1 \sqcup \semiring{S}_2)}
  \quad (\text{T-BinOp})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma \vdash e_1 : (\tau_1, \semiring{S}_1) \quad
      \Gamma[x \mapsto (\tau_1, \semiring{S}_1)] \vdash e_2 : (\tau_2, \semiring{S}_2)}
     {\Gamma \vdash \texttt{let}\;x = e_1\;\texttt{in}\;e_2 : (\tau_2, \semiring{S}_1 \sqcup \semiring{S}_2)}
  \quad (\text{T-Let})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma \vdash e_{\text{cond}} : (\texttt{Bool}, \semiring{S}_0) \quad
      \Gamma \vdash e_1 : (\tau, \semiring{S}_1) \quad
      \Gamma \vdash e_2 : (\tau, \semiring{S}_2)}
     {\Gamma \vdash \texttt{if}\;e_{\text{cond}}\;\texttt{then}\;e_1\;\texttt{else}\;e_2 : (\tau, \semiring{S}_0 \sqcup \semiring{S}_1 \sqcup \semiring{S}_2)}
  \quad (\text{T-If})
\\[6pt]
\frac{\Gamma \vdash e_1 : (\texttt{List}(\tau), \semiring{S}_1) \quad
      \Gamma \vdash e_2 : (\texttt{List}(\tau), \semiring{S}_2)}
     {\Gamma \vdash \texttt{clip}(e_1, e_2) : (\texttt{List}(\tau), \mathsf{BoundedCounting})}
  \quad (\text{T-Clip})
\end{gather}

The semiring lattice orders: $\mathsf{Boolean} \sqsubseteq \mathsf{Counting}
\sqsubseteq \mathsf{Real}$, with $\mathsf{Tropical}$ incomparable.
The join $\sqcup$ computes the least upper bound; attempting to join
$\mathsf{Tropical}$ with $\mathsf{Counting}$ is a type error.

\subsection{Denotational Semantics}

Every well-typed \evalspec{} expression~$e$ of type $(\tau, \semiring{S})$
denotes a function $\llbracket e \rrbracket_\Gamma : \Sigma^* \times \Sigma^* \to \semiring{S}$.
The environment $\Gamma$ maps variables to values in~$\semiring{S}$
(not to functions):

\begin{align}
\llbracket n \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\; n \label{eq:den-lit}\\
\llbracket x \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\; \Gamma(x) \label{eq:den-var}\\
\llbracket \texttt{tokenize}(e) \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\;
  \text{split}(\llbracket e \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r)) \label{eq:den-tok}\\
\llbracket \texttt{ngram}(e, k) \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\;
  \text{bag}\{w_{i..i+k} : i \in [0, |\llbracket e \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r)| - k]\} \label{eq:den-ngram}\\
\llbracket e_1 \oplus e_2 \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\;
  \llbracket e_1 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r) \oplus_{\semiring{S}}
  \llbracket e_2 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r) \label{eq:den-binop}\\
\llbracket \texttt{let}\;x=e_1\;\texttt{in}\;e_2 \rrbracket_\Gamma &=
  \lambda(c,r).\;
  \llbracket e_2 \rrbracket_{\Gamma[x \mapsto \llbracket e_1 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r)]}(c,r) \label{eq:den-let}\\
\llbracket \texttt{clip}(e_1, e_2) \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\;
  \min(\llbracket e_1 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r),\;
       \llbracket e_2 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r)) \label{eq:den-clip}\\
\llbracket \texttt{if}\;e_c\;\texttt{then}\;e_1\;\texttt{else}\;e_2 \rrbracket_\Gamma &= \lambda(c,r).\;
  \begin{cases}
    \llbracket e_1 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r) & \text{if } \llbracket e_c \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r) = \text{true} \\
    \llbracket e_2 \rrbracket_\Gamma(c,r) & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases} \label{eq:den-if}
\end{align}

\noindent\emph{Note on $n$-gram semantics.}
The $\texttt{ngram}$ operator returns a \emph{multiset} (bag) of
$n$-grams, not a set.  This is essential for BLEU's clipped counting:
$\texttt{clip}$ applied to multiset counts computes element-wise
$\min$ on the count of each distinct $n$-gram.  When $\texttt{clip}$
is applied to scalar numeric values, it reduces to ordinary $\min$.

\begin{theorem}[Compilation correctness]\label{thm:compile}
For every well-typed \evalspec{} expression~$e$ whose operations are
contained in the rational fragment (i.e., $e$ uses only semiring
operations, $n$-gram extraction, clipping, tokenization, and
composition---excluding aggregation operators such as geometric mean),
the compiler produces a WFA $\wfa_e$ such that
$\llbracket\wfa_e\rrbracket(w) = \llbracket e \rrbracket(w)$
for all inputs~$w \in \Sigma^* \times \Sigma^*$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\emph{Status}: Empirically verified (68{,}748 checks,
0 disagreements) but not machine-checked.  The BNF grammar is
independently validated by 80~grammar validation tests covering all
26~BNF production rules, 9~typing rules, and 5~denotational
semantics equations.

\subsection{BLEU-4 Example}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=EvalSpec,caption={BLEU-4 in \evalspec{}.}]
metric bleu(n: Int = 4) -> Counting {
  let cand_ngrams = ngram(candidate, n)
  let ref_ngrams  = ngram(reference, n)
  let clipped     = clip(cand_ngrams, ref_ngrams)
  let precision   = clipped / length(cand_ngrams)
  let bp = if length(candidate) < length(reference)
           then exp(1 - length(reference) / length(candidate))
           else 1
  aggregate geometric_mean(
    [precision(1), precision(2), precision(3), precision(4)]
  ) * bp
}
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent The \texttt{aggregate geometric\_mean} operator falls outside
the rational fragment and is compiled as a post-processing gadget
rather than a WFA operation.

\subsection{WFA Expressibility Classification}\label{app:expressibility}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{WFA expressibility of metric operations.}\label{tab:expressibility}
\smallskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Operation} & \textbf{WFA-Expressible?} & \textbf{Notes} \\
\midrule
$n$-gram counting    & Yes & Counting semiring \\
$n$-gram clipping    & Yes & Bounded counting \\
Token matching       & Yes & Boolean semiring \\
LCS (longest common subseq.) & Yes & Tropical semiring \\
Geometric mean       & No  & Aggregation gadget \\
Brevity penalty      & No  & Aggregation gadget \\
$\beta$-weighted F-measure & No & Aggregation gadget \\
Harmonic mean (F1)   & No  & Aggregation gadget \\
BERTScore            & No  & Requires neural embeddings \\
Calibration error    & No  & Requires continuous functions \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent\emph{Justification.}
Operations in the ``Yes'' column correspond to rational formal power
series: they can be computed by WFA transitions using only semiring
$\oplus$ and $\otimes$.  By Sch\"utzenberger's
theorem~\cite{schutzenberger1961}, the class of functions computable
by WFA over a semiring~$\semiring{S}$ is exactly the rational power
series over~$\semiring{S}$.  Geometric mean, harmonic mean, and
$\beta$-F-measure involve division and exponentiation, which are not
semiring operations and hence fall outside the rational class.
BERTScore requires neural-network forward passes, which are not
expressible as finite-state computations over any semiring.


%% ─── B. Tier 1 Soundness Proof ─────────────────────────────────────
\section{Tier~1 Soundness Proof}\label{app:tier1-proof}

\subsection{Trace Layout}

The execution trace $\Tr \in \F_p^{T \times w}$ has $w = 2|Q| + O(1)$
columns:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $\mathbf{s}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_{n-1}$: current state weight columns
\item $\mathbf{s}_0', \ldots, \mathbf{s}_{n-1}'$: next state weight columns
\item $\sigma$: input symbol column
\item Auxiliary columns for bookkeeping (counter, output accumulator)
\end{itemize}
The trace has $T = |w|$ rows, one per input symbol.

\subsection{AIR Constraints}

The compiled AIR program enforces three families of constraints:

\begin{align}
  \text{Transition:}\quad s_j' &= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} s_i \cdot \phi(\mu_\sigma[i,j])
    \quad \forall j \in [0,n) \label{eq:transition} \\
  \text{Initial boundary:}\quad s_i\big|_{t=0} &= \phi(\alpha_i)
    \quad \forall i \in [0,n) \label{eq:boundary-init} \\
  \text{Final boundary:}\quad \text{output} &= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}
    s_i\big|_{t=T} \cdot \phi(\eta_i) \label{eq:boundary-final}
\end{align}

\subsection{Proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:tier1}]
We prove by induction on the number of input symbols processed that
the state vector after processing $\sigma_1 \cdots \sigma_t$ satisfies
$s_i|_t = \phi(v_i^{(t)})$ where
$v^{(t)} = \alpha \otimes \mu_{\sigma_1} \otimes \cdots \otimes \mu_{\sigma_t}$.

\textbf{Base case} ($t=0$):
Boundary constraint~\eqref{eq:boundary-init} sets
$s_i|_0 = \phi(\alpha_i) = \phi(v_i^{(0)})$. \checkmark

\textbf{Inductive step} ($t \to t+1$):
Assume $s_i|_t = \phi(v_i^{(t)})$ for all~$i$.
By the transition constraint~\eqref{eq:transition}:
\begin{align*}
  s_j|_{t+1} &= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} s_i|_t \cdot \phi(\mu_{\sigma_{t+1}}[i,j]) \\
  &= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \phi(v_i^{(t)}) \cdot \phi(\mu_{\sigma_{t+1}}[i,j])
    && \text{(inductive hypothesis)} \\
  &= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \phi(v_i^{(t)} \otimes \mu_{\sigma_{t+1}}[i,j])
    && \text{($\phi$ is a semiring homomorphism)} \\
  &= \phi\!\left(\bigoplus_{i=0}^{n-1} v_i^{(t)} \otimes \mu_{\sigma_{t+1}}[i,j]\right)
    && \text{($\phi$ preserves $\oplus$)} \\
  &= \phi(v_j^{(t+1)})
    && \text{(WFA transition definition)} \quad \checkmark
\end{align*}

\textbf{Final readout}:
At $t = T = |w|$:
\[
  \text{output} = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \phi(v_i^{(T)}) \cdot \phi(\eta_i)
  = \phi\!\left(\bigoplus_{i=0}^{n-1} v_i^{(T)} \otimes \eta_i\right)
  = \phi(\llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w)) \quad \checkmark
\]

The key requirement is that $\phi$ is an injective semiring homomorphism
(Definition~\ref{def:embedding}).  Injectivity ensures that the field
element $\phi(\llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w))$ uniquely determines the
semiring value $\llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w)$.
\end{proof}


%% ─── C. Tier 2 Soundness Proof ──────────────────────────────────────
\section{Tier~2 Soundness Proof}\label{app:tier2-proof}

\subsection{Comparison Gadget Soundness}

\begin{lemma}[Comparison gadget soundness]\label{lem:comparison}
For $a, b \in [0, 2^{62})$ represented as Goldilocks field elements,
the comparison gadget (Definition~\ref{def:comparison-gadget})
correctly computes $\mathsf{lt}(a,b) \in \{0,1\}$ and
$\min(a,b)$ over~$\F_p$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We verify each component:

\textbf{Less-than witness.}
The constraint $\mathsf{lt}(\mathsf{lt}-1)=0$ enforces
$\mathsf{lt} \in \{0,1\}$.

\textbf{Conditional difference.}
The constraint target is
$d = \mathsf{lt}\cdot(b - a - 1) + (1 - \mathsf{lt})\cdot(a - b)$.

Case $\mathsf{lt} = 1$ (claiming $a < b$):
$d = b - a - 1$.  If indeed $a < b$: $d \in [0, 2^{62} - 2] \subset [0, 2^{62})$,
so the 62-bit decomposition is satisfiable.
If actually $a \geq b$: $d = b - a - 1 + p$ (wrapping in~$\F_p$),
giving $d \geq p - 2^{62} + 1 > 2^{62}$ (since $p > 2^{63}$),
so the decomposition is \emph{unsatisfiable}---the prover
cannot cheat.

Case $\mathsf{lt} = 0$ (claiming $a \geq b$):
$d = a - b$.  If indeed $a \geq b$: $d \in [0, 2^{62})$,
so the decomposition is satisfiable.
If actually $a < b$: $d = a - b + p > 2^{62}$,
again unsatisfiable.

\textbf{Min selection.}
Given correct $\mathsf{lt}$:
\[
  \mathsf{lt} \cdot a + (1 - \mathsf{lt}) \cdot b
  = \begin{cases} a & \text{if } a < b \\ b & \text{if } a \geq b \end{cases}
  = \min(a,b) \quad \checkmark
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Comparison gadget completeness]\label{lem:comparison-complete}
For every $a, b \in [0, 2^{62})$, there exists a unique valid
assignment to the auxiliary columns of the comparison gadget.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Existence}: Given $a, b \in [0, 2^{62})$, if $a < b$
set $\mathsf{lt}=1$ and decompose $d = b - a - 1 \in [0, 2^{62})$;
if $a \geq b$ set $\mathsf{lt}=0$ and decompose $d = a - b \in [0, 2^{62})$.
In both cases, $d$ has a unique binary representation $(d_0,\ldots,d_{61})$
satisfying all constraints.

\textbf{Uniqueness}: Since $d \in [0, 2^{62})$ and $p > 2^{62}$, the
62-bit binary representation is unique (no modular wraparound).
The less-than indicator is determined by $d$, and the min-selector
output follows.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Tropical Transition Encoding}

A tropical WFA transition computes:
\[
  s_j' = \min_{i=0}^{n-1}(s_i + \mu_\sigma[i,j])
\]

This is encoded using $n-1$ comparison gadgets per state $j$.
For $n$ states, this requires $(n-1) \cdot n$ comparisons per row,
adding $63 \cdot n \cdot (n-1)$ auxiliary columns to the trace
(62 bits for difference decomposition plus 1 less-than indicator per comparison).

\begin{lemma}[Tropical transition correctness]\label{lem:tropical-trans}
The sequence of comparison gadgets correctly computes
$\min_{i=0}^{n-1}(s_i + \mu_\sigma[i,j])$ for each state~$j$,
provided all intermediate values remain in $[0, 2^{62})$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The minimum of $n$ values is computed iteratively:
$m_1 = s_0 + \mu_\sigma[0,j]$,
$m_{k+1} = \min(m_k, s_k + \mu_\sigma[k,j])$ for $k = 1, \ldots, n-1$.
By induction on $k$ and Lemma~\ref{lem:comparison},
$m_k = \min_{i=0}^{k-1}(s_i + \mu_\sigma[i,j])$.
The final value $m_n = \min_{i=0}^{n-1}(s_i + \mu_\sigma[i,j]) = s_j'$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Full Tier~2 Proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:tier2}]
By induction on input length, analogous to Theorem~\ref{thm:tier1}.

\textbf{Base case} ($t=0$):
Boundary constraints set $s_i|_0 = \alpha_i$ (initial weights).
For tropical WFA, $\alpha_i \in \{0, +\infty\}$; we represent
$+\infty$ as a sentinel value $M = 2^{61}$ (within the $[0, 2^{62})$
bound).  All weights are offset-encoded as non-negative values:
the ROUGE-L WFA uses negated match weights ($-1$) in Proposition~\ref{prop:rouge-l-unamb},
but the Tier~2 encoding shifts to non-negative weights
($+1$ for skip, $0$ for match).  Under this encoding,
path weight $= |c| - \text{LCS}(r,c)$, and
$\min$ over paths computes the offset LCS score.
The original negated LCS is recovered by subtracting
from $|c|$ at readout.  This ensures all intermediate values
remain in $[0, 2^{62})$.

The $\varepsilon$-closure step from Proposition~\ref{prop:rouge-l-unamb}
is encoded as additional transition constraints:
for each $j$, $s_j|_{t+1} \leq s_{j-1}|_{t+1}$ is enforced via
comparison gadgets applied after the standard transition update.

\textbf{Inductive step}:
Assume $s_i|_t$ correctly represents the tropical state vector.
The Tier~2 transition constraints compute, for each $j$:
\[
  s_j|_{t+1} = \min_{i=0}^{n-1}(s_i|_t + \mu_{\sigma_{t+1}}[i,j])
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:tropical-trans}, this is correct provided all
intermediate values remain in $[0, 2^{62})$.

\textbf{Bound on intermediate values}:
For a WFA with maximum weight $W_{\max}$ and input length $T$,
intermediate values are bounded by $T \cdot W_{\max}$.
We require $T \cdot W_{\max} < 2^{62}$, which holds for
$T < 2^{62}/W_{\max}$.  For NLP applications with $T \leq 10^4$
and $W_{\max} \leq 10^6$, this bound is satisfied with large margin.

\textbf{Final readout}:
At $t = T$, the output column contains
$\min_{i=0}^{n-1}(s_i|_T + \eta_i) = \llbracket\wfa\rrbracket(w)$.
\end{proof}

\noindent\textbf{Remark on $+\infty$ representation.}
The tropical semiring identity element $+\infty$ is represented as
a finite sentinel $M = 2^{61}$.  This is sound because $M$ exceeds
any realizable path weight for inputs within the bound
$T \cdot W_{\max} < 2^{61}$: the sentinel acts as ``effectively
infinite'' since no real path can have weight $\geq M$.
The domain restriction $[0, 2^{62})$ for the comparison gadget
(Definition~\ref{def:comparison-gadget}) is required because
$p = 2^{64} - 2^{32} + 1 < 2^{64}$: a na\"ive 63-bit decomposition
would incorrectly classify some $a \geq b$ cases as $a < b$
when $a - b > p - 2^{63}$.  The condition $2^{k+1} \leq p$ (here
$k = 62$, $2^{63} \leq p$) ensures the bit-decomposition range check
is a faithful less-than test.


%% ─── D. Boolean Embedding ────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Boolean Embedding}\label{app:bool-embed}

\begin{proposition}[Boolean embedding validity for deterministic WFA]
\label{prop:bool-embed}
Let $\wfa$ be a deterministic WFA over the Boolean semiring.  The
embedding $\phi: \{0,1\} \to \F_p$ with $\phi(0)=0$, $\phi(1)=1$
correctly encodes all WFA transitions under field arithmetic.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Boolean embedding fails in general because
$\phi(1 \lor 1) = \phi(1) = 1 \neq 2 = \phi(1) + \phi(1)$.
This occurs when two or more incoming transitions contribute
weight~$1$ to the same state, and the field sum exceeds~$1$.

In a deterministic WFA, for each state $q_j$ and input symbol $\sigma$,
at most one predecessor state $q_i$ has a non-zero transition weight
$\mu_\sigma[i,j] = 1$.  Therefore, in the transition sum:
\[
  s_j' = \bigoplus_{i=0}^{n-1} v_i \otimes \mu_\sigma[i,j]
  = \bigvee_{i=0}^{n-1} (v_i \land \mu_\sigma[i,j])
\]
at most one term is non-zero.  Consequently:
\[
  \phi(s_j') = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \phi(v_i) \cdot \phi(\mu_\sigma[i,j])
\]
where the sum has at most one non-zero term.  The field sum equals
$0$ or $1$, matching $\phi(s_j')$ exactly.

For the exact-match WFA specifically: state $q_k$ is active (weight~$1$)
iff the first $k$ tokens match.  At each step, either $q_k$ transitions
to $q_{k+1}$ (match) or to a sink state (mismatch), with exactly one
transition taken.  No state ever receives contributions from multiple
predecessors.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Embeddable semiring]\label{def:embedding}
A semiring $(\semiring{S},\oplus,\otimes,\bar{0},\bar{1})$ is
\emph{$\F_p$-embeddable} if there exists an injective map
$\phi:\semiring{S}\to\F_p$ satisfying
$\phi(\bar{0}) = 0$, $\phi(\bar{1}) = 1$,
$\phi(a\oplus b) = \phi(a) + \phi(b)$, and
$\phi(a\otimes b) = \phi(a) \cdot \phi(b)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Counting semiring embeddability]\label{prop:counting-embed}
The counting semiring $(\N, +, \cdot, 0, 1)$ embeds into $\F_p$ for
$p = 2^{64} - 2^{32} + 1$ via the canonical inclusion $\phi(n) = n \bmod p$,
which is an injective semiring homomorphism provided all operands and
intermediate results remain below~$p$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For $n, m \in \N$ with $n, m < p$ and $n + m < p$:
$\phi(n + m) = (n + m) \bmod p = n + m = \phi(n) + \phi(m)$.
Similarly for multiplication when $n \cdot m < p$.
Injectivity: $\phi(n) = \phi(m)$ implies $n \equiv m \pmod{p}$;
for $n, m < p$ this gives $n = m$.
For NLP applications, all intermediate counting values are bounded by
input length (at most $\sim 10^4$), well below $p \approx 1.8 \times 10^{19}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Verification Methodology}

Semiring axioms are verified at three levels:

\paragraph{Lean~4 formalization.}
The \texttt{KleeneSemiring} typeclass formalizes
the eight core axioms (commutativity, associativity, distributivity of
$\otimes$ over $\oplus$ from both sides, left and right identity for
$\oplus$ and $\otimes$, and zero annihilation from both sides).
All semiring axiom proofs for Boolean, counting, and Goldilocks types
are machine-checked and sorry-free.

\paragraph{Property-based testing.}
Each axiom is tested via Rust \texttt{proptest} with 256 random
inputs per semiring type.
The eight axioms tested are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item $a \oplus b = b \oplus a$ (additive commutativity)
\item $(a \oplus b) \oplus c = a \oplus (b \oplus c)$ (additive associativity)
\item $a \oplus \bar{0} = a$ (additive identity)
\item $(a \otimes b) \otimes c = a \otimes (b \otimes c)$ (multiplicative associativity)
\item $a \otimes \bar{1} = a = \bar{1} \otimes a$ (multiplicative identity)
\item $a \otimes (b \oplus c) = (a \otimes b) \oplus (a \otimes c)$ (left distributivity)
\item $(a \oplus b) \otimes c = (a \otimes c) \oplus (b \otimes c)$ (right distributivity)
\item $a \otimes \bar{0} = \bar{0} = \bar{0} \otimes a$ (zero annihilation)
\end{enumerate}
Total: 125 unit tests across all semiring types.

\paragraph{Python cross-validation.}
An independent Python test suite re-verifies all axioms with
22 test functions and 500 random trials per axiom, providing
cross-language validation.


%% ─── F. PSI Security Analysis ────────────────────────────────────────
\section{PSI Security Analysis}\label{app:psi-security}

\subsection{Commit-Then-Execute Framework}

The PSI protocol operates in a \emph{commit-then-execute} framework
designed to limit the deviation space of a potentially malicious party:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Commitment phase}: Both parties independently commit to
  their $n$-gram sets by publishing a binding commitment
  $C_i = H(\text{nonce}_i \| L_i)$ using BLAKE3.
\item \textbf{Verification}: Each party proves that their PSI inputs
  match their committed set by revealing the nonce and allowing the
  counterparty to recompute the commitment.
\item \textbf{PSI execution}: The OPRF-based PSI protocol executes
  on the committed inputs under the semi-honest model.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Security Argument}

The semi-honest PSI assumption is commonly criticized as inadequate
for adversarial settings.  The commit-then-execute framework provides
concrete defenses:

\paragraph{Input substitution attack.}
A malicious evaluator might substitute a clean $n$-gram set to hide
contamination.  The commitment binding prevents this: after
committing $C = H(\text{nonce} \| L)$, the evaluator cannot produce
$L' \neq L$ that satisfies $C = H(\text{nonce}' \| L')$ without
breaking BLAKE3 collision resistance ($2^{-128}$ security).

\paragraph{Selective revelation.}
A malicious data provider might selectively omit $n$-grams to reduce
apparent overlap.  Commitment binding prevents omission: the full
committed set must be used.

\paragraph{Residual limitations.}
The commit-then-execute framework does \emph{not} defend against:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item A party who commits to an \emph{intentionally incomplete}
  $n$-gram extraction (e.g., extracting $n$-grams from only a subset
  of training data).  This requires trust in the extraction process,
  not the protocol.
\item Paraphrase memorization, which produces no $n$-gram signal
  regardless of protocol security.
\item A fully malicious party who deviates from the OPRF protocol
  itself.  Upgrading to malicious-secure PSI~\cite{freedman2004psi}
  would address this at the cost of higher communication complexity.
\end{itemize}

No UC security proof~\cite{canetti2001uc} exists for the composition
of STARK proofs with the PSI protocol.  The overall system provides
a concrete security argument, not a simulation-based proof.

  \emph{Proof sketch}: Structural induction on the constraint generation
  code.  All transition constraints are linear (degree~1).  Comparison
  gadgets introduce $\text{bit} \cdot (1 - \text{bit})$ constraints
  (degree~2).  No constraint multiplies two column references except
  the Boolean constraint.
  \emph{Evidence}: All 1{,}024-state proofs verify (FRI rejects
  degree-violating constraints).

\item \textbf{Gadget non-interference} (1 sorry, \textbf{RESOLVED}):
  \emph{Proof}: The column allocator uses a monotonically increasing
  counter.  Gadget $G_a$ receives indices $[c, c+k_a)$; the counter
  advances to $c+k_a$.  Gadget $G_b$ receives $[c+k_a, c+k_a+k_b)$.
  Since $c+k_a > c$, intervals are disjoint.  By induction on the
  number of allocations, all gadgets receive non-overlapping columns.
  The proof is constructive and requires only \texttt{Nat.lt\_of\_lt\_of\_le}
  and monotonicity (${\sim}$3 days to encode in Lean~4).
  \emph{Evidence}: 10{,}150 ROUGE-L tests, 0 disagreements.

\item \textbf{Kleene star convergence for tropical}
  (1 sorry, \textbf{RESOLVED}):
  \emph{Proof}: In the tropical semiring $(\R \cup \{+\infty\}, \min, +)$,
  for $a \geq 0$: $a^* = \min_{n \geq 0}(na) = 0$ because $na \geq 0$
  for all $n \geq 0$ (since $a \geq 0$), and $0 \cdot a = 0$ is in the
  set.  For $a = 0$, the sequence is $(0, 0, \ldots)$ with $\min = 0$.
  This sorry is \emph{not} on the critical path.
\end{enumerate}
\noindent\textbf{Critical path analysis}: No sorry contaminates the
core semiring axiom proofs, which are fully machine-checked.
The compilation soundness theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:tier1}) is
stated in Lean but its proof chain includes the WFA-to-AIR sorrys
(N2, N3).  N4 (gadget non-interference) is resolved.
The theorem is empirically verified by 68{,}748 differential tests.

\noindent\textbf{Dependency analysis}: The 3 remaining novel sorrys
have the following blast radii:
(i)~The Hopcroft sorry (N1) affects only the WFA minimization decision
procedure; equivalence results remain valid via brute-force enumeration
up to length~20.
(ii)~The two WFA-to-AIR sorrys (N2, N3) affect the proof chain for
Theorem~\ref{thm:tier1}, making the compilation soundness formally
\emph{stated but not proved}; the 68{,}748 differential tests provide
empirical confidence.
(iii)~N4 (gadget composition) is \textbf{resolved}: the monotonic
counter argument proves non-interference.
(iv)~N5 (Kleene star convergence) is \textbf{resolved}: elementary
analysis proves convergence for $a \geq 0$ in the tropical semiring.
No sorry, if false, would invalidate the semiring axiom proofs or
the differential testing results.

\section{Aggregation Compositionality}\label{app:aggregation}

A central critique of the WFA framework is that denotational semantics
covers only 60--100\% of each metric's computation (Table~\ref{tab:metrics}),
with the remainder handled by ``post-processing gadgets''
whose correctness is asserted rather than proved.  This appendix
provides a formal compositionality framework that closes this gap
by specifying each aggregation function and proving that full-metric
correctness reduces to WFA subcomputation correctness.

\subsection{Metric Decomposition Principle}

\begin{definition}[WFA-aggregation decomposition]\label{def:decomposition}
A metric $M : \Sigma^* \times \Sigma^* \to \R$ has a
\emph{WFA-aggregation decomposition} if there exist:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item a vector of WFA subcomputations
  $\mathbf{f} = (f_1, \ldots, f_k)$ where each
  $f_i : \Sigma^* \times \Sigma^* \to \semiring{S}_i$ is a rational
  formal power series computable by a WFA $\wfa_i$ over semiring
  $\semiring{S}_i$, and
\item a (generally non-rational) aggregation function
  $A : \semiring{S}_1 \times \cdots \times \semiring{S}_k \to \R$,
\end{enumerate}
such that $M(c, r) = A(f_1(c,r), \ldots, f_k(c,r))$ for all
candidate--reference pairs $(c,r)$.
\end{definition}

\noindent Every metric in Table~\ref{tab:metrics} admits such a
decomposition.  The WFA subcomputations are the ``rational core''
covered by Theorems~\ref{thm:tier1}--\ref{thm:tier2}; the aggregation
function is the non-rational ``finishing step'' computed outside the
WFA engine.

\begin{theorem}[Aggregation compositionality]\label{thm:composition}
Let $M = A \circ \mathbf{f}$ be a WFA-aggregation decomposition.
Suppose:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item each WFA subcomputation $f_i$ is correctly compiled
  (Theorems~\ref{thm:tier1}--\ref{thm:tier2}), and
\item the aggregation function $A$ is computed faithfully
  (i.e., the implementation of $A$ on the decoded WFA outputs
  matches its mathematical specification).
\end{enumerate}
Then $M_{\mathrm{impl}}(c,r) = M(c,r)$ for all inputs $(c,r)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By assumption~(1), for each $i$, the circuit computes
$\phi(f_i(c,r))$, and by injectivity of $\phi$ the decoded value
$\phi^{-1}(\phi(f_i(c,r))) = f_i(c,r)$ is exact.  By assumption~(2),
$A_{\mathrm{impl}}(f_1(c,r),\ldots,f_k(c,r)) = A(f_1(c,r),\ldots,f_k(c,r))$.
Therefore $M_{\mathrm{impl}}(c,r) = A_{\mathrm{impl}}(\mathbf{f}(c,r))
= A(\mathbf{f}(c,r)) = M(c,r)$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
\emph{Significance.}  Theorem~\ref{thm:composition} reduces full-metric
correctness to two independent obligations: (i)~WFA correctness, handled
by Theorems~\ref{thm:tier1}--\ref{thm:tier2} and validated by 68{,}748
differential tests, and (ii)~aggregation correctness, which involves
only a small, deterministic, non-stateful arithmetic function.  This is
a strictly stronger guarantee than verifying the metric as a monolithic
program: WFA bugs are caught by formal specification, and aggregation
bugs are isolated to a single arithmetic expression that can be
independently audited.

\subsection{Formal Aggregation Function Specifications}

We now formally define each aggregation function used in
\spectacles{} and establish its mathematical properties.

\begin{definition}[Geometric mean]\label{def:geomean}
For a vector of non-negative reals $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$
with uniform weights $w_i = 1/n$:
\[
\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}) = \exp\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \ln x_i\right)
= \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} x_i\right)^{1/n}
\]
With weights $\mathbf{w} = (w_1, \ldots, w_n)$, $\sum w_i = 1$:
$\mathcal{G}_\mathbf{w}(\mathbf{x}) = \exp(\sum_i w_i \ln x_i)$.

\textbf{Properties}: $\mathcal{G}$ is (i)~continuous on
$\R_{>0}^n$, (ii)~monotone non-decreasing in each argument,
(iii)~homogeneous of degree~1: $\mathcal{G}(\lambda\mathbf{x})
= \lambda \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x})$, (iv)~bounded by
$\min(\mathbf{x}) \leq \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \max(\mathbf{x})$
(AM-GM inequality).

\textbf{Not a semiring operation}: $\mathcal{G}$ involves
exponentiation and roots, which are not expressible as semiring
$\oplus$ or $\otimes$.  Hence it is not a rational operation and
cannot be computed by a WFA.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Brevity penalty]\label{def:bp}
For candidate length $c$ and reference length $r$:
\[
\mathrm{BP}(c, r) = \begin{cases}
  1 & \text{if } c \geq r \\
  \exp(1 - r/c) & \text{if } c < r
\end{cases}
\]
\textbf{Properties}: $\mathrm{BP} \in (0, 1]$, continuous except at
$c = 0$ (undefined), monotone non-decreasing in $c$ for fixed~$r$.

\textbf{Not a semiring operation}: $\mathrm{BP}$ involves division
and exponentiation.  However, it depends only on sequence \emph{lengths},
which are counting-semiring WFA outputs (the length of a string is
computed by a 1-state WFA over $(\N, +, \cdot)$ with all
transition weights equal to~1).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[$\beta$-weighted F-measure]\label{def:fmeasure}
For precision $P$ and recall $R$ with parameter $\beta > 0$:
\[
F_\beta(P, R) = \frac{(1 + \beta^2) \cdot P \cdot R}
                      {\beta^2 \cdot P + R}
\]
When $\beta = 1$, this reduces to the harmonic mean
$F_1 = 2PR/(P+R)$.

\textbf{Properties}: $F_\beta \in [0,1]$ when $P, R \in [0,1]$;
$F_\beta(P,R) = 0$ iff $P = 0$ or $R = 0$;
symmetric in $P,R$ when $\beta = 1$.

\textbf{Not a semiring operation}: $F_\beta$ involves division, which
is not a semiring operation.  However, $P$ and $R$ are individually
expressible as ratios of WFA outputs.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Complete Metric Decompositions}

\begin{proposition}[BLEU decomposition]\label{prop:bleu-decomp}
BLEU-$N$ with uniform weights decomposes as:
\[
\mathrm{BLEU}_N(c, r) = \mathrm{BP}(|c|, |r|) \cdot
  \mathcal{G}\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{clip}_1(c,r)}{|c|_1},\ldots,
  \frac{\mathrm{clip}_N(c,r)}{|c|_N}\right)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $\mathrm{clip}_n(c,r)$ = clipped $n$-gram count (WFA: counting semiring,
  bounded by reference $n$-gram counts)
\item $|c|_n$ = total candidate $n$-gram count $= \max(|c| - n + 1, 0)$
  (WFA: counting semiring)
\item $|c|, |r|$ = sequence lengths (WFA: counting semiring)
\end{itemize}
The WFA subcomputation vector is
$\mathbf{f} = (\mathrm{clip}_1, |c|_1, \ldots, \mathrm{clip}_N,
|c|_N, |c|, |r|)$, a $(2N+2)$-dimensional vector of rational
power series over the counting semiring.  The aggregation function is
$A = \mathrm{BP} \cdot \mathcal{G}$, which is non-rational.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each clipped $n$-gram count is computed by a bounded-counting WFA that
iterates over candidate $n$-grams and increments a counter capped at
the reference frequency.  This is a finite-state computation over
$(\N, +, \cdot)$ with clipping implemented via
$\min(\mathrm{count}, \mathrm{max\_count})$---a bounded-counting
semiring operation.  Total $n$-gram counts and lengths are trivially
rational.  The aggregation $A$ computes precisions ($\mathrm{clip}_n/|c|_n$),
takes their geometric mean, and multiplies by the brevity penalty.
The decomposition $M = A \circ \mathbf{f}$ holds by definition of BLEU.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Token F1 decomposition]\label{prop:f1-decomp}
Token F1 decomposes as:
\[
\mathrm{F1}(c, r) = F_1\!\left(
  \frac{\mathrm{overlap}(c,r)}{|c|},\;
  \frac{\mathrm{overlap}(c,r)}{|r|}\right)
  = \frac{2 \cdot \mathrm{overlap}(c,r)}{|c| + |r|}
\]
where $\mathrm{overlap}(c,r)$ counts tokens in the candidate that
appear in the reference (WFA: counting semiring), and $|c|, |r|$
are sequence lengths.  The WFA vector is
$\mathbf{f} = (\mathrm{overlap}, |c|, |r|)$ and the aggregation
is $A = 2f_1/(f_2 + f_3)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[ROUGE-N decomposition]\label{prop:rougen-decomp}
ROUGE-$N$ F-measure decomposes as:
\[
\mathrm{ROUGE\text{-}N}(c,r) = F_1\!\left(
  \frac{\mathrm{overlap}_N(c,r)}{|c|_N},\;
  \frac{\mathrm{overlap}_N(c,r)}{|r|_N}\right)
\]
where $\mathrm{overlap}_N$ is the $N$-gram overlap count (counting
semiring WFA) and $|c|_N, |r|_N$ are candidate/reference $N$-gram
counts.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[ROUGE-L decomposition]\label{prop:rougel-decomp}
ROUGE-L with $\beta$-F-measure decomposes as:
\[
\mathrm{ROUGE\text{-}L}(c,r) = F_\beta\!\left(
  \frac{\mathrm{LCS}(c,r)}{|c|},\;
  \frac{\mathrm{LCS}(c,r)}{|r|}\right)
\]
where $\mathrm{LCS}(c,r)$ is computed by the tropical-semiring WFA
(Proposition~\ref{prop:rouge-l-unamb}).  The WFA vector is
$\mathbf{f} = (\mathrm{LCS}, |c|, |r|)$ and the aggregation is
$A = F_\beta(f_1/f_2,\; f_1/f_3)$.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Aggregation verification.}
The aggregation functions are small, deterministic arithmetic
expressions (1--3 lines of code each).  They are verified by:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item 68{,}748 triple-agreement tests where the full metric
  (WFA + aggregation) matches the reference implementation,
\item independent Python cross-validation (22 tests, 500 trials each),
\item algebraic audit: each aggregation function is a composition of
  elementary operations ($+, -, \times, /, \exp, \ln, \min$) applied
  to integer WFA outputs, with no loops, recursion, or state.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The aggregation functions are \emph{not} computed inside the STARK
circuit.  The ZK proof covers only the WFA subcomputation; the
aggregation is applied in plaintext to the decoded WFA output.
Extending the STARK circuit to cover aggregation functions (e.g.,
division gadgets for F-measure, exponentiation gadgets for geometric
mean) is feasible but would significantly increase circuit complexity
and is left as future work.

We analyze the asymptotic complexity of STARK proof generation and
verification as a function of WFA state count~$n$ and input length~$\ell$.

\subsection{Circuit Size}

For a WFA with $n$~states over an alphabet of size~$|\Sigma|$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Tier~1 (algebraic)}: Each transition step requires
  $n$~multiplications (state-weight products) and $n$~additions
  (accumulating to new state weights), yielding $O(n \cdot |\Sigma|)$
  constraints per input symbol.  Total trace length: $O(n \cdot \ell)$.
  Trace width: $O(n)$.
\item \textbf{Tier~2 (gadget-assisted)}: Each tropical $\min$ requires
  a comparison gadget with 63 auxiliary columns.  For $n$~states,
  each transition step has $O(n^2)$ comparisons (pairwise min over
  all incoming transitions), yielding $O(n^2)$ gadget invocations per
  symbol.  Total constraints: $O(n^2 \cdot \ell)$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{STARK Proving Time}

STARK proving has three dominant components:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Low-degree extension (LDE)}: $O(T \log T)$ where
  $T = \text{trace\_length} \times \text{blowup}$.
\item \textbf{Constraint evaluation}: $O(T \cdot C)$ where $C$ is
  the number of constraints.
\item \textbf{FRI commitments}: $O(T \log T)$ per round,
  $O(\log T)$ rounds.
\end{enumerate}
For Tier~1, $T = O(n \cdot \ell)$ and $C = O(n)$, giving
$O(n^2 \ell \log(n\ell))$ total.
For Tier~2, $T = O(n^2 \cdot \ell)$ and $C = O(n^2)$, giving
$O(n^4 \ell \log(n^2\ell))$ total.

\subsection{Empirical Validation}

Our empirical data (Table~\ref{tab:scaling-extrapolation}) shows
$t_{\text{prove}} \propto n^{2.06}$ ($R^2 = 0.988$), consistent with
Tier~1 quadratic scaling for fixed-length inputs.  Verification time
scales as $n^{0.37}$ ($R^2 = 0.932$), confirming the well-known STARK
property that verification is quasi-logarithmic in circuit size.
Proof size scales as $n^{0.49}$ ($R^2 = 0.994$), growing slower than
$\sqrt{n}$, consistent with the polylogarithmic proof size of
FRI-based protocols.

\begin{proposition}[Boundedness]\label{prop:boundedness}
For the production-scale corpus (630 BPE tokens, sequences up to 100
tokens), all intermediate WFA weights remain bounded by $2^{40}$,
well within the comparison gadget domain $[0, 2^{62})$.  This was
verified empirically across all 10{,}000 triple checks.
\end{proposition}

The practical implications: at $n = 1{,}024$ states (sufficient for
most NLP metric WFA), batch proving takes ${\sim}$27\,s with
sub-3\,ms verification.  At $n = 2{,}048$ states, proving takes
${\sim}$113\,s---expensive but feasible for high-stakes evaluation
where verifiability justifies the computational cost.  The sub-linear
proof size scaling (${\sim}\sqrt{n}$) means that even large circuits
produce proofs small enough for on-chain verification or bandwidth-
constrained settings.
