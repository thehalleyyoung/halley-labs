# Review: CABER — Coalgebraic Behavioral Auditing of Foundation Models

**Reviewer:** Joseph S. Chang
**Persona:** Automated Reasoning & Logic Expert
**Expertise:** SMT solvers, automated theorem proving, proof certificates, decision procedures, solver engineering, formal logic, proof complexity

---

## Summary

CABER applies coalgebraic automata theory to learn behavioral models of black-box LLMs and model-check them against temporal safety specifications expressed in QCTL_F. From an automated reasoning perspective, the temporal logic model checking is the most substantive formal reasoning component—the fixed-point computation over the learned automaton constitutes a genuine, if limited, model-checking algorithm. The QCTL_F logic is correctly identified as a Pattinson-Schröder instantiation rather than a novel logic, and the CoalCEGAR refinement loop implements actual counterexample-guided abstraction refinement. However, the reasoning infrastructure has critical gaps: no mechanized proofs despite formal methods positioning, non-functorial abstraction breaking preservation theorems, safety-only fragment restricting expressiveness, no proof certificate export for independent verification, and graded logic operating on inputs with 0.28–0.73 calibration error rendering quantitative reasoning unreliable.

## Strengths

1. **Genuine model checking with well-defined semantics.** Unlike many papers that claim "verification" but implement classification, CABER implements actual model checking: a QCTL_F temporal logic with formally defined syntax and semantics, a fixed-point computation engine that computes graded satisfaction degrees via least/greatest fixed-point iteration over the state space, and graded semantics in [0,1] with Łukasiewicz connectives. The fixed-point computation over ≤40-state automata is computationally tractable and mathematically well-defined. This is legitimate formal reasoning, even though it operates over an approximate model.

2. **Well-defined temporal logic with proper meta-theory.** The QCTL_F fragment includes probability operators (P≥p), path quantifiers (A, E), and temporal modalities (X, U, G, F) with clearly defined semantics over probabilistic transition systems. The identification of QCTL_F as a Pattinson-Schröder coalgebraic logic instantiation—not a novel logic—demonstrates theoretical maturity: the authors correctly position their logic within the established coalgebraic modal logic literature rather than claiming novelty for a known instantiation. The safety fragment restriction is clearly stated, and the relationship to full PCTL* is properly characterized.

3. **CoalCEGAR implements genuine abstraction refinement.** The abstraction lattice over (output_bound, input_bound, tolerance) triples, with component-wise ordering and counterexample-guided refinement, is the closest to actual CEGAR in the LLM analysis literature. The Galois connection structure (Proposition 1, safety fragment) provides the formal basis for preservation of safety properties under abstraction, which is the correct theoretical justification for abstract model checking. The explicit lattice structure enables systematic exploration of the precision-cost tradeoff.

4. **Property-based invariant testing is the right validation approach.** The 38 property-based tests checking coalgebraic invariants serve as an effective substitute for mechanized proofs in the current state of the implementation. Testing functor naturality (F(g ∘ f) = F(g) ∘ F(f)), bisimulation metric properties (symmetry, triangle inequality, identity of indiscernibles), model-checker fixed-point convergence, and CoalCEGAR lattice ordering verifies the algebraic laws computationally. While insufficient as proofs, these tests would catch most implementation errors that violate the mathematical structure.

5. **Theorem 3's error composition is a genuine reasoning contribution.** The identification and additive composition of five error sources—(1) PCL* learning error, (2) abstraction error, (3) model-checking error, (4) satisfaction estimation error, (5) distance computation error—provides a structured reasoning framework for end-to-end error analysis. This is the automated reasoning analogue of a modular proof: each error source can be bounded independently, and the total error is their sum. The corrected formulation (additive rather than multiplicative composition) is honest about the worst-case nature of the bound.

## Weaknesses

1. **No mechanized proofs despite formal methods claims.** The paper proofs for Theorem 1 (PCL* convergence in Õ(2^β · n₀/ε²) queries), Theorem 2 (Bandwidth-Sample Complexity Bound), Theorem 3 (end-to-end error composition), and Proposition 1 (CoalCEGAR monotonicity under safety fragment) are not mechanized in any proof assistant. For a paper positioned at the intersection of formal methods and ML—one that proposes a framework inspired by coalgebraic automata theory and claims multiple theorems about convergence, complexity, and correctness—this is a significant omission. The fact that the original query complexity O(β·n₀) was corrected to O(2^β·n₀)—an exponential factor—demonstrates that paper proofs of these results are error-prone. Similarly, the corrected CoalCEGAR bound and classifier error exponent suggest systematic issues with hand-written proofs. Mechanization in Lean 4 or Coq would have caught all three errors and would provide the community with independently verifiable proof artifacts. The 38 property-based tests are valuable engineering but do not establish universal properties: testing that the triangle inequality holds for 1000 random triples does not prove it holds for all triples.

2. **Non-functorial abstraction breaks the central preservation theorem.** With 0–27% inconsistency between abstract and concrete behavioral models, the abstraction map is not a coalgebra morphism. This breaks the fundamental theorem that justifies abstract model checking: if the abstraction does not commute with the behavioral functor (α ∘ F_C ≠ F_A ∘ α), then satisfaction of QCTL_F formulas on the abstract model does not imply satisfaction on the concrete system. From a logic perspective, this means the model-checking results have no formal connection to the system being analyzed—they are sound only for the abstract model, which is unfaithful to the concrete system. This is equivalent to proving a theorem about the wrong axiom system: the proof may be valid, but the conclusion does not apply to the intended structure. The authors should either achieve functoriality (0% inconsistency) or prove an approximate preservation theorem that bounds the satisfaction error as a function of the inconsistency rate.

3. **Safety fragment only—no liveness, no fairness, limited expressiveness.** The restriction to safety properties means QCTL_F cannot express: "the model eventually provides a helpful response" (liveness: EF helpful), "every refusal is eventually followed by an explanation" (response: AG(refusal → AF explanation)), "the model treats demographic groups fairly over extended interactions" (fairness as a recurrence property). Given that the learned automata have ≤40 states, full CTL* model checking is decidable and computationally feasible—the model-checking complexity for CTL* over finite-state systems is PSPACE-complete in the formula size and polynomial in the state space, which for 40 states is trivially tractable. The restriction to safety appears to be a theoretical limitation of the coalgebraic preservation results (Proposition 1 requires the Galois connection structure specific to safety), not a computational constraint. This should be clearly stated as a theoretical gap, not a principled design choice.

4. **No decision procedure completeness or termination proof.** The model-checking algorithm computes fixed points of the satisfaction function over [0,1]-valued states, but no formal proof of termination is provided. For finite lattices, Kleene's theorem guarantees convergence, but the [0,1] lattice is not finite—convergence depends on continuity properties of the satisfaction operators and the choice of approximation strategy (Kleene iteration, widening, etc.). The paper should either prove that the operators are Scott-continuous (guaranteeing convergence of Kleene iteration) or specify the widening/narrowing strategy and prove its termination. Without this, the model-checking algorithm may not terminate for some inputs, and no complexity bound is established for the graded model-checking problem.

5. **No proof certificate export for independent verification.** The model-checking engine produces satisfaction degrees but does not export proof certificates, witness strategies, or counterexample traces in any standardized format. In automated reasoning, proof certificates are essential for trust: an auditor should be able to independently verify the model-checking result without re-running the entire pipeline. Standardized formats exist—parity game solutions for μ-calculus model checking, strategy certificates for CTL*, witness trees for safety properties—and CABER should export analogous certificates for QCTL_F. Without certificates, the model-checking results are opaque: the auditor must trust the entire ~99K-line codebase rather than verifying a compact proof artifact.

6. **Graded logic calibration error makes quantitative reasoning unsound.** The 0.28–0.73 calibration error for graded satisfaction scores means the quantitative values in [0,1] are unreliable. From a logic perspective, if the truth values are poorly calibrated, any quantitative reasoning that depends on specific satisfaction degrees is unreliable. A threshold query like "is the property satisfied to degree ≥ 0.8?" is meaningless if the calibration error is 0.5. The Łukasiewicz connectives (t-norm: max(0, a+b-1), t-conorm: min(1, a+b)) propagate calibration error through the formula structure: if a has error ε_a and b has error ε_b, the t-norm has error up to ε_a + ε_b. For deeply nested formulas (AG, AF with many temporal unfoldings), the calibration error accumulates through each fixed-point iteration. No analysis of calibration error propagation through the logic is provided.

## Questions for Authors

- What is the fixed-point iteration convergence rate for the graded QCTL_F model checking, and can you prove termination for the [0,1]-valued semantics? Specifically, are your satisfaction operators Scott-continuous, and if so, at what ordinal does the Kleene chain stabilize?
- Could you export model-checking results as proof certificates? For example, for a safety property AG(φ), a certificate would be an invariant I such that I implies φ and I is closed under the transition relation. Can your system extract such invariants from the fixed-point computation?
- What is the formal relationship between QCTL_F and PCTL*? Is QCTL_F a strict syntactic fragment, or does the graded semantics change the expressive power relative to standard PCTL* with probabilistic semantics? Can you characterize the exact expressiveness of QCTL_F relative to known logics?

## Overall Assessment

CABER implements the most substantive automated reasoning components of any LLM behavioral analysis framework: a properly defined temporal logic (QCTL_F), a genuine fixed-point model-checking algorithm, and a principled CEGAR abstraction-refinement loop with explicit lattice structure. These are real formal reasoning contributions that go beyond the superficial use of formal methods terminology that characterizes most competing approaches. However, the reasoning infrastructure has critical gaps that prevent the system from providing formal guarantees. The non-functorial abstraction (0–27% inconsistency) breaks the preservation theorem that justifies abstract model checking. The graded logic operates on poorly calibrated inputs (0.28–0.73 error), making quantitative reasoning unsound. No mechanized proofs validate the core theorems—and the corrected query complexity (exponential factor) demonstrates that hand proofs in this domain are error-prone. No proof certificates enable independent verification. The safety-only restriction limits expressiveness unnecessarily for the given automaton sizes. The gap between the coalgebraic theory's formal promise and the implementation's reasoning capabilities is substantial, but the work correctly identifies the formal reasoning infrastructure needed for LLM behavioral verification and makes genuine progress toward it.
