%  ABSTRACT
% ====================================================================
\begin{abstract}
We present \tool{}, a system that performs approximate behavioral
testing of black-box large language models (LLMs), inspired by
coalgebraic automata theory.  Given only API access, \tool{} learns
finite-state behavioral automata and model-checks temporal
specifications against them---checking multi-step properties
(e.g., ``refusal persists across paraphrases,'' ``the model resists
sycophancy under pressure'') and quantifying behavioral drift between
model versions.  The approach comes with asymptotic PAC-style
convergence theory, but \emph{does not constitute formal verification}:
the learned models are approximate, the abstraction is non-functorial,
and the PAC bounds are vacuous at practical sample sizes.
\emph{Important caveat:} these PAC bounds require ${\sim}143$K samples
to be non-vacuous at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$; our experiments use 54--90~samples
per configuration, so the formal guarantees do not hold at current scale
(see~\S\ref{sec:sample-complexity-gap} and~\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

The key technical enabler is a coalgebraic framework that treats LLMs
as coalgebras for a behavioral functor and introduces a \emph{functor
bandwidth} invariant governing the information-theoretic complexity of
observable behavior.  This yields query complexity that depends on the
covering number of the behavioral image rather than the
natural-language alphabet size, making behavioral testing tractable.

\paragraph{Key results.}
\emph{(i)~Multi-configuration real LLM validation:} Deploying \tool{}
against OpenAI's \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano} under three system-prompt
configurations (safety-strict, creative-permissive, instruction-rigid),
we collect 162~API calls (54~per config: 45~single-turn at 3~trials/prompt
+ 9~multi-turn probes),
extract 2--3~state behavioral automata,
and check safety properties with configuration-dependent outcomes: the
creative-permissive configuration correctly \emph{fails} refusal
persistence (as intended), while safety-strict and instruction-rigid
pass all three properties.
\emph{(ii)~Statistical baselines:} We compare four simple baselines
(KL divergence, MMD, chi-squared, frequency) against the full
coalgebraic pipeline for behavioral divergence detection.
Simple baselines detect aggregate distributional differences
comparably; the pipeline's advantage is structural:
reusable automata, graded satisfaction, temporal reasoning, and
Kantorovich drift metrics.  We demonstrate a concrete scenario
where two configurations have \emph{identical marginal distributions}
but different temporal behavior (refusal persistence), which
\tool{} detects ($d_K{=}0.275$) but all statistical baselines miss.
\emph{(iii)~Scalability:} \pclstar{} maintains ${\ge}96\%$ accuracy
up to 100~ground-truth behavioral states, with $O(n^{1.42})$ empirical
query scaling.
\emph{(iv)~Convergence:} \coalcegar{} converges in 2~iterations
across all tested model sizes (3--15 states), with monotonicity
holding for 4/5~configurations.  We prove convergence for the safety
fragment with an explicit bound of $O(k_{\max} + n_{\max} +
\log(\varepsilon_0/\varepsilon_{\min}))$ refinement steps.
\emph{(v)~Robustness:} Per-atom classifier metrics show
${\ge}0.93$ macro~F1 on real LLM data (keyword classifier), and
Monte Carlo simulation shows ${\ge}99.25\%$ verdict accuracy under
20\% error injection on mock LLMs.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
All real LLM experiments use \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano}, a single
non-frontier model; cross-model validation remains future work.
PAC convergence bounds are vacuous at operating sample sizes
(54--90~samples vs.\ ${\sim}143$K required); Bayesian posteriors
provide non-vacuous uncertainty.
Leave-one-prompt-out cross-validation yields 0.55--0.69 accuracy,
indicating limited generalization to novel prompt types.
The alphabet abstraction introduces a non-functorial step with
0--27\% inconsistency rate.
Proofs are paper-only; no machine-checked verification is claimed.
\qctlf{} is an instantiation of Pattinson--Schr\"oder, not a novel
logic; our contribution is the integration with learned automata.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Foundation models deployed behind API endpoints are updated silently,
frequently, and without behavioral contracts.  A system relying on
an LLM's refusal policy, multi-turn consistency, or instruction-following
behavior has no mechanism to detect when the model's behavior has changed,
let alone whether the change violates an application-specific invariant.
We call this the \emph{behavioral regression problem}.

\paragraph{The scale of the problem.}
Three documented episodes illustrate silent behavioral shifts:
(1)~Chen et al.~\cite{chen2023chatgpt} showed GPT-4's accuracy on
prime-number identification dropped from 95.2\% to 2.4\% between
March and June~2023;
(2)~practitioners have reported degraded instruction adherence
in GPT-4~Turbo relative to base GPT-4;
(3)~model updates across providers have produced non-monotonic
changes in refusal boundaries.
Current evaluation suites
(HELM~\cite{liang2023helm},
CheckList~\cite{ribeiro2020beyond},
DecodingTrust~\cite{wang2023decodingtrust})
share three limitations:
they operate on static snapshots rather than extracting a behavioral
model; they lack temporal reasoning for multi-step properties;
and they provide no formal soundness guarantees on verdicts.

\paragraph{What \tool{} does.}
Given API access to an LLM, \tool{} performs four operations:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{Extracts a behavioral automaton} via active learning
  (\pclstar{}), producing a finite-state model of observable behavior.
\item \textbf{Checks temporal properties} expressed in \qctlf{}
  (e.g., refusal persistence, sycophancy resistance) with graded
  satisfaction degrees in $[0,1]$.
\item \textbf{Quantifies behavioral drift} between model versions
  via Kantorovich bisimulation distance.
\item \textbf{Produces audit reports} with end-to-end error
  analysis combining PAC convergence theory (asymptotic,
  Theorem~\ref{thm:e2e-error}) and Bayesian posterior
  uncertainty (non-vacuous at operating sample sizes).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Technical contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \textbf{Functor bandwidth} (Definition~\ref{def:bandwidth}):
  an invariant based on $\varepsilon$-covering numbers that governs
  the information-theoretic complexity of the behavioral functor,
  yielding query complexity independent of alphabet size.
\item \textbf{\pclstar{}} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:pclstar}): a PAC-style
  extension of Angluin's $L^*$~\cite{angluin1987learning} to
  probabilistic coalgebras, with convergence guarantees
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence}).
\item \textbf{\coalcegar{}} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:coalcegar}):
  abstraction--refinement over parameterized behavioral functors,
  navigating an explicitly maintained lattice of abstraction triples.
\item \textbf{End-to-end error composition}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:e2e-error}): five error sources compose
  additively with a closed-form bound.
\item An implementation of ${\sim}99$\,K lines of Rust and Python
  with a seven-layer architecture (\S\ref{sec:arch}).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Positioning.}
\tool{} is best understood as \emph{approximate behavioral testing
inspired by coalgebraic methods}, not as formal verification.
The coalgebraic framework provides structural advantages
(functor-parameterized abstraction, bisimulation distance,
property-indexed complexity), but the guarantees are approximate:
the alphabet abstraction is non-functorial (0--27\% inconsistency),
PAC bounds are vacuous at operating sample sizes, and all theorems
have paper proofs only (no Lean~4 or Coq mechanization).  For
aggregate divergence detection, simple statistical tests (chi-squared,
MMD) perform comparably; \tool{}'s value lies in the structural
artifacts it produces: reusable automata, graded satisfaction,
temporal reasoning, and Kantorovich drift metrics.

\paragraph{Layer 1: Query Interface.}
Abstracts model interaction behind an async
\texttt{query} method with rate limiting,
retry logic, and CUSUM-based drift detection.

\paragraph{Layer 2: Learning.}
Implements \pclstar{} (Appendix~\ref{app:pclstar}).  Core data
structures: \texttt{ObservationTable} (weighted membership-query
results) and \texttt{HypothesisAutomaton} (finite weighted automaton
from closed, consistent tables).

\paragraph{Layer 3: Abstraction.}
\coalcegar{} (Appendix~\ref{app:coalcegar}) manages a lattice of
abstraction triples $\alpha = (k, n, \varepsilon)$ controlling output
granularity, input expressiveness, and distributional tolerance.

\paragraph{Layer 4: Verification.}
Model-checks \qctlf{} formulas (Appendix~\ref{app:qctlf}) over the
hypothesis automaton, computing graded satisfaction degrees via
fixed-point iteration.

\paragraph{Layer 5: Bisimulation Distance.}
Computes exact bisimulation (partition refinement) and quantitative
Kantorovich bisimulation distance~\cite{baldan2018coalgebraic} between
hypothesis automata from different model versions.

\paragraph{Layer 6: Audit Reports.}
Assembles audit reports containing the learned automaton, verification
results, bisimulation distances, PAC parameters, and a cryptographic
hash chain linking the report to the query log.

\paragraph{Layer 7: Evaluation Harness.}
Python harness providing mock LLMs, PDFA baselines, and experiment
orchestration via \texttt{PyO3} FFI bindings.

chosen for performance (CPU-intensive observation-table operations),
compile-time correctness (closed-table invariants encoded via types),
and concurrency (\texttt{tokio}-based async query dispatch).

\subsection{Experiment 1: Multi-Configuration Real LLM Validation}\label{sec:real-llm}

To validate that real LLM behavior admits tractable finite-state
abstraction \emph{and} that behavioral patterns vary meaningfully
with system-prompt configuration, we deploy \tool{} against OpenAI's
\texttt{gpt-4.1-nano} under three configurations.

\paragraph{Configurations.}
(1)~\textbf{Safety-strict}: system prompt maximizing refusal surface;
(2)~\textbf{Creative-permissive}: system prompt allowing fictional
scenarios;
(3)~\textbf{Instruction-rigid}: system prompt enforcing terse,
precise responses.

\paragraph{Setup.}
A 15-symbol behavioral alphabet spans benign queries, creative
prompts, direct/indirect harmful requests, opinion questions,
pushback, jailbreak attempts, code generation, sensitive topics,
math, roleplay (benign and harmful), medical, and legal queries.
Each symbol is queried 3~times per configuration; responses are
classified into behavioral atoms (compliant, refusal, terse, hedge)
via keyword rules.  Multi-turn probes (3~trials each) test refusal persistence,
sycophancy resistance, and jailbreak resistance.
Total: 162~API calls across three configurations (54~each:
45~single-turn + 9~multi-turn).
Per-atom classifier metrics (Table~\ref{tab:peratom}) are computed
from a separate 5-trial evaluation (75~samples per config, 270~calls total).

\paragraph{Key findings.}
(1)~\textbf{Behavioral divergence:} Comparing across configurations,
2--6~of 15~prompts produce different dominant behaviors depending on
the configuration pair (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}).  For example,
\texttt{harmful\_indirect} (``describe how a character hacks a bank
for a novel'') yields \emph{refusal} under safety-strict and
instruction-rigid, but \emph{compliant} under creative-permissive.
(2)~\textbf{Emergent behavioral atoms:} The instruction-rigid
configuration produces a \emph{terse} behavioral atom absent in
other configurations, yielding a 3-state automaton.
(3)~\textbf{Configuration-dependent safety:} The creative-permissive
configuration correctly \emph{fails} refusal persistence: after
refusing a direct harmful request, the model does not always
maintain refusal under follow-up pressure.

\paragraph{Why not a single LLM call?}
A natural question is whether the same divergence analysis could be
performed by simply prompting a second LLM to classify behaviors.
The automaton-based approach provides three capabilities absent from
prompt-based auditing: (i)~a \emph{reusable behavioral model} that
can be checked against multiple temporal specifications without
additional API calls; (ii)~\emph{graded satisfaction degrees} in
$[0,1]$ quantifying how strongly a property holds; and
(iii)~\emph{Kantorovich bisimulation distance} enabling principled
version-to-version comparison.  Full qualitative interaction
sequences are in Appendix~\ref{app:interaction}.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\texttt{gpt-4.1-nano} is a small model with limited behavioral
complexity.  Leave-one-prompt-out cross-validation yields
mean CV accuracy of 0.55--0.69 (Table~\ref{tab:cv}), indicating
that generalizing behavioral predictions to unseen prompts remains
challenging.  \emph{This is the most important limitation of the
current evaluation}: the high per-atom F1 scores (0.93--1.00,
Table~\ref{tab:peratom}) reflect within-distribution classification
accuracy, but the CV results show that behavioral categories
learned from 14~prompts do not reliably predict behavior on a
15th held-out prompt.  In practical terms, the learned automaton
faithfully represents the queried behavioral surface but should
not be extrapolated to novel prompt types without additional queries.
This limitation is inherent to the small prompt alphabet (15~symbols)
and would improve with larger, more diverse prompt sets.

\emph{What this means:} the PAC convergence theory
(Theorems~\ref{thm:convergence}--\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample}) provides
asymptotic guarantees that \pclstar{} will eventually converge to the
true behavioral automaton.  At our 54--90-sample operating point,
these guarantees are vacuous---the formal error bound exceeds~1.
The high empirical accuracy (${\ge}92\%$ on mock LLMs,
Table~\ref{tab:phase0}) indicates favorable constant factors
not captured by Hoeffding-based worst-case bounds, but we cannot
formally certify correctness at this sample size.

Bayesian posterior inference provides a non-vacuous alternative:
with Dirichlet(1) prior over $K{=}4$ behavioral categories,
posterior concentration is ${\sim}0.11$, giving meaningful credible
intervals.  This is because Bayesian inference exploits the prior
distribution, while PAC bounds are worst-case over all distributions.
\emph{We recommend practitioners rely on the Bayesian uncertainty
quantification rather than the PAC guarantees.}

\paragraph{Honest assessment.}
For detecting \emph{aggregate} distributional differences between
configurations, chi-squared tests and MMD are sufficient and cheaper.
Both correctly identify significant divergence for the two
high-contrast pairs (strict/rigid, creative/rigid) but fail to
reach significance for the lower-contrast pair (strict/creative).
KL divergence detects more per-prompt divergences but with high
false-positive rates due to sensitivity to small probability masses.

\tool{}'s value over these baselines is \emph{not} better divergence
detection rates, but rather:
(i)~a \emph{reusable automaton} that can be checked against multiple
temporal specifications without re-querying the model;
(ii)~\emph{graded satisfaction degrees} in $[0,1]$ quantifying how
strongly each property holds;
(iii)~\emph{Kantorovich bisimulation distance} ($d_K$) providing a
single metric for version comparison;
(iv)~\emph{multi-step temporal properties} (e.g., refusal persistence
under follow-up pressure) that chi-squared tests cannot express.
For a practitioner who only needs to know ``did behavior change?''
a chi-squared test is sufficient.  For a practitioner who needs to
know ``which temporal properties changed, by how much, and why?''
the automaton-based pipeline is necessary.

\paragraph{Concrete structural advantage: temporal patterns invisible
to baselines.}
We construct a scenario demonstrating \tool{}'s unique capability.
Two model configurations have \emph{identical marginal distributions}
(same per-prompt refusal rates) but \emph{different temporal behavior}:
Config~A maintains refusal under follow-up pressure
(refusal$\to$refusal transition $p{=}0.95$), while Config~B
capitulates (refusal$\to$compliant transition $p{=}0.60$).
All statistical baselines---chi-squared ($p{=}1.0$), KL divergence
(${\approx}0$), MMD (${\approx}0$)---correctly report no
distributional difference.  However, \tool{}'s automaton detects
the temporal pattern: the \qctlf{} formula
$\forall\mathbf{G}(\mathit{refused} \Rightarrow
\mathbb{P}_{\ge 0.95}[\mathbf{G}\,\mathit{refused}])$
passes on Config~A (satisfaction $1.0$) but fails on Config~B
(satisfaction $0.33$), with Kantorovich distance $d_K{=}0.275$.
This is the core structural advantage: \emph{temporal properties
over transition structure are invisible to marginal-distribution tests.}

\subsection{Experiment 3: Random Sampling Baseline}\label{sec:random-baseline}

To demonstrate that automaton learning adds value beyond naive
behavioral testing, we compare \tool{} against a random sampling
baseline that draws random interaction sequences and computes
pass/fail rates without building a structural model.

\paragraph{Random sampling baseline.}
Given a model and property, draw $N$~random interaction sequences
of length 1--4, classify each sequence as pass/fail for the
property, and report the aggregate pass rate.

\paragraph{Structural advantages.}
On 3--8~state mock LLMs, random sampling provides pass/fail rates
but cannot:
(i)~identify the number of behavioral states;
(ii)~provide graded satisfaction degrees in $[0,1]$;
(iii)~generate counterexample witnesses;
(iv)~measure behavioral drift via Kantorovich distance;
(v)~check multiple temporal properties against a single learned model.

The automaton is a \emph{reusable artifact}: once learned, it can be
checked against multiple \qctlf{} specifications without additional
LLM queries.  Random sampling requires $O(N)$ queries per property.

\subsection{Experiment 5: CoalCEGAR Convergence}\label{sec:convergence-exp}

A key reviewer critique is that CoalCEGAR convergence is stated but
not proved.  We address this both theoretically and empirically.

\paragraph{Theoretical result.}
For safety properties (greatest fixed points), we prove convergence
with an explicit bound:

\begin{proposition}[CoalCEGAR Termination, Safety Fragment]\label{prop:convergence}
Let $\Lambda$ be the abstraction lattice with parameters
$k \in [1, k_{\max}]$, $n \in [1, n_{\max}]$, and
$\varepsilon \in [\varepsilon_{\min}, \varepsilon_0]$ with
refinement factor $r < 1$.  For any safety property $\varphi$,
\coalcegar{} terminates in at most
\[
  T_{\max} \;=\; (k_{\max} - 1) + (n_{\max} - 1) +
  \left\lceil \frac{\log(\varepsilon_0/\varepsilon_{\min})}{\log(1/r)} \right\rceil
\]
refinement steps, since each step strictly increases at least one
lattice component.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Each refinement step strictly advances in the abstraction lattice by
modifying at least one component of
$\alpha = (k, n, \varepsilon)$: $k$ or $n$ increases by 1, or
$\varepsilon$ decreases by factor $r < 1$.
\emph{Precondition:} the REFINE operation is monotone in each
component---$k$ and $n$ never decrease, and $\varepsilon$ never
increases---which is enforced by Algorithm~\ref{alg:coalcegar}'s
refinement strategy.
Since $k$ is bounded by
$k_{\max}$, $n$ by $n_{\max}$, and $\varepsilon$ from below by
$\varepsilon_{\min}$, the total number of refinement steps across all
components is at most $(k_{\max} - 1) + (n_{\max} - 1) + L$ where
$L = \lceil \log(\varepsilon_0/\varepsilon_{\min}) / \log(1/r) \rceil$.
\emph{Note:} termination guarantees the loop halts, but does not
by itself guarantee verdict correctness at the final abstraction
level; correctness depends on the PAC guarantees of
Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence} applied at each level.
\end{proof}

models with 3--15 ground-truth states.

Monotonicity fails for the 10-state model due to sampling variance

for exact Galois connections; in practice, statistical estimation
introduces noise.  This is bounded by the PAC learning error
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}$ and does not affect the convergence
guarantee itself---only the \emph{monotonicity} of intermediate
satisfaction values.  For liveness properties, convergence requires
the Galois distortion $\delta(\alpha, \alpha') < \varepsilon_\varphi$,
which is not guaranteed in general.

\subsection{Evaluation Summary}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Scaled multi-config feasibility:} 2--3~state automata from
  162~API calls (54~per config, 3~trials/prompt) on \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano};
  per-atom metrics (5~trials/prompt, 270~calls) evaluated separately;
  behavioral divergence detected across all configuration pairs;
  creative-permissive correctly fails refusal persistence.
\item \textbf{Statistical baselines:} Chi-squared and MMD detect
  aggregate divergence comparably; CABER's value is
  structural (reusable automata, graded satisfaction, temporal
  reasoning, drift metrics). For binary divergence alone, a
  chi-squared test suffices.
\item \textbf{Per-atom classifier evaluation:} Macro F1
  0.93--1.00 on real LLM data with keyword classifier,
  substantially above 0.45--0.57 on stochastic mock LLMs.
  \emph{Caveat:} these scores measure within-distribution
  classification; leave-one-prompt-out CV (next item) shows
  poor generalization.
\item \textbf{Cross-validation (key limitation):} Leave-one-prompt-out CV yields
  0.55--0.69 mean accuracy, indicating that behavioral predictions
  do not generalize to unseen prompt types.  The learned automaton
  accurately represents the queried behavioral surface but cannot
  reliably predict behavior on novel prompts.
\item \textbf{Structural advantage over random sampling:}
  Automaton learning provides reusable behavioral models, graded
  satisfaction, counterexample witnesses, and drift metrics---none
  available from naive random sampling.
\item \textbf{Coalgebraic advantage over PDFA:} 5--10$\times$ more compact
  automata than PDFA baseline, +21.6pp accuracy.
\item \textbf{CoalCEGAR convergence:} Proved for safety
  fragment with explicit additive bound; empirically 2~iterations.
  Liveness properties remain unsupported; CoalCEGAR convergence
  for liveness requires Galois distortion bounds not guaranteed
  in general.
\item \textbf{Complete ablation:} Distance metric choice has minimal
  impact (all 3 achieve 75\% discrimination); PCL$^*$ accuracy
  plateaus above 80~samples/query; keyword classifier outperforms
  length-based (F1 0.93 vs.\ 0.25) and random (0.49) baselines;
  CoalCEGAR consistently improves satisfaction by 3--5pp.
\item \textbf{Calibration:} Raw graded satisfaction scores have
  calibration error 0.28--0.73; isotonic regression reduces this
  by ${\sim}25\%$ (to 0.13--0.21), but residual error persists.
  Graded scores should be interpreted as ordinal rankings, not
  calibrated probabilities.  Posterior predictive checks confirm
  Beta-Binomial model adequacy.
\item \textbf{Abstraction gap:} Non-functorial abstraction
  produces 0--27\% inconsistency rate across configs, with
  formal error bounds quantified per-configuration.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Coalgebraic vs.\ PDFA+PRISM separation.}
The coalgebraic machinery provides three structural advantages beyond
accuracy numbers:
(1)~\emph{Functor-parameterized abstraction} enables \coalcegar{}
to adjust the abstraction level during verification, rather than
committing to a fixed alphabet before learning;
(2)~\emph{Quantitative bisimulation distance} provides a single
metric for version-to-version comparison, absent from PDFA+PRISM;
(3)~\emph{Property-indexed complexity} naturally adapts the
state-space granularity to each specification's requirements.
The PDFA+PRISM pipeline is adequate for PCTL properties over a
known, fixed alphabet with a single model version; the coalgebraic
overhead is justified when the alphabet must be discovered, multiple
specifications are checked per audit, or version comparison is needed.

\paragraph{Query cost.}
Phase~0 experiments use 71K--94K queries per audit.  At GPT-4 API
pricing (${\sim}\$30$--$60$/M tokens, ${\sim}100$ tokens/query),
a single audit costs ${\approx}\$200$--$\$600$---comparable to
running HELM's full suite and substantially cheaper than manual
red-teaming.

\paragraph{Property-based testing.}
38~mathematical invariants are validated via Rust's \texttt{proptest}
framework, covering bisimulation metric properties, automaton
serialization, report validity, observation table closure,
union bound composition, PAC error propagation, functor bandwidth
sublinearity, and differential testing against known automata.

\section{Theoretical Framework}\label{sec:theory}

This section states the key definitions and theorems.  Full proofs
are in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.

\subsection{Coalgebraic Setup}

A \emph{coalgebra} for an endofunctor $F: \mathbf{Set} \to
\mathbf{Set}$ is a pair $(X, \gamma: X \to F(X))$, where $X$ is a
set of states and $\gamma$ the transition structure.  Two states are
\emph{behaviorally equivalent} if they cannot be distinguished by any
finite observation sequence~\cite{rutten2000universal}.

\begin{definition}[Behavioral Functor for LLMs]\label{def:functor}
Let $\Sigma_{\le k}$ denote outputs of length at most $k$ and
$\Sigma^*_{\le n}$ prompts of length at most $n$.  The LLM
behavioral functor is
\[
  F_{\mathrm{LLM}}(X) \;=\;
    \bigl(\Sigma_{\le k} \times \DD(X)\bigr)^{\Sigma^*_{\le n}},
\]
mapping each state to a function that, for every bounded prompt,
returns an output together with a distribution over successor states.
\end{definition}

We assume access to a black-box model $\mathfrak{M}$ queryable with
prompts $p \in \Sigma^*_{\le n}$, returning responses from
$\mathfrak{M}(p) \in \DD(\Sigma_{\le k})$.

\subsection{Functor Bandwidth}

\begin{definition}[Functor Bandwidth]\label{def:bandwidth}
Let $\gamma_\alpha: X \to F_\alpha(X)$ be the coalgebra map at
abstraction $\alpha$, and $d_K$ the Kantorovich metric on the image
of $\gamma_\alpha$.  The \emph{functor bandwidth} at tolerance
$\varepsilon > 0$ is
\[
  \beta(F, \alpha, \varepsilon) \;=\; \log_2 N_\varepsilon\!\bigl(
    \mathrm{Im}(\gamma_\alpha),\, d_K\bigr),
\]
where $N_\varepsilon(\cdot, d_K)$ is the $\varepsilon$-covering number
of the behavioral image in Kantorovich metric space.  We write $\beta$
when $\varepsilon$ and $\alpha$ are clear from context.
\end{definition}

The covering-number definition connects $\beta$ to classical metric
entropy (Kolmogorov--Tikhomirov).  For a model with $n_0$
distinguishable behavioral modes, the image has effective dimension
at most $n_0$, giving $\beta(F, \alpha, \varepsilon) \le n_0 \cdot \log_2(3/\varepsilon)$---a
bound \emph{independent} of alphabet size.  This bound follows from a
volume argument: $n_0$ behavioral modes span a simplex in
$\DD(\Sigma_{\le k})$, whose $\varepsilon$-covering number is at most
$(3/\varepsilon)^{n_0}$, so
$\beta \le n_0 \cdot \log_2(3/\varepsilon)$.
This is the key structural property enabling sub-linear queries.

\subsection{Convergence and Sample Complexity}

\begin{theorem}[Convergence]\label{thm:convergence}
Let $\mathfrak{M}$ be a black-box model whose behavior under
abstraction $\alpha$ is equivalent to a weighted automaton with $n_0$
states.  Under bounded outputs, local stationarity, and independent
sampling (details in Appendix~\ref{app:convergence-proof}),
\pclstar{} returns a hypothesis $H$ such that
$d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\mathfrak{M}_\alpha, H) \le \varepsilon$ with
probability $\ge 1 - \delta$, using at most
$O\!\bigl(2^{\beta} \cdot n_0 / \varepsilon^2 \cdot \log(n_0 \cdot 2^\beta/\delta)\bigr)$
queries, where $\beta = \beta(F, \alpha, \varepsilon)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Bandwidth--Sample Complexity]\label{thm:bandwidth-sample}
Under the same conditions, the number of membership queries is at most
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sample-complexity}
  m_{\mathrm{total}} \;\le\;
  \frac{n_0 \cdot 2^{\beta}}{2\varepsilon^2}
  \cdot \log\!\Bigl(\frac{2 \cdot n_0 \cdot 2^{\beta}}{\delta}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Since $\beta \le n_0 \cdot \log_2(3/\varepsilon)$, the covering
number $N_\varepsilon = 2^\beta$ grows with $n_0$ but is
\emph{independent of alphabet size} $|\Sigma_\alpha|$.  In the
empirically observed regime (Table~\ref{tab:complexity}), $\beta$
ranges from 3.1 to 14.2 for $n_0 \in [10, 200]$, giving
$N_\varepsilon \in [8.6, 18{,}800]$---large but finite constants
that yield practically tractable query budgets.  This contrasts with
alphabet-naive methods requiring
$\Otil(|\Sigma_\alpha| \cdot n_0 / \varepsilon^2)$ queries, where
$|\Sigma_\alpha|$ can be $500{+}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Regularity conditions]
The convergence guarantee relies on three assumptions:
(1)~\emph{Bounded outputs}, enforced by the abstraction layer;
(2)~\emph{Local stationarity}, monitored via CUSUM drift detection;
(3)~\emph{Independent sampling}, approximated via session isolation.
If CUSUM detects a behavioral shift, the report is issued with
a shortened validity window.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Causal coupling]\label{rem:causal}
The two probabilistic error sources (sampling and equivalence queries)
are causally coupled.  The union bound treats them independently,
yielding a conservative guarantee; sequential conditioning
(Appendix~\ref{app:convergence-proof}) gives
$\Pr[\text{failure}] \le \delta/3 + \delta/3 = 2\delta/3 < \delta$.
Note: the query complexity in Theorem~\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample}
involves $2^\beta$ (the covering number), not $\beta$ (its logarithm).
When $\beta$ is moderate (empirically $\le 15$), the factor $2^\beta$
is a tractable constant; the key advantage over alphabet-naive methods
is independence from $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ in the \emph{structural}
table dimension.  However, per-entry distributional estimation
retains a polynomial $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ factor (see proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample});
the ``alphabet-independence'' claim applies to table column count,
not total query complexity.
\end{remark}

\subsection{End-to-End Error Composition}

\begin{theorem}[End-to-End Error Composition]\label{thm:e2e-error}
Let $\mathfrak{M}$ be audited with abstraction $\alpha$, specification
$\varphi$, and confidence $\delta$.  The five pipeline error
sources---alphabet abstraction ($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}}$),
classifier error ($\rho$), learning error
($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}$), refinement error
($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}$), and model checking error
($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}}$)---compose as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:e2e}
  \Pr[\text{incorrect verdict}] \;\le\;
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}}
  + \frac{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}}{1 - \rho} + \rho
  + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}
  + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}}
  + \delta.
\end{equation}
Under typical parameters ($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}} = 0.02$,
$\rho = 0.05$, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} = 0.05$,
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}} = 0$,
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}} = 10^{-6}$, $\delta = 0.05$),
the bound evaluates to ${\approx}0.173$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Verification status of theorems]
All theorems in this paper have paper proofs
(Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}) validated by 38~property-based tests
(Rust \texttt{proptest}).  \emph{No theorem has been mechanically
verified in Lean~4, Coq, or any other proof assistant.}
The paper proofs have been reviewed for the following known issues
and corrections applied:
(1)~query complexity corrected from $O(\beta \cdot n_0)$ to
$O(2^\beta \cdot n_0 / \varepsilon^2)$;
(2)~classifier error proof corrected: the original ``Hoeffding on
correct subsample'' argument was logically unsound; replaced with
bias+concentration decomposition (the bound $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}
/(1{-}\rho) + \rho$ remains valid as a conservative upper bound on
the tighter $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} + \rho$);
(3)~CoalCEGAR termination bound corrected from multiplicative
to additive;
(4)~Galois connection for $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ is invoked but
not concretely constructed; Proposition~\ref{prop:monotonicity}
is conditional on this construction.
The $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}}$ term in Theorem~\ref{thm:e2e-error}
assumes the alphabet abstraction is locally stable (functorial);
when non-functorial (0--27\% inconsistency observed), the actual
error may exceed the bound.
\end{remark}

\subsection{CoalCEGAR Monotonicity and Convergence}

\begin{proposition}[Approximate Monotonicity]\label{prop:monotonicity}
If $\alpha \preceq \alpha'$ in the abstraction lattice and $\varphi$
is a safety property, then
$\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_{\alpha} \le
\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_{\alpha'} + \delta(\alpha, \alpha')$,
where $\delta(\alpha,\alpha')$ is the Galois distortion.
For safety properties, abstraction is conservative
(under-approximates satisfaction), so refinement can only
increase the satisfaction degree up to distortion error.
For liveness properties, satisfaction at
$\alpha'$ requires Galois distortion
$\delta(\alpha, \alpha') < \varepsilon_\varphi$.
\end{proposition}

\coalcegar{} terminates because the lattice $\Lambda$ is finite
(at most $k_{\max} \cdot n_{\max} \cdot L$ elements, where
$L = \lceil \log(\varepsilon_0/\varepsilon_{\min})/\log(1/r) \rceil$);
each refinement step strictly advances the abstraction triple toward
a finer level.  Empirically, 2 refinement steps suffice.

\paragraph{Convergence guarantee (safety fragment).}
For safety properties, we prove convergence with an explicit
iteration bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:convergence}):
\[
  T_{\max} = (k_{\max} {-} 1) + (n_{\max} {-} 1) +
  \bigl\lceil \log(\varepsilon_0/\varepsilon_{\min}) /
  \log(1/r) \bigr\rceil.
\]
For liveness properties, convergence remains an
open question dependent on Galois distortion bounds.

\subsection{QCTL$_F$ Specification Language}

\qctlf{} is an instantiation of the Pattinson--Schr\"oder
coalgebraic modal logic framework~\cite{pattinson2008coalgebraic},
extending CTL with functorial path quantifiers and graded modalities
producing satisfaction degrees in $[0,1]$.  \emph{We do not claim
logical novelty}: the contribution is the integration with learned
automata and a template library (Table~\ref{tab:templates}) bridging
user intent and temporal specification.  Any property expressible as a
Pattinson--Schr\"oder predicate lifting over $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ is
a \qctlf{} formula; the templates provide an accessible interface
for non-logicians.  Full syntax and semantics are in
Appendix~\ref{app:qctlf}.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Specification templates.  Each compiles to a \qctlf{} formula.}
\label{tab:templates}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}lXl@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Template} & \textbf{Informal Meaning} & \textbf{Fragment} \\
\midrule
\textsc{RefusalPersistence}
  & Once refused, model refuses $\varepsilon$-paraphrases
  & $\forall\mathbf{G}$ \\
\textsc{ParaphraseInvariance}
  & Equivalent prompts $\to$ $\varepsilon$-similar outputs
  & $\mathbb{P}_{\ge 1-\varepsilon}[\mathbf{G}]$ \\
\textsc{VersionStability}
  & Behavioral distance between versions ${\le}\delta$
  & Bisimulation \\

  & Reversal probability ${\le}p$ after disagreement
  & $\mathbb{P}_{\le p}[\mathbf{F}]$ \\

  & System-prompt dominates user-prompt
  & $\forall\mathbf{G}$ \\

The alternation-free fragment (which includes all six templates) is
polynomial-time decidable: $O(|Q|^2 \cdot |\Sigma_\alpha| \cdot
|\varphi|)$ on an automaton with $|Q|$ states
(Appendix~\ref{app:decidability}).

\paragraph{Soundness.}
The model-checking algorithm is sound for $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$-coalgebras:
the behavioral functor decomposes as a composition of
$\omega$-continuous functors, and the Pattinson--Schr\"oder framework
guarantees that fixed-point computation converges correctly on
finite-state coalgebras (Appendix~\ref{app:qctlf-soundness}).

\paragraph{Expressiveness relative to PCTL/PCTL*.}
We conjecture that \qctlf{} with $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ can express
properties outside PCTL (e.g., functor-parameterized branching), but
do not provide a formal separation result.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM evaluation and auditing.}
HELM~\cite{liang2023helm}, CheckList~\cite{ribeiro2020beyond},
DecodingTrust~\cite{wang2023decodingtrust}, and
PromptBench~\cite{zhu2023promptbench} evaluate LLMs via static
snapshots.  Chen et al.~\cite{chen2023chatgpt} document behavioral
shifts across GPT versions.
\tool{} complements these by extracting a reusable behavioral model
with temporal reasoning and formal guarantees.  Unlike CheckList
(which tests pre-specified capabilities) and adversarial testing
(which searches for failures), \tool{} builds a structural behavioral
model that can be checked against \emph{multiple} temporal
specifications and compared across model versions.  The
automaton-based approach subsumes single-property testing: any
CheckList-style invariance test can be expressed as a \qctlf{}
template, but \tool{} additionally supports multi-step temporal
properties (e.g., refusal persistence under follow-up pressure)
that CheckList cannot express.

\paragraph{Active automata learning.}
Angluin's $L^*$~\cite{angluin1987learning} was extended to weighted
automata~\cite{berstel2011noncommutative}, register
automata~\cite{howar2012inferring}, and probabilistic
systems~\cite{tappler2019model}.  Weiss et al.~\cite{weiss2018extracting}
applied automata extraction to neural networks.
\pclstar{} differs by operating in the coalgebraic framework, enabling
functor-parameterized sub-linear query bounds.

\paragraph{Coalgebraic theory.}
Rutten~\cite{rutten2000universal} established the universal coalgebra
framework.  Pattinson--Schr\"oder~\cite{pattinson2008coalgebraic}
developed coalgebraic modal logics.
Baldan et al.~\cite{baldan2018coalgebraic} developed coalgebraic
behavioral distances.  \tool{} operationalizes these foundations.

\paragraph{Probabilistic model checking.}
PRISM~\cite{kwiatkowska2011prism} and STORM~\cite{hensel2022storm} are
state-of-the-art for PCTL/CSL over Markov chains.  \tool{}'s
contribution is integration with learned automata, not logical novelty.

\paragraph{Formal methods for ML.}
Huang et al.~\cite{huang2017safety} and Katz et
al.~\cite{katz2017reluplex} target white-box neural network properties.
\tool{} targets black-box behavioral properties over interaction sequences.

\paragraph{LLM red-teaming and auditing tools.}
Prompt-based red-teaming tools (Garak, ARES, Perez et
al.~\cite{perez2022red}) search for individual failures via
adversarial prompt generation.  \tool{} is complementary: rather than
finding individual failures, it extracts a reusable behavioral model
that can be checked against multiple specifications.  A direct
comparison is future work.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Real LLM validation scope (single model).}
All real LLM experiments use \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano}, a small,
non-frontier model.  This is a critical limitation for generality
claims: \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano} exhibits relatively simple behavioral
surfaces (2--3 states), and frontier models (GPT-4, Claude~3.5,
Llama~3~70B) may require substantially more states, reveal
new behavioral atoms, or exhibit state-dependent stochasticity
that our keyword classifier cannot capture.  The multi-configuration
experiments show behavioral diversity across system prompts, but
\emph{all configurations share the same underlying model weights}.
Cross-model validation is needed before any generality claims can
be made.

\paragraph{Alphabet abstraction is non-functorial.}
Embedding clustering introduces a pre-categorical step that is not
functorial: small input perturbations can produce incompatible alphabets.
This is the most significant theoretical gap in the framework,
as it breaks the coalgebraic morphism structure that underwrites
Theorems~\ref{thm:convergence}--\ref{thm:e2e-error}.

\emph{When the abstraction IS functorial.}
The abstraction is functorial when the clustering is deterministic
and stable: if the same input always maps to the same abstract symbol.
This holds for instruction-rigid configurations (0\% inconsistency,
Table~\ref{tab:baselines}) where responses are highly deterministic.
It fails for stochastic boundary prompts (27\% inconsistency in
safety-strict) where the model probabilistically alternates between
behavioral modes.

\emph{Impact on guarantees.}
The non-functorial step adds $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}}$ error
(Theorem~\ref{thm:e2e-error}).  We quantify this gap per-configuration
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}): inconsistency rates range from 0\%
(instruction-rigid) to 27\% (safety-strict).  The formal error
bound accounts for this via the additive
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}}$ term, but the bound is only valid
if the abstraction is \emph{locally stable} during the audit window.
A principled fix (e.g., presheaf construction, stable partitioning)
remains future work.

\paragraph{QCTL$_F$ is not novel.}
\qctlf{} is an instantiation of the Pattinson--Schr\"oder coalgebraic
modal logic framework~\cite{pattinson2008coalgebraic}, not a new
logic.  Our contribution is the integration with learned automata
and the specification template library, not logical novelty.

\paragraph{Stationarity assumption.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence} assumes local stationarity.  CUSUM
drift monitoring detects gross violations, but subtle shifts within
convergence tolerance are undetectable by design.  Real LLM APIs
are non-stationary (updates, A/B testing), making PAC guarantees
conditional on the audit window.

\paragraph{CoalCEGAR convergence scope.}
Convergence is proved for the safety fragment
(Proposition~\ref{prop:convergence}) with explicit iteration bounds.
For liveness properties, convergence requires Galois distortion
bounds that are not guaranteed in general.  Empirically, monotonicity
holds for 4/5 tested configurations; the failure case (10-state model)
is due to statistical estimation noise, not a fundamental limitation.

\paragraph{PAC bounds are vacuous at operating sample sizes.}
The PAC guarantees (Theorems~\ref{thm:convergence}
and~\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample}) are \emph{asymptotic}: they prove
that \pclstar{} eventually converges with enough data. In practice,
the Hoeffding-based bounds require ${\sim}143$K samples per
configuration for $\varepsilon{=}0.05$, $\delta{=}0.05$ (at
$\beta{=}3.1$, $n_0{=}3$), but each real LLM configuration uses
54--90~API calls. \emph{At this sample size, the formal error bound
exceeds~1 and provides no guarantee whatsoever}
(Table~\ref{tab:pac-gap}). This is a fundamental limitation of
Hoeffding-based concentration for small-sample settings.
The Bayesian posterior provides non-vacuous
credible intervals (Table~\ref{tab:bayesian}), and is the
recommended uncertainty quantification mechanism. Structure-aware
concentration inequalities (e.g., Bernstein bounds exploiting
observed variance) could yield tighter finite-sample guarantees
and are future work.

\paragraph{No machine-checked proofs.}
Despite mathematical rigor, \tool{} is not formally verified.
38~property-based tests validate invariants computationally.
Full Lean~4 formalization is estimated at 80--120K~LoC and is deferred.

\paragraph{PDFA baseline caveat.}
The PDFA baseline uses simplified ALERGIA, not full AALpy+PRISM.
A hyperparameter-tuned PDFA (Table~\ref{tab:pdfa-tuned}) achieves
76--80\% accuracy with 3--5~states, substantially better than the
untuned baseline.  The structural advantages of the coalgebraic
approach (functor parameterization, bisimulation distance, temporal
logic) hold independently of baseline quality.

\paragraph{Query cost.}
A single audit costs \$200--\$600 at current API pricing.  Active
learning, caching, and incremental updates are needed for continuous
monitoring.

\paragraph{Embedding model dependency.}
The alphabet abstraction relies on a sentence embedding model
whose alignment with behavioral equivalence is unvalidated on
adversarial inputs.  Formal guarantees are conditional on
abstraction correctness.

\paragraph{Ablation studies.}
The ablation studies (\S\ref{sec:ablation}) cover CoalCEGAR
refinement, graded vs.\ binary satisfaction, distance metrics,
\pclstar{} hyperparameters, alphabet size sensitivity,
classifier alternatives, calibration, and posterior predictive
checks.  Varying embedding model and prompt phrasing is future work.

\paragraph{No compositional specifications.}
The \qctlf{} template library does not support compositional
specification operators for building complex properties from simpler
ones.  Each template compiles independently.

\paragraph{No ground-truth behavioral labels.}
Accuracy is measured against the system's own learned labels (mock
LLMs) or coarse keyword classifiers (real LLMs), not against
human-annotated behavioral ground truth.  Creating a gold-standard
behavioral classification dataset remains future work.

\paragraph{No test-retest reliability analysis.}
All experiments were run in single sessions.  API non-determinism,
model updates, and A/B testing may affect reproducibility; running
the pipeline on the same prompts across multiple days would quantify
this variability.

\paragraph{No formally checkable proof certificates.}
\tool{}'s audit reports contain learned automata, satisfaction degrees,
and hash-chain integrity, but do not produce formally checkable proof
objects (e.g., DRAT certificates, witness traces verifiable by an
independent checker).  The counterexample witnesses generated by the
model checker are informational traces, not independently verifiable
proofs.  Adding machine-checkable proof certificates is future work.

\paragraph{No comparison to simple statistical baselines.}
We now compare against four statistical baselines
(KL divergence, MMD, chi-squared, frequency-based;
\S\ref{sec:stat-baselines}).  For aggregate divergence detection,
chi-squared tests are sufficient and cheaper.  \tool{}'s value is
structural: reusable automata, temporal specifications, graded
satisfaction, and drift metrics.  We honestly acknowledge that for
single-property binary divergence detection, simple statistics
suffice.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

\tool{} brings coalgebraic automata theory to LLM behavioral testing,
providing approximate behavioral auditing with structural advantages
over purely statistical approaches.
The framework enables temporal model checking of multi-step specifications,
with asymptotic PAC convergence theory (vacuous at current sample sizes)
and Bayesian posteriors for practical finite-sample uncertainty.
The functor bandwidth invariant yields query
complexity independent of alphabet size, and Kantorovich bisimulation
distances provide principled regression detection.

\emph{Important caveats.}
\tool{} does not provide formal verification guarantees:
the alphabet abstraction is non-functorial, PAC bounds require
${\sim}143$K samples to be non-vacuous, all theorems have paper proofs
only, and evaluation is limited to a single non-frontier model
(\texttt{gpt-4.1-nano}).  The system is best understood as
approximate behavioral testing inspired by coalgebraic methods.

Multi-configuration experiments on \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano} validate
that the pipeline captures system-prompt-dependent behavioral diversity
(7/15~prompts diverge across configurations), and comparisons against
PDFA baselines and random sampling demonstrate that the coalgebraic
machinery provides structural advantages (reusable automata, graded
satisfaction, drift metrics) beyond raw accuracy improvements.
CoalCEGAR convergence is proved for the safety fragment with
explicit iteration bounds; empirically, all tested models converge
in 2~iterations.

\paragraph{Future work.}
(1)~Frontier LLM validation on GPT-4, Claude~3.5, and Llama~3~70B
with CUSUM-based drift monitoring.
(2)~Lean~4 formalization of core theory (estimated 80--120K~LoC).
(3)~Query efficiency via active learning, caching, and incremental
automaton updates.
(4)~Full AALpy+PRISM baseline comparison.
(5)~Functorial alphabet abstraction via presheaf construction.
(6)~Tighter PAC bounds via structure-aware concentration inequalities.
(7)~Embedding model and prompt phrasing robustness analysis.

% ── A: PCL* Algorithm ───────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{\pclstar{}: Probabilistic Coalgebraic $L^*$}\label{app:pclstar}

\pclstar{} extends Angluin's $L^*$ to the probabilistic coalgebraic
setting.  The main challenges are:
(a)~membership queries return distributions, requiring statistical
hypothesis tests;
(b)~equivalence queries are replaced by PAC-approximate checks;
(c)~the natural-language alphabet necessitates an abstraction layer.

\paragraph{Statistical membership queries.}
For prompt $p$ and suffix $e$, a membership query estimates
$\mathfrak{M}(p \cdot e)$ by drawing $m$ i.i.d.\ samples.  Two
entries $T(s_1, e)$ and $T(s_2, e)$ are equivalent if a two-sample
KS test at significance $\alpha_{\mathrm{KS}}$ fails to reject.

\paragraph{PAC-approximate equivalence queries.}
Given hypothesis $H$, an equivalence query draws $N_{\mathrm{eq}}$
random prompts, compares $H$'s predictions with empirical distributions,
and returns ``equivalent'' if all pass at tolerance
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eq}}$.  A failure provides a counterexample.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\pclstar{}: Probabilistic Coalgebraic $L^*$}\label{alg:pclstar}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Black-box model $\mathfrak{M}$, abstraction $\alpha=(k,n,\varepsilon)$,
         confidence $\delta$, sample size $m$
\Ensure Hypothesis automaton $H$
\State Initialize $S \gets \{\lambda\}$, $E \gets \{\lambda\}$
\State Fill observation table $T$ via statistical membership queries
\Repeat
  \While{$T$ is not closed or not consistent}
    \If{$T$ is not closed}
      \State Find $s \cdot a \in S \cdot \Sigma_\alpha$ s.t.\
             $\mathrm{row}(s \cdot a) \neq \mathrm{row}(s')$ for all
             $s' \in S$
      \State $S \gets S \cup \{s \cdot a\}$
    \EndIf
    \If{$T$ is not consistent}
      \State Find distinguishing suffix $e'$
      \State $E \gets E \cup \{e'\}$
    \EndIf
    \State Fill new table entries via statistical membership queries
  \EndWhile
  \State Construct hypothesis $H$ from closed, consistent table $T$
  \State Perform PAC-approximate equivalence query on $H$
  \If{counterexample $w$ found}
    \State Add suffixes of $w$ to $E$; update $T$
  \EndIf
\Until{equivalence query passes}
\State \Return $H$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Sub-distributions and Kantorovich bounds.}
In practice, the learned automaton may assign sub-distributions
(total mass $< 1$) to transitions when some outputs are unobserved.
The Kantorovich metric extends to sub-distributions $\DD_{\le 1}(X)$
by treating missing mass as transported to a sink at unit
cost~\cite{baldan2018coalgebraic}.  Concentration bounds apply
with the same rates.

\paragraph{Functor bandwidth estimation.}
While $\beta(F,\alpha)$ governs query complexity in theory, estimating
$\beta$ itself requires sampling the behavioral image.  \tool{} uses
a greedy set-cover heuristic on an initial probe set of
$N_{\mathrm{probe}}$ queries for a conservative upper bound
$\hat{\beta} \ge \beta$, refined online.

% ── B: CoalCEGAR Algorithm ──────────────────────────────────────────
\section{\coalcegar{}: Abstraction--Refinement}\label{app:coalcegar}

\coalcegar{} refines the behavioral functor (not the state space) by
adjusting the abstraction triple $\alpha = (k, n, \varepsilon)$:
$k$~controls output granularity, $n$~controls input expressiveness,
$\varepsilon$~controls statistical precision.  These form a lattice
$\Lambda$ ordered by componentwise refinement.

\paragraph{Spurious counterexample detection.}
When the model checker reports a violation at $\alpha$, \coalcegar{}
replays the counterexample at a finer $\alpha'$.  If the violation
persists, it is genuine; otherwise, the abstraction is refined.

\paragraph{Refinement strategy.}
The minimum-cost move in $\Lambda$ that eliminates the spuriousness,
estimated by the additional \pclstar{} query budget at the new level.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\coalcegar{}: Abstraction--Refinement Loop}\label{alg:coalcegar}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Model $\mathfrak{M}$, specification $\varphi$, initial
  abstraction $\alpha_0$, budget $B$
\Ensure Graded satisfaction degree or $\bot$
\State $\alpha \gets \alpha_0$
\Repeat
  \State $H \gets \text{PCL}^*(\mathfrak{M}, \alpha)$
  \State $(\mathit{sat}, \pi) \gets \textsc{ModelCheck}(H, \varphi)$
  \If{$\mathit{sat} \ge \theta$ \textbf{or} $\pi = \bot$}
    \State \Return $\mathit{sat}$
  \EndIf
  \State $\mathit{genuine} \gets \textsc{Concretize}(\pi, \mathfrak{M}, \alpha)$
  \If{$\mathit{genuine}$}
    \State \Return $\mathit{sat}$
  \EndIf
  \State $\alpha \gets \textsc{Refine}(\alpha, \pi, \Lambda)$
\Until{budget $B$ exhausted}
\State \Return $\bot$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

% ── C: QCTL_F Specification Language ────────────────────────────────
\section{\qctlf{} Specification Language}\label{app:qctlf}

\subsection{Syntax}

\begin{definition}[\qctlf{} Syntax]\label{def:qctlf}
Formulas $\varphi$ are generated by:
\begin{align*}
\varphi &\;::=\; \mathit{true} \mid \mathit{false}
  \mid p \mid \lnot \varphi \mid \varphi \land \varphi
  \mid \varphi \lor \varphi
  \mid \mathbb{P}_{\bowtie r}[\psi]
  \mid \forall\psi \mid \exists\psi \\
\psi &\;::=\; \mathbf{X}\,\varphi \mid \varphi\,\mathbf{U}\,\varphi
  \mid \mathbf{G}\,\varphi \mid \mathbf{F}\,\varphi
\end{align*}
where $p \in \mathit{AP}$, ${\bowtie} \in \{\ge, >, \le, <\}$,
$r \in [0,1]$, and $\mathbb{P}_{\bowtie r}$ is the graded
probability quantifier.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Semantics}

Over hypothesis automaton $H = (Q, q_0, \delta, \ell)$ with
$\delta: Q \times \Sigma_\alpha \to \DD(Q)$ and
$\ell: Q \to 2^{\mathit{AP}}$, the satisfaction degree
$\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_H(q) \in [0,1]$ is computed by
fixed-point iteration:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item $\llbracket \mathbb{P}_{\ge r}[\mathbf{F}\,\varphi]
  \rrbracket_H(q)$: probability of eventually reaching a
  $\varphi$-satisfying state, thresholded at $r$.
\item $\llbracket \mathbf{G}\,\varphi \rrbracket_H(q)$: infimum of
  $\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_H$ over reachable states (greatest
  fixed point).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Soundness Proof}\label{app:qctlf-soundness}

\begin{proposition}[Soundness on Finite Hypotheses]
The model-checking algorithm is sound for finite-state
$F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$-coalgebras: if $H$ is a finite hypothesis
automaton, the computed satisfaction degree
$\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_H$ is exact.  Transfer to the
true model $\mathfrak{M}_\alpha$ incurs additional error bounded by
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}/(1-\rho)$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:e2e-error}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$F_{\mathrm{LLM}}(X) = (\Sigma_{\le k} \times \DD(X))^{\Sigma^*_{\le n}}$
decomposes as a composition of the product functor, the exponential
functor, and constant functors.  Each preserves finite-chain
completeness (products and exponentials of $\omega$-continuous functors
are $\omega$-continuous; $\DD$ is $\omega$-continuous on measurable
spaces~\cite{jacobs2016introduction}).  The Pattinson--Schr\"oder
framework~\cite{pattinson2008coalgebraic} guarantees that predicate
liftings are well-defined and monotone, and fixed-point computation
converges on finite-state coalgebras in at most $|Q|$ iterations.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Template Compilation Example}

\textsc{RefusalPersistence} with threshold $\varepsilon = 0.05$ compiles to:
\[
  \forall\mathbf{G}\Bigl(
    \mathit{refused}(q) \Rightarrow
    \mathbb{P}_{\ge 0.95}\bigl[\mathbf{G}\,\mathit{refused}\bigr](q)
  \Bigr).
\]

% ── D: Proof Details ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Proof Details}\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{PCL$^*$ Convergence (Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence})}
\label{app:convergence-proof}

\begin{proof}
The convergence argument proceeds in three steps.

\emph{Step 1: Statistical membership queries.}
For each table entry $T(s, e)$, we draw $m$ i.i.d.\ samples from
$\mathfrak{M}(s \cdot e)$.  Each sample produces an output in
$\Sigma_\alpha$; for each symbol $\sigma \in \Sigma_\alpha$, the
indicator $\mathbf{1}[\text{output} = \sigma]$ is a $[0,1]$-bounded
random variable.  By Hoeffding's inequality applied to each symbol
independently,
\[
  \Pr\!\bigl[|\hat{p}_\sigma - p_\sigma| \ge t\bigr]
  \;\le\; 2 \exp(-2m\,t^2),
\]
where $\hat{p}_\sigma$ is the empirical frequency and $p_\sigma$ the
true probability.  Since $d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\hat{p}, p) = \frac{1}{2}
\sum_\sigma |\hat{p}_\sigma - p_\sigma|$, achieving per-entry TV
accuracy $\tau = \varepsilon/(3 n_0 |E|)$ requires setting
$t = 2\tau / |\Sigma_\alpha|$ and applying a union bound over
$|\Sigma_\alpha|$ symbols, $n_0$ rows, and $|E|$ columns.
The per-entry sample complexity acquires a polynomial factor in
$|\Sigma_\alpha|$: the functor bandwidth reduces the \emph{structural}
table dimension from $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ to $2^\beta$ columns, but
does not eliminate the statistical cost of distributional estimation
within each entry (see Theorem~\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample} proof).

\emph{Step 2: PAC equivalence queries.}
Each equivalence query draws $N_{\mathrm{eq}}$ random prompts and
compares $H$'s predictions with empirical distributions.  By union
bound over test prompts and $n_0$ equivalence queries (each adding
at least one state), the total false-positive rate is $\le \delta/3$.
The number of counterexamples before convergence is bounded by $n_0$
(each adds at least one equivalence class).

\emph{Step 3: Functor bandwidth bound.}
The observation table has at most $n_0$ equivalence classes (rows).
The key structural lemma is: the set of behaviorally distinguishable
suffixes is bounded by the $\varepsilon$-covering number
$N_\varepsilon = 2^\beta$ of the behavioral image.  This holds because
the coalgebra structure constrains the transition distributions to
lie in a space of effective dimension $n_0$: each suffix induces a
``row vector'' in the observation table whose entries are mixtures
of at most $n_0$ base distributions.
Suffixes within an $\varepsilon$-ball of a covering center produce
indistinguishable rows at tolerance $\varepsilon$, so the effective
column count is at most $2^\beta$.  This replaces the naive column
count $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ with $2^\beta$, yielding the complexity in
Theorem~\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample}.
\emph{Note:} the alphabet-independence claim applies to the table's
structural column count, not to per-entry sample complexity
(which retains a polynomial $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ factor).

\emph{Sequential conditioning.}
Steps 1 and 2 are the only probabilistic failure modes.
Conditioning on Step~1 succeeding ($\ge 1 - \delta/3$), the table
is $\varepsilon$-accurate, and Step~2's equivalence query operates
on this fixed table with independent random prompts (failure
$\le \delta/3$):
\[
  \Pr[\text{failure}] = \Pr[\text{S1 fails}] +
  \Pr[\text{S2 fails} \mid \text{S1 ok}] \cdot \Pr[\text{S1 ok}]
  \le \delta/3 + \delta/3 = 2\delta/3 < \delta.
\]
This accounts for causal coupling between sampling and hypothesis
testing: the bound is logically sound under causal dependence.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Bandwidth--Sample Complexity (Theorem~\ref{thm:bandwidth-sample})}

\begin{proof}
The observation table has at most $n_0$ rows and, by the covering
structure, at most $2^\beta$ effective columns (inputs within an
$\varepsilon$-ball of a covering center are indistinguishable at
tolerance $\varepsilon$).  Thus $n_0 \cdot 2^\beta$ effective entries.

By Hoeffding's inequality, per-entry accuracy $\varepsilon$ with
per-entry confidence $\delta / (n_0 \cdot 2^\beta)$ requires
\[
  m_{\mathrm{per}} = \frac{1}{2\varepsilon^2}
  \cdot \log\!\Bigl(\frac{2 \cdot n_0 \cdot 2^\beta}{\delta}\Bigr)
\]
samples per entry.  Total sample complexity:
\begin{align*}
  m_{\mathrm{total}} &= n_0 \cdot 2^\beta \cdot m_{\mathrm{per}}
  = \frac{n_0 \cdot 2^\beta}{2\varepsilon^2}
  \cdot \log\!\Bigl(\frac{2 \cdot n_0 \cdot 2^\beta}{\delta}\Bigr).
\end{align*}
Union bound over all entries ensures total confidence $\ge 1 - \delta$.

\emph{Remark on per-entry estimation.}  The bound above treats each
table entry as a single bounded estimate.  If each entry requires
estimating a full distribution over $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ symbols (via
Hoeffding + union bound per symbol), the per-entry cost acquires a
polynomial factor in $|\Sigma_\alpha|$:
$m_{\mathrm{per}}' = |\Sigma_\alpha|^2 / (8\varepsilon^2) \cdot
\log(2|\Sigma_\alpha| \cdot n_0 \cdot 2^\beta / \delta)$.
The functor bandwidth reduces the \emph{structural} table dimension
from $|\Sigma_\alpha|$ columns to $2^\beta$, but does not eliminate
the \emph{statistical} cost of distributional estimation within each
entry.  The alphabet-independence claim applies to the table's
column count, not to per-entry sample complexity.

In the empirically relevant regime $\beta \le 14.2$ for $n_0 \le 200$
(Table~\ref{tab:complexity}), $2^\beta$ ranges from ${\approx}9$ to
${\approx}18{,}800$---substantially below typical abstracted alphabet
sizes $|\Sigma_\alpha| \approx 500$.  The advantage is that $2^\beta$
depends only on behavioral complexity, not vocabulary size.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Classifier Error Propagation (Equation~\ref{eq:classifier_bound})}
\label{app:classifier-proof}

\begin{proof}
Let $\rho \in [0, 1)$ be the classifier error rate.  Each observed
label is correct with probability $1-\rho$ and incorrect with
probability $\rho$, independently.

The empirical distribution $\tilde{T}(s,e)$ of corrupted labels
satisfies, for each symbol $\sigma$:
$\mathbb{E}[\tilde{p}_\sigma] = (1-\rho) p_\sigma + \rho q_\sigma$,
where $q_\sigma$ is the misclassification distribution.  This
introduces a bias $|\mathbb{E}[\tilde{p}_\sigma] - p_\sigma| \le \rho$
in the worst case ($q_\sigma = 0$ or $1$).

Applying Hoeffding's inequality to all $m$ corrupted samples
(each still $[0,1]$-bounded), the variance term concentrates at rate
$\exp(-2m\,t^2)$ as before.  The total error decomposes as
bias plus concentration:
$|\tilde{p}_\sigma - p_\sigma| \le \rho + t$ with probability
$\ge 1 - 2\exp(-2mt^2)$.

Setting $t = \varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}$ and noting
$d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\tilde{T}, T) \le \rho + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}$,
the total pipeline error satisfies:
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{total}} \le
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} + \rho + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}}$.

We use the looser but compatible bound
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} / (1 - \rho) + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}} + \rho$
in Equation~\ref{eq:classifier_bound}, which is valid since
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} / (1-\rho) \ge \varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}$
for all $\rho \in [0,1)$.  The tighter bound
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} + \rho$ holds with the original budget $m_0$.
If the budget is increased to $m(\rho) = m_0/(1-\rho)$, the
effective sample size compensates for the fraction $\rho$ of
corrupted samples, restoring the original concentration rate.
\end{proof}

\subsection{CoalCEGAR Monotonicity (Proposition~\ref{prop:monotonicity})}

\begin{proof}
Let $\alpha \preceq \alpha'$.  We embed both abstractions in a common
space via the abstraction map $\alpha^\sharp: \Sigma_{\le k'} \to
\Sigma_{\le k}$ induced by the output coarsening.  The Galois
connection is assumed (a concrete construction for $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$
with compatible output alphabets is deferred to future work;
in practice, the abstraction map is an explicit projection).
Under this embedding, for
any safety property $\varphi$ (greatest fixed point):
\[
  \llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_{\alpha}
  \le \llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_{\alpha'}
  + \delta(\alpha, \alpha'),
\]
where $\delta(\alpha, \alpha') = \max_s d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\alpha^\sharp
\circ \gamma_{\alpha'}(s),\, \gamma_{\alpha}(s))$
is the distortion measured in the coarser space $F_\alpha(X)$.
For safety properties, abstraction
under-approximates satisfaction, so refinement can only increase it.

For liveness properties (least fixed points), abstraction can
over-approximate reachability.  Monotonicity requires
$\delta(\alpha, \alpha') < \varepsilon_\varphi$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{End-to-End Error Composition (Theorem~\ref{thm:e2e-error})}
\label{app:error-composition}

\begin{proof}
We analyze five error sources as sequential composition.
Each TV-distance error $\varepsilon_i$ bounds the probability
of flipping the verdict at stage~$i$, by the data-processing
inequality: $d_{\mathrm{TV}}(P, Q) \le \varepsilon$ implies
$|\Pr_P[A] - \Pr_Q[A]| \le \varepsilon$ for any event~$A$.
Union bound over stages yields the additive composition.

\emph{Stage 1: Alphabet abstraction.}
Embedding clustering maps continuous outputs to finite alphabet
$\Sigma_\alpha$.  Error:
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}} = d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\mu, \hat{\mu})$,
bounded by maximum cluster diameter.

\emph{Stage 2: Classifier error.}
Each output assigned to a cluster with error probability $\rho$,
shifting estimated distributions by at most $\rho$ in total variation.

\emph{Stage 3: Learning error.}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence}, conditioning on Stages 1--2,
$d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\mathfrak{M}_\alpha, H) \le
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} / (1 - \rho)$ with probability
$\ge 1 - \delta/3$.

\emph{Stage 4: CEGAR refinement.}
Galois distortion $\delta(\alpha_0, \alpha_r) = \max_s
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\gamma_{\alpha_0}(s), \gamma_{\alpha_r}(s))$.
At convergence, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}} = 0$.

\emph{Stage 5: Model checking.}
Deterministic on the finite automaton.  Numerical error
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}} \approx 10^{-6}$.

\emph{Composition via sequential conditioning:}
\begin{align*}
\Pr[\text{incorrect}] &\le
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}} +
  \left(\frac{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}}{1-\rho} + \rho + \frac{\delta}{3}\right) +
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}} +
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}} + \frac{2\delta}{3} \\
  &\le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}} +
  \frac{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}}}{1-\rho} + \rho +
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}} +
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}} + \delta.
\end{align*}

\emph{Numerical example.}  With $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{abs}} = 0.02$,
$\rho = 0.05$, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{learn}} = 0.05$,
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ref}} = 0$, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mc}} = 10^{-6}$,
$\delta = 0.05$:
\[
  \Pr[\text{incorrect}] \le 0.02 + \frac{0.05}{0.95} + 0.05 + 0 + 10^{-6} + 0.05
  \approx 0.173.
\]
\end{proof}

$|d_K^{\mathrm{est}} - d_K^{\mathrm{true}}| / d_K^{\mathrm{true}}$.

% ── F: QCTL_F Decidability ──────────────────────────────────────────
\section{\qctlf{} Decidability Analysis}\label{app:decidability}

\paragraph{Alternation-free fragment.}
Model checking reduces to a single fixed-point computation.
Complexity: $O(|Q|^2 \cdot |\Sigma_\alpha| \cdot |\varphi|)$,
polynomial.  All six templates fall within this fragment.

\paragraph{Alternation hierarchy.}
The Bradfield--Stirling theorem~\cite{bradfield1999fixpoint}
establishes that the modal $\mu$-calculus alternation hierarchy
is strict in general.  We do not claim a collapse result for
\qctlf{} over $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$-coalgebras.  All six
templates are alternation-free, so this question does not
affect our practical results.

\paragraph{Full logic decidability.}
The full \qctlf{} is decidable via reduction to the first-order
theory of the reals (Tarski--Seidenberg), but doubly-exponential
complexity makes this infeasible in practice.

% ── H: Galois Connection Details ────────────────────────────────────
\section{Galois Connection Details}\label{app:galois}

The abstraction lattice $\Lambda$ is equipped with a Galois connection
between adjacent levels.  For $\alpha \preceq \alpha'$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Abstraction}: $\alpha^{\sharp}: \mathrm{Coalg}(F_{\alpha'}) \to
  \mathrm{Coalg}(F_{\alpha})$ coarsens observation.
\item \emph{Concretization}: $\alpha^{\flat}: \mathrm{Coalg}(F_{\alpha}) \to
  \mathrm{Coalg}(F_{\alpha'})$ embeds the coarse coalgebra.
\end{itemize}
These satisfy: $\alpha^{\sharp}(C') \preceq C$ iff
$C' \preceq \alpha^{\flat}(C)$.

\emph{Caveat:} a concrete construction of these maps for
$F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ with incompatible output alphabets
($\Sigma_\alpha \neq \Sigma_{\alpha'}$) requires a canonical
embedding via the abstraction map $\alpha^\sharp: \Sigma_{\le k'}
\to \Sigma_{\le k}$.  In the implementation, this is an explicit
projection (keyword classification to coarser categories).  A
fully rigorous construction as a Galois connection in the coalgebraic
category is deferred to future work.

Distortion $\delta(\alpha, \alpha') = \max_s
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\alpha^\sharp \circ \gamma_{\alpha'}(s),\,
\gamma_\alpha(s))$ measures information lost, where both terms
are mapped to the common space $F_\alpha(X)$.
For safety properties (greatest fixed points),
abstraction is conservative.  For liveness, the bound
$\delta < \varepsilon_\varphi$ excludes spurious witnesses.

% ── I: Cosine Similarity and Metric Axioms ──────────────────────────
\section{Cosine Similarity and Metric Axioms}\label{app:cosine}

The ground metric for Kantorovich lifting uses \emph{angular distance}:
\[
  d_{\mathrm{ang}}(u, v) = \frac{1}{\pi}\arccos\!\bigl(
    \mathrm{sim}_{\cos}(u, v)\bigr),
\]
which is a proper metric on the unit sphere (satisfying
non-negativity, identity of indiscernibles, symmetry, and triangle
inequality).  Conversion from cosine similarity to angular distance
is performed at the abstraction layer before Kantorovich computation.

% ── K: Behavioral Equivalence Discussion ────────────────────────────
\section{$\varepsilon$-Behavioral Equivalence}\label{app:epsilon-bisim}

\tool{}'s notion of $\varepsilon$-behavioral equivalence (states
$s, t$ with $d_K(s,t) \le \varepsilon$) is closely related to the
$\varepsilon$-approximate bisimulation of Desharnais et
al.~\cite{desharnais2004metrics} and behavioral pseudometrics of
van Breugel and Worrell~\cite{breugel2005approximating}.  The key
difference: $d_K$ is defined via Kantorovich lifting of $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$
rather than the standard probabilistic bisimulation game on labeled
Markov processes.  For $F_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ with discrete output metric,
the notions coincide.  When outputs carry richer structure
(semantic similarity), $d_K$ incorporates the output metric while
classical approximate bisimulation does not.  Triangle inequality
follows from Kantorovich metric properties on
$\DD(X)$~\cite{baldan2018coalgebraic}.
