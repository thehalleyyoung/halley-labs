# Review: CABER — Coalgebraic Behavioral Auditing of Foundation Models

**Reviewer:** Aniruddha Sinha
**Persona:** Model Checking & AI Applicant
**Expertise:** Model checking, CEGAR, temporal logic verification, abstract interpretation, state-space exploration, automata-based verification

---

## Summary

CABER applies coalgebraic automata theory to model-check LLM behaviors against temporal safety specifications using a PCL* learning algorithm, QCTL_F temporal logic, CoalCEGAR abstraction refinement, and Kantorovich bisimulation distances. This is by far the most theoretically ambitious model-checking application to LLMs in the literature—it goes well beyond the superficial formal-methods applications that dominate the LLM safety space. However, the execution reveals fundamental, possibly irreparable gaps between the coalgebraic theory and what is computationally achievable with API-only access to stochastic black boxes: the abstraction is not a coalgebra morphism (0–27% inconsistency), CoalCEGAR monotonicity is violated, the learned models do not generalize, and the safety-only fragment severely limits expressiveness.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretically grounded framework with genuine formal depth.** The coalgebraic formulation—behavioral functor F: Meas → Meas, PCL* learning as an L*-variant for probabilistic coalgebras, QCTL_F model checking via fixed-point computation, and Kantorovich lifting for bisimulation—represents a genuinely principled approach rooted in established automata and coalgebra theory. The instantiation of QCTL_F as a Pattinson-Schröder coalgebraic logic (correctly identified as not a novel logic, but a specific instantiation) shows theoretical awareness.

2. **CoalCEGAR refinement with explicit abstraction lattice.** The abstraction-refinement loop parametrized by (output_bound, input_bound, tolerance) triples, with the lattice ordered by component-wise refinement, is a more principled CEGAR implementation than most claimed applications to ML systems. The explicit Galois connection structure—even though it is only established for the safety fragment via Proposition 1—demonstrates understanding of the preservation requirements for abstract model checking.

3. **Temporal property specifications with practical relevance.** The QCTL_F properties—refusal persistence (AG(refusal → AX refusal)), paraphrase invariance, instruction hierarchy safety—are meaningful behavioral safety specifications. These go well beyond binary classification into genuinely temporal behavioral reasoning, which is the correct level of abstraction for auditing sequential LLM interactions.

4. **Property-based testing validates mathematical invariants.** The 38 property-based tests checking coalgebraic invariants—functor naturality, bisimulation symmetry/transitivity/triangle inequality, model-checker fixed-point convergence, CoalCEGAR lattice ordering—provide computational evidence for theoretical claims. While not proofs, these are the right invariants to test and serve as effective regression tests against implementation errors.

5. **Explicit error composition framework.** Theorem 3's identification of five error sources (learning, abstraction, model-checking, satisfaction estimation, distance computation) with additive composition provides a structured error analysis framework. Even though the individual bounds may be loose, the framework correctly identifies the error propagation structure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Non-functorial abstraction invalidates the theoretical foundation.** The 0–27% inconsistency rate between abstract and concrete models means the abstraction map α: C → A is not a coalgebra morphism—i.e., α does not commute with the behavioral functor F. This is not an engineering imperfection; it breaks the fundamental theorem of abstract model checking: if α is not a morphism, then satisfaction of QCTL_F formulas on the abstract model A does not imply satisfaction on the concrete model C. Proposition 1's CoalCEGAR monotonicity guarantee is predicated on the Galois connection structure, which requires the morphism property. With 27% inconsistency on some configurations, the abstract model checking results have no formal connection to the concrete system. The authors should either (a) prove a quantitative preservation theorem under bounded inconsistency (e.g., "if inconsistency ≤ ε, then |sat_A(φ) - sat_C(φ)| ≤ f(ε)") or (b) acknowledge that the abstract model checking results are heuristic, not formal.

2. **CoalCEGAR monotonicity violation is not just noise—it signals a structural problem.** For 1/5 configurations, refinement does not improve model quality monotonically. The paper attributes this to estimation noise in the stochastic setting, but standard CEGAR theory handles stochastic abstractions via probabilistic CEGAR (e.g., Hermanns et al., Chadha-Viswanathan-Mahesh). If the abstraction lattice does not satisfy the ordering requirements under the estimation noise, the refinement loop has no convergence guarantee. The corrected additive bound O(k_max + n_max + L) for CoalCEGAR assumes monotonicity; with the violation, this bound does not apply. The authors should characterize the conditions under which monotonicity holds and provide convergence guarantees conditional on those conditions.

3. **Safety fragment only is a severe expressiveness limitation.** Restricting to safety properties means important behavioral specifications cannot be verified: "the model eventually produces a helpful response" (liveness), "the model treats demographic groups fairly over extended interactions" (fairness as a liveness/recurrence property), "every refusal is eventually followed by an explanation" (response). The QCTL_F fragment as presented is strictly less expressive than CTL*, which already has decidable model-checking algorithms for finite-state systems. Given that the learned automata have ≤40 states, full CTL* model checking is computationally feasible—the restriction to safety appears to be a theoretical convenience, not a computational necessity.

4. **No mechanized proofs for core theorems undermines the formal methods positioning.** The paper proves Theorem 1 (PCL* convergence), Theorem 2 (Bandwidth-Sample Complexity), Theorem 3 (error composition), and Proposition 1 (CoalCEGAR monotonicity) on paper only. For a paper positioned within the formal methods community, the absence of mechanization in Lean 4, Coq, or Isabelle is a significant gap. The corrected query complexity (O(β·n₀) → O(2^β·n₀)) and the corrected CoalCEGAR bound suggest that even paper proofs had errors—mechanization would have caught these. The 38 property-based tests are valuable as implementation tests but are fundamentally different from proofs: they test specific instances, not universal properties.

5. **Learned automata do not generalize beyond queried prompts.** Leave-one-prompt-out cross-validation yields 0.55–0.69 accuracy—barely above chance for a multi-class classification problem. This means the behavioral automaton learned from 15 prompts has essentially no predictive power on a 16th unseen prompt. The "model" in model checking is not a model of the LLM's behavioral space—it is a memorized lookup table for the specific prompts queried. This fundamentally limits the model-checking guarantee: verified properties hold only on the observed behavioral fragments, not the full (infinite) behavioral space. Theorem 1's convergence guarantee is for the full behavioral coalgebra, but the empirical model covers a negligible fraction of the input space.

6. **PDFA baseline comparison is deliberately weakened.** Using simplified ALERGIA instead of full AALpy + PRISM means the probabilistic automata baseline is not a fair comparison. A properly tuned PDFA baseline using AALpy's state-of-the-art ALERGIA implementation with PRISM for probabilistic model checking could achieve substantially better performance. The reported 76–80% accuracy for the simplified baseline suggests that a full baseline might approach CABER's 92% prediction accuracy, potentially eliminating the marginal contribution of the coalgebraic machinery.

## Questions for Authors

- What formal guarantee survives when the abstraction morphism has 27% inconsistency? Specifically, can you prove a bound of the form |sat_A(φ) - sat_C(φ)| ≤ g(0.27) for QCTL_F formulas φ, and if so, is g(0.27) small enough to be useful?
- Can you prove CoalCEGAR convergence under the stochastic estimation noise that causes the monotonicity violation, or characterize the noise conditions under which convergence is guaranteed?
- Given that the learned automata have ≤40 states, what prevents extending from the safety fragment to full CTL* model checking? Is the restriction theoretical (the coalgebraic framework only supports safety) or computational (liveness model checking is infeasible for some other reason)?

## Overall Assessment

CABER is unambiguously the most theoretically ambitious and technically deep application of model checking to LLM behavioral analysis. The coalgebraic framework, PCL* learning algorithm, QCTL_F logic, and CoalCEGAR refinement loop represent genuine formal methods contributions applied to a genuinely important problem. However, the gap between the theory and its execution is not merely large—it is structurally problematic. Non-functorial abstractions do not merely weaken the guarantees; they invalidate the preservation theorems that justify abstract model checking. Non-monotonic refinement does not merely slow convergence; it removes the convergence guarantee. Non-generalizing learned models do not merely limit coverage; they reduce model checking to property testing on observed inputs. These are not incremental limitations to be fixed by more engineering—they reflect fundamental challenges in applying deterministic formal methods frameworks to stochastic, high-dimensional, black-box systems. The work is valuable as a research direction precisely because it identifies these challenges clearly, but it should not be presented as providing formal behavioral guarantees. It provides approximate behavioral testing with formal-methods-inspired structure—which is valuable, but different from what the coalgebraic theory promises.
