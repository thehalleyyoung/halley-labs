# Review: Witness-Certified Coalgebraic Compression for Finite-Domain TLA+ Model Checking

**Reviewer:** Yuan Cheng (Probabilistic Modeling & Stochastic Processes)
**Score: 6/10**

---

The project's core contribution—a coalgebraic endofunctor F(X) = P(AP) × (P(X))^Act × Fair(X) incorporating stuttering closure and TLA+-style fairness—is mathematically elegant, but the probabilistic and stochastic aspects of the proposal raise concerns from my perspective. The equivalence oracle is implemented as a bounded conformance tester exploring to depth k, which the authors correctly acknowledge provides only a "practical guarantee" rather than a formal one. From a stochastic modeling standpoint, the absence of any probabilistic analysis of this testing procedure is a missed opportunity: what is the probability that a distinguishing sequence of length > k exists but is not discovered? Without even a heuristic probabilistic bound—analogous to randomized property testing guarantees—the conformance tester is essentially a deterministic depth-bounded search with no coverage quantification. The differential testing against TLC provides a safety net but only up to TLC's own tractability bound, creating a circular dependency for the largest benchmarks where TLC times out. The L* convergence bound of O(n² · k) membership queries is clean, but the feasibility analysis reveals the stochastic nature of the problem: for Paxos-3 with estimated quotient size 10³–10⁴, the query count reaches 10¹⁰, and the 1 μs/query estimate assumes perfect caching. In practice, cache miss rates for large state spaces follow heavy-tailed distributions, and the actual runtime variance could be substantial. The proposal would benefit from a probabilistic runtime model—perhaps a Markov chain model of the learner's observation table growth—to provide confidence intervals rather than point estimates.

The Merkle-hashed bisimulation witness scheme is well-designed for integrity verification but lacks any probabilistic soundness analysis. The O(log n) membership proof and spot-checking capability are standard Merkle tree properties, but the proposal does not address the probability of hash collisions in the SHA-256 chain or the statistical guarantees of spot-checking: if an auditor verifies k randomly sampled equivalence classes out of n total, the probability of detecting a single corrupted class is k/n, which could be unacceptably low for large quotients without guidance on sampling strategy. The compression ratio metric (|quotient|/|original|) is reported without any statistical analysis across the benchmark suite—the "expected range 3–20×" is acknowledged as borrowed from symmetry reduction literature, but different protocol families may exhibit different compression distributions. A Bayesian analysis of compression ratios conditioned on protocol structure (number of participants, symmetry degree, fairness constraint count) would significantly strengthen the empirical claims. The T-Fair coherence condition—that stuttering-equivalent paths must agree on acceptance pair satisfaction—is the most novel mathematical contribution, but its interaction with the bounded conformance tester introduces subtle probabilistic concerns: if the tester fails to discover a counterexample distinguishing two states' fairness behavior, the quotient may incorrectly merge states with different liveness properties. The proposal acknowledges this risk implicitly but does not quantify it.

The evaluation plan is thorough in its metrics (compression ratio, wall-clock time, witness size, verification time) but would benefit from statistical rigor. Running benchmarks at fixed participant counts (3, 5, 7 for Two-Phase Commit) provides only point samples from a scaling curve. Fitting a parametric model—exponential, polynomial, or a mixture—to the compression ratio as a function of participant count would enable interpolation and extrapolation predictions. The differential testing correctness validation is the strongest empirical safeguard, but the proposal should specify the statistical power of this test: for a property verified on the quotient, how many independent TLC runs on the original are needed to achieve a given confidence level in the agreement? Overall, the coalgebraic foundations are solid and the engineering plan is credible, but the proposal systematically underinvests in probabilistic analysis of its own heuristic components, which weakens the "certified" claim in the title.
