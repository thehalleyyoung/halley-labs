# Review: Witness-Certified Coalgebraic Compression for Finite-Domain TLA+ Model Checking

**Reviewer:** Aniruddha Sinha (Model Checking & State-Space Exploration)
**Score: 8/10**

---

This is a strong contribution to the model checking community that directly addresses the state explosion problem—the central challenge of our field—through a principled coalgebraic bisimulation quotient construction. The TLA-lite fragment definition is carefully scoped: excluding CHOOSE, recursive operators, and unbounded quantification ensures finite-domain state spaces while retaining expressiveness for all standard distributed protocol benchmarks (Two-Phase Commit, Paxos, Raft, PBFT, Peterson's). From a state-space exploration perspective, the on-the-fly quotient construction interleaving exploration and learning is the most practically valuable contribution. Unlike post-hoc symmetry reduction or partial-order reduction—which require the full state space to be enumerated before compression—the L*-style learner constructs the quotient incrementally, meaning the exploration never materializes states that are bisimulation-equivalent to already-explored states. This is a qualitative improvement over TLC's exhaustive enumeration and potentially even over Apalache's symbolic approach, which encodes the full transition relation in SMT regardless of symmetry. The convergence bound of O(n² · k) where n is quotient size rather than original state space size is crucial: it means the learner's complexity is governed by the compressed representation, not the original.

The key weakness from a model checking perspective is the equivalence oracle. The bounded conformance tester exploring to depth k provides no formal guarantee—the proposal correctly acknowledges this—but in the model checking tradition, we expect either a formal completeness argument or a clear statement of the fragment for which completeness holds. For safety properties (universal CTL*\X), the bisimulation quotient is correct by construction once the coalgebraic morphism is verified, so the equivalence oracle's incompleteness only affects convergence speed, not soundness. But for liveness under fairness—the paper's core novel contribution—the T-Fair coherence condition requires that stuttering-equivalent paths agree on acceptance pair satisfaction. If the bounded tester fails to distinguish two states whose fairness behaviors differ only on paths longer than k, the quotient may be unsound for liveness. The proposal should either prove that the conformance testing depth k needed to distinguish fairness behaviors is bounded by a function of the specification structure (e.g., the number of fairness constraints times the diameter of the quotient), or explicitly downgrade the liveness claim from "preservation" to "preservation modulo bounded testing depth." The Merkle-hashed witness scheme partially mitigates this concern—a third party can verify the bisimulation relation's closure conditions—but the witness only certifies that the *computed* relation is a bisimulation, not that the quotient is *minimal* or that the conformance tester has converged.

The benchmark selection is appropriate but conservative. Two-Phase Commit with 3–7 participants and Paxos with 3 nodes are standard but small by modern model checking standards—TLC handles these in minutes to hours. The proposal should include at least one benchmark where TLC genuinely struggles (e.g., Raft with 5 nodes, or a Byzantine protocol with 4+ nodes) to demonstrate that the compression provides meaningful speedup in practice, not just on toy instances. The subsystem breakdown (~55K LoC, ~24K novel) is realistic for the scope described. The evaluation plan's differential testing against TLC is the right correctness validation methodology, and the structured JSON output with comparison tables demonstrates engineering maturity. The two-paper strategy (CALCO/FoSSaCS for theory, CAV/TACAS for systems) is well-conceived and realistic.
