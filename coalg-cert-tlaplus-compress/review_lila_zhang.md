# Review: Witness-Certified Coalgebraic Compression for Finite-Domain TLA+ Model Checking

**Reviewer:** Lila Zhang (Symbolic AI & Automated Theorem Proving)
**Score: 7/10**

---

The coalgebraic framework for TLA+ bisimulation is a compelling application of categorical methods to a practical verification problem, and the symbolic reasoning aspects deserve careful scrutiny. The functor F(X) = P(AP) × (P(X))^Act × Fair(X) is well-chosen: the powerset component P(X)^Act handles nondeterministic successors cleanly, avoiding the explicit enumeration that plagues TLC. However, from a symbolic reasoning perspective, the proposal misses an opportunity to leverage SMT solving more deeply. The TLA-lite semantic engine evaluates specifications by constructing the concrete fair transition system—essentially an explicit-state approach at the semantic level—before applying the coalgebraic learner. An alternative architecture would encode the TLA-lite semantics symbolically and use SMT queries to answer the L* learner's membership and equivalence questions. This would avoid materializing the concrete transition system entirely, potentially enabling the learner to operate on specifications whose full state spaces exceed memory. The proposal mentions Apalache's SMT-based approach only as a baseline, not as a potential integration target. A hybrid approach—SMT-backed membership oracle for the L* learner, with the coalgebraic quotient construction providing the compression that Apalache's monolithic SMT encoding lacks—could be significantly more powerful than either approach alone.

The Myhill-Nerode theorem for bounded TLA-lite (Contribution 3) is the result most directly relevant to symbolic reasoning, establishing that the minimal F-coalgebra quotient exists, is unique, and has bounded size for finite-domain specifications. This is analogous to the classical Myhill-Nerode theorem's role in regular language learning, and the proof strategy should be straightforward given the local finiteness of the F-coalgebra for bounded domains. However, the proposal does not discuss the effective computability of the distinguishing sequences—while the minimal quotient exists, the L* learner needs to *find* distinguishing sequences, and the bounded conformance tester provides only a heuristic for this. In SAT/SMT solving, bounded model checking provides similar depth-limited guarantees, but with the crucial difference that the SAT solver explores the full Boolean structure at each depth rather than testing individual sequences. A symbolic equivalence oracle—encoding "do states s₁ and s₂ have identical F-behaviors up to depth k?" as an SMT query—would be more thorough than the proposed conformance tester and would leverage the finite-domain structure of TLA-lite that makes such queries decidable. The proposal's proof generation capability is limited to the Merkle-hashed bisimulation witness, which certifies the relation but does not produce a human-readable or machine-checkable proof of why each equivalence class is correct. A richer proof artifact—perhaps in a format checkable by Isabelle/HOL or Lean—would significantly strengthen the "certified" claim and align with the formal verification community's standards for trusted results.

The TLA-lite parser and AST design (~5K LoC) is appropriately scoped, but the exclusion of CHOOSE and recursive operators deserves more justification from a symbolic reasoning perspective. CHOOSE is TLA+'s Hilbert choice operator, and its exclusion is mathematically justified (it introduces non-constructive elements), but many practical TLA+ specifications use CHOOSE for selecting arbitrary elements from finite sets—a pattern that could be handled by enumerating the finite choices and encoding them as nondeterministic branches. Similarly, bounded recursive operators (with a fixed recursion depth) could be unfolded. These extensions would broaden TLA-lite's applicability without compromising the finite-domain guarantee. The knowledge representation aspect—how the observation table is structured and queried—is underspecified. The L* observation table for the stuttering-fair functor must encode not just state equivalence but fairness acceptance pair satisfaction, and the table's size (O(|quotient|² · |alphabet|)) suggests that the alphabet (action set) is a significant scaling factor that should be analyzed per benchmark.
