# Review: Formally Certified Manipulation Evidence via Causal-Bayesian Inference and Adversarial Evaluation

**Reviewer:** Sara Roy (Machine Learning & Formal Verification)
**Score: 7/10**

---

VMEE's ML components are deliberately constrained—small MLP policies (<200K parameters) for adversarial RL, no neural components in the core detection pipeline—which is the right architectural choice for a system whose value proposition is formal verifiability. Neural network verification remains intractable for networks of practical size (VNN-COMP benchmarks show that verifying even simple properties of networks with >10K neurons is challenging), so avoiding neural components in the evidence-generating path is a sound engineering decision. The adversarial RL stress-testing layer is well-positioned as a supporting component rather than a core guarantee: it discovers evasion strategies within the explored strategy space but does not claim exhaustive coverage. The PPO training with domain-specific reward shaping on CPU is feasible for the small policy networks described, and the MDP formulation—where the agent's state includes the detector's Bayesian belief—creates a rich exploration problem that tests the detection pipeline's robustness to adaptive adversaries.

The ML-verification integration, however, has a subtle gap at the causal discovery layer. Theorem M7.4 (DAG robustness under adversarial shift) provides quantitative bounds on posterior degradation when the assumed DAG G differs from the true DAG G* by SHD ≤ k edges. The total variation bound d_TV(π(τ|D,G), π(τ|D,G*)) ≤ f(k, n, κ) is the right formalization, and extending Uhler et al. (2013) and Peters & Bühlmann (2015) to the posterior-over-effects setting is a genuine contribution. But the tightness target—non-vacuous (< 0.5) for k ≤ 3, n ≥ 10³, κ ≤ 10²—is optimistic. In practice, causal discovery on financial time series data with regime shifts (addressed by windowed discovery with structural change detection) may produce DAGs that differ from the true structure by substantially more than 3 edges, especially during volatility regime transitions when the conditional independence structure itself changes. The system should provide degradation curves: how does the TV bound grow as a function of k beyond 3? If the bound becomes vacuous at k = 5 (plausible for regime-shift-induced misspecification), then the formal guarantees may not hold precisely when they are most needed—during market stress events. This is a fundamental tension between the system's formal guarantees and its practical operating environment that deserves more explicit treatment.

The sim-to-real calibration methodology (Supporting Problem #7) is critical for the ML components' credibility but is underspecified. Calibrating against published empirical LOB statistics (Cont et al. 2014, Huang & Polak 2011) using KS-statistics on marginal distributions is a reasonable starting point, but marginal calibration does not guarantee joint distributional fidelity. Two simulators can match all marginal statistics while having very different dependence structures—and it is precisely the dependence structure that determines whether the causal discovery engine recovers the correct DAG. The calibration should include multivariate statistics: copula structure, tail dependence coefficients, cross-correlation decay profiles. Without joint distributional calibration, the RL adversary may discover evasion strategies that exploit artifacts of the simulator's dependence structure rather than genuine detection blind spots. The 24-hour CPU training budget for RL is honestly scoped, but the number of effective independent episodes depends heavily on the simulator's mixing time—if the LOB simulator has long-range temporal correlations (as real markets do), the effective sample size may be much smaller than the raw episode count of 10⁶+, reducing the RL agent's strategy diversity.
