# Review: Formally Certified Manipulation Evidence via Causal-Bayesian Inference and Adversarial Evaluation

**Reviewer:** Yuan Cheng (Probabilistic Modeling & Stochastic Processes)
**Score: 8/10**

---

VMEE's probabilistic architecture makes a compelling case for exact Bayesian inference in an evidentiary context where approximation error is genuinely unacceptable. The decision to compile Bayesian networks into arithmetic circuits for exact marginal and MAP queries—rather than relying on MCMC or variational approximations—is well-justified for a system whose outputs must withstand formal scrutiny. Arithmetic circuit compilation exploits the bounded treewidth of LOB microstructure models (estimated tw = 8–14 for 50–200 variable models) to produce circuits with 10⁶–10⁷ edges, enabling exact posterior computation in polynomial time for fixed treewidth. The treewidth justification is thoughtful: LOB features (bid-ask spread, queue imbalance, order arrival rate, cancellation rate) exhibit sparse conditional independence structure because each feature depends on a bounded neighborhood of temporally and structurally adjacent features. The graceful degradation policy—falling back to bounded-cutset conditioning when treewidth exceeds 15—is a principled compromise that preserves the probabilistic-to-logical proof bridge for the conditioned submodel.

The generative intent model with manipulation-phase HMM (New Mathematics #6) is probabilistically well-motivated: latent states corresponding to manipulation phases (setup → execution → withdrawal → profit-taking) with order-flow observables as emissions is a natural factorization of the manipulation process. Compiling the HMM's temporal unrolling into arithmetic circuits enables exact inference over manipulation phase, avoiding the convergence issues that plague MCMC on multimodal posteriors (manipulation vs. non-manipulation hypotheses create well-separated modes). However, the HMM's state-space design raises questions about probabilistic calibration. The four-phase model assumes a fixed manipulation lifecycle, but real manipulation strategies vary in temporal structure: some spoofing sequences complete in seconds with two phases (place-cancel), while layering may involve extended multi-day buildup. The HMM should accommodate variable-length phases and variable phase ordering, which requires either a hierarchical HMM (adding complexity and potentially exceeding the treewidth budget) or a more flexible generative model like a semi-Markov process with explicit phase duration distributions.

My most significant concern is with the Bayes factor computation and its interpretation as evidence strength. The system computes exact Bayes factors comparing manipulation vs. non-manipulation hypotheses, which is probabilistically correct given the model. But Bayes factors are notoriously sensitive to prior specification on the alternative hypothesis: the Jeffreys-Lindley paradox shows that Bayes factors can diverge arbitrarily depending on the prior's spread over the parameter space, even with infinite data. For the manipulation intent model, the prior over manipulation parameters (aggressiveness, timing, layer count for spoofing) directly affects the Bayes factor. If the prior is too diffuse, the Bayes factor will systematically underweight the manipulation hypothesis (because the prior-predictive under the alternative integrates over many unlikely parameter configurations). If the prior is too concentrated, it may miss atypical manipulation strategies. The problem statement does not specify how manipulation priors are calibrated or what sensitivity analysis is performed. For evidence artifacts intended to support regulatory action, this prior sensitivity is a critical vulnerability—an adversarial defense attorney could argue that the Bayes factor reflects prior choice, not evidence strength. I would recommend reporting Bayes factors under multiple prior specifications (reference priors, empirical Bayes, adversarially-chosen priors) and providing a minimum Bayes factor over a class of priors as a robust evidence measure.
