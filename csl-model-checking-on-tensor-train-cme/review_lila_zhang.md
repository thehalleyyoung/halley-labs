# Review: Certified CSL Model Checking via Tensor-Train Decomposition of Chemical Master Equation States

**Reviewer:** Lila Zhang (Symbolic AI & Automated Theorem Proving)
**Score: 6/10**

---

From a symbolic reasoning perspective, TN-Check represents a primarily numerical approach to model checking, and the symbolic components—CSL parsing, satisfaction-set algebra, proposition encoding—are relatively straightforward. The most interesting symbolic aspect is the encoding of atomic propositions as rank-1 TT masks for axis-aligned predicates (e.g., "species A has copy number > 10"). This encoding is elegant: each atomic predicate constraining a single species' copy number corresponds to a characteristic function that factors across species, yielding a TT representation with bond dimension 1. The satisfaction-set algebra—intersection (Hadamard product of TT masks), union (addition with truncation), and complement (1 − mask)—operates in TT format without materialization. However, for non-axis-aligned predicates (e.g., "n_A + n_B ≤ 10"), the TT rank of the characteristic function grows, potentially significantly. The proposal acknowledges this as a limitation but does not quantify it: for a linear constraint involving k species, the mask's TT rank is at most (M+1)^(k-1), which is exponential in the number of species referenced by the predicate. Since many biologically relevant properties involve multi-species constraints (e.g., conservation laws, stoichiometric invariants), this limitation may be more binding than the proposal suggests.

The symbolic reasoning gap is most apparent in the proof generation and verification story. The error-contraction theorem (Theorem 1), complexity separation theorem (Theorem 3), and MPO bond-dimension preservation theorem (Theorem 2) are stated as mathematical results to be proved, but the proposal does not specify how these proofs will be verified. In the automated theorem proving tradition, claimed theorems should be either formally verified in a proof assistant or accompanied by detailed pen-and-paper proofs subject to peer review. Given the critical role of Theorem 1 in the soundness of the entire verification pipeline—if the error bound is wrong, all certified results are invalid—formal verification in Lean 4 or Coq would be appropriate. The clamping gap (the bound ‖p_clamped − p_SVD‖₁ ≤ f(ε_trunc)) is particularly concerning from a proof-theoretic perspective: the nonlinearity of the clamping operation means standard SVD approximation theorems (Eckart-Young) do not apply directly, and the proof must account for the redistribution of probability mass from negative entries to zero. Uschmajew's (2015) non-negativity-preserving TT rounding via alternating projections introduces a different approximation guarantee that must be carefully integrated into the error-contraction framework.

The SAT/SMT integration opportunities are limited but worth noting. The species ordering problem—finding the 1D chain ordering that minimizes TT bond dimension—is an NP-hard optimization problem (equivalent to optimal variable ordering for BDDs). The proposal uses Reverse Cuthill-McKee, spectral ordering, and METIS-based partitioning as heuristics, which are standard for sparse matrix bandwidth reduction. However, the optimality gap between these heuristics and the true minimum bond dimension can be significant (2–5× for irregular network topologies), and a SAT-based exact solver for small networks (N ≤ 20) would provide useful lower bounds for benchmarking the heuristics. More ambitiously, the CME-to-MPO compilation step—translating reaction networks into matrix product operators—could benefit from symbolic simplification: algebraic identities among reaction propensities (e.g., conservation laws reducing the effective dimensionality) could be detected by a symbolic algebra system and exploited to reduce the MPO bond dimension before numerical computation begins. The proposal's treatment of Hill/Michaelis-Menten kinetics via polynomial interpolation is a pragmatic choice, but symbolic computation of the optimal interpolation polynomial (minimizing TT rank of the resulting MPO) is an interesting subproblem that connects numerical approximation to symbolic optimization.
