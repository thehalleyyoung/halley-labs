# Review: Certified CSL Model Checking via Tensor-Train Decomposition of Chemical Master Equation States

**Reviewer:** Aniruddha Sinha (Model Checking & State-Space Exploration)
**Score: 8/10**

---

This proposal represents a significant advance in probabilistic model checking by introducing tensor-train compression as a fundamentally new state-space representation primitive. From the model checking perspective, the key insight is that TT/MPS compression exploits low-rank correlation structure—a different compression principle than BDDs (which exploit repeated Boolean substructure) or STAMINA's truncation (which exploits probability-mass concentration). The CSL model-checking algorithms operating natively on MPS-compressed probability vectors—satisfaction-set algebra via rank-1 TT masks, projected-rate-matrix construction in MPO form, and fixpoint iteration with TT rounding for unbounded-until—are genuine algorithmic contributions with no direct precedent in either the tensor-network or formal-verification literature. The three-valued semantics for nested probability operators, returning {true, false, indeterminate} with certified indeterminate regions, is a principled approach that builds on the established framework of Katoen, Klink, Leucker, and Wolf (2007). This is the correct design choice: rather than claiming full nested CSL model checking (which would require solving the open problem of tight error composition through arbitrary nesting depth), the proposal delivers sound results with honest uncertainty quantification.

The state-space management strategy is where TN-Check fundamentally differs from existing tools. PRISM and Storm enumerate states (explicitly or via MTBDDs) and store the transition-rate matrix in sparse format; STAMINA truncates the reachable state space but still enumerates within the truncation window. TN-Check never materializes the full state space: the probability vector is stored as an MPS with O(N·χ²·d) parameters, and the transition-rate matrix as an MPO with O(N·d²·D²) parameters, where N is species count, d is local dimension (max copy number), χ is bond dimension, and D is operator bond dimension. For the MAPK cascade (N=22, d=30, χ≈150), this is 200M parameters (1.6 GB) compared to the 10³² explicit states. The scalability claim—extending verified species count from ~25 (STAMINA's frontier) to 50–100+—is bold but plausible given the structural argument: modular reaction networks produce approximately factorizable distributions, and the complexity separation theorem (Theorem 3) provides theoretical backing for the modular subclass. However, I have concerns about the practical scalability for non-modular systems. Bistable genetic switches exhibit bimodal probability distributions with long-range correlations between species copy numbers, which may require high bond dimensions (χ > 200) to represent accurately. The pilot gate's adversarial model selection is crucial for validating this concern.

The verification methodology is sound but the temporal logic coverage deserves scrutiny. CSL's bounded-until operator P≥p[φ U≤t ψ] requires computing the transient probability distribution under a modified rate matrix (absorbing states for ¬φ ∧ ¬ψ), which the proposal handles via projected-rate-matrix construction in MPO form—mathematically correct, and Theorem 2 guarantees bond-dimension preservation for axis-aligned propositions. The unbounded-until fixpoint iteration is the riskiest component: each iteration involves Hadamard product and MPO-MPS contraction that inflate bond dimension, followed by truncation that may destroy the fixpoint signal for slowly-mixing CTMCs. The "bounded-until fallback with large time bound" is a pragmatic safety net, but it weakens the claim of full unbounded-until support. Steady-state computation via DMRG-like ground-state solver is well-motivated by the connection to quantum many-body physics, but the CME generator's non-Hermiticity means convergence guarantees from the Hermitian DMRG literature do not transfer directly. The evaluation plan's comparison against PRISM, Storm, and STAMINA on identical hardware and identical CSL properties is the right methodology, and the failure-mode reporting—classifying failures by network topology—will be invaluable for understanding TN-Check's practical domain.
