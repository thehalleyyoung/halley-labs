# Certified CSL Model Checking via Tensor-Train Decomposition of Chemical Master Equation States

**Slug:** `csl-model-checking-on-tensor-train-cme`

---

## Problem Statement

The Chemical Master Equation’s transition-rate matrix for a reaction network with N species and per-species copy-number bound M decomposes exactly as Q = Σⱼ ⊗ᵢ Aᵢ⁽ʲ⁾ — a sum of Kronecker products where each factor Aᵢ⁽ʲ⁾ is at most pentadiagonal — because each elementary reaction touches only 2–4 species. This tensor structure is identical to a quantum many-body Hamiltonian with local interactions, and indeed is formally analogous to Lindbladian superoperators in open quantum systems, for which tensor-network time evolution has been studied (Jaschke et al., 2018). Kazeev and Schwab (2014, 2015) exploited this to represent CME probability vectors as tensor trains (TT) / matrix product states (MPS) and the generator as a matrix product operator (MPO), demonstrating compressed time evolution on systems with 2–5 species. Dolgov and Khoromskij (2014) extended this to quantized tensor trains; Gelss, Matera, and Schütte (2016) applied tensor decompositions to chemical kinetics more broadly. More recently, Nicholson and Gingrich (Phys. Rev. X, 2023) and Zima, Nicholson, and Gingrich (J. Chem. Phys., Jan 2025) applied DMRG-based methods to 7-species toggle switches, computing steady-state distributions and parameter sweeps. What none of this work does is *model checking*: evaluating temporal-logic properties — bounded-until, unbounded-until, steady-state queries — directly on the compressed TT representation, with sound three-valued semantics for nested probability operators. The gap is the verification layer.

Probabilistic model checking of continuous-time Markov chains against Continuous Stochastic Logic (CSL) specifications is a solved problem — in theory. PRISM and Storm implement the full CSL semantics on explicitly enumerated or MTBDD-compressed state spaces, practical up to roughly 10⁸ states (~10–15 species). STAMINA (Neupane et al., 2019; Roberts et al., 2022) pushes this frontier further through iterative state-space truncation with probability-mass bounds; its practical limit depends on model structure (not just species count), with demonstrated results on models up to approximately 25 species. Rubicon (Holtzen et al., CAV 2021) bridges probabilistic inference with model checking via exact symbolic representations, but does not target the CTMC/CME setting or tensor-network compression. But even STAMINA enumerates states within its truncation window; it compresses the *boundary*, not the *representation*. Consider the MAPK cascade (BioModels BIOMD0000000009): 22 species, 48 reactions, with the biologically critical property P≥0.99[F≤3600 (active_MAPK > threshold)] — does the cascade activate with near-certainty within one hour? At species bound M=30, the explicit state space is 30²² ≈ 10³² states; PRISM exhausts memory attempting enumeration and STAMINA’s truncation window cannot contract the reachable set below 10¹⁰ states for this diffuse, multi-pathway system. Yet the MAPK cascade is *modular* — the three-layer kinase relay (MAPKKK → MAPKK → MAPK) produces approximately factorizable probability distributions across weakly coupled modules, suggesting TT ranks χ ≤ 100–200 should capture the distribution at ε = 10⁻⁴ tolerance. We propose TN-Check, a system that implements CSL model-checking algorithms natively on tensor-train-compressed state vectors, never materializing the full state space. Single-level CSL properties receive tight certified bounds; nested probability operators are handled via sound three-valued semantics returning {true, false, indeterminate} with certified indeterminate regions. If biological CME distributions are TT-compressible — which we hypothesize based on the modular, locally-coupled structure of biochemical reaction networks and test empirically on 20+ published BioModels — this extends certified probabilistic verification to 50–100+ species, a 2–5× improvement in species count over STAMINA and a qualitative leap in the class of models amenable to formal verification. Even if TT ranks are higher than conjectured, verification at 30–40 species would represent a meaningful advance over the current frontier.

We are precise about what is new and what is borrowed. The CME-to-MPO encoding and TT time evolution are due to Kazeev-Schwab and the DMRG/TDVP lineage (with the CME generator’s Metzler structure — non-negative off-diagonals, zero column sums — enabling adaptation of Lindbladian tensor-network techniques); we implement but do not claim them. The CSL parsing and semantic framework follow Baier and Katoen. Our contributions are: (1) algorithms for CSL model-checking operators — time-bounded until, unbounded-until fixpoint iteration, and satisfaction-set algebra — that execute directly on MPS-compressed probability vectors and MPO-compressed generators, preserving TT structure throughout, with sound three-valued semantics for nested probability queries; (2) an error-contraction analysis that bounds how TT truncation error propagates through the Markov semigroup and composes through CSL operators, yielding certified probability intervals for single-level properties and certified indeterminate regions for nested operators; (3) a complexity separation theorem establishing that TT-based verification is provably more efficient than explicit enumeration for modular CTMCs; and (4) a complete system integrating SBML import, CME compilation, adaptive-rank TT time integration, and CSL evaluation, benchmarked against PRISM, Storm, and STAMINA on models from the BioModels repository. The adaptive rank component uses a greedy-doubling heuristic with per-bond singular-value monitoring; a learned MLP rank controller is deferred to follow-up work unless ablation on pilot models shows >3× improvement over greedy-doubling.

Four points of mathematical honesty. First, the claim that biological CME distributions have low TT rank is an empirical hypothesis, not a theorem. The area-law results for 1D gapped Hermitian Hamiltonians (Hastings, 2007) do not apply: the CME generator is non-Hermitian, biological networks are not 1D, and transient distributions are not ground states. Systems with small spectral gaps (metastable dynamics) may require higher bond dimensions during transient phases, and the practical species-count frontier may be lower for such models. Kazeev and Schwab (2015) proved low TT rank only for zero-deficiency networks — a class so restrictive it excludes toggle switches and oscillators. We conjecture that modular reaction networks produce low-rank distributions because weakly-coupled biochemical modules yield approximately factorizable probability distributions, and we design our evaluation to validate or falsify this conjecture on 20+ published BioModels at 20–100+ species. Second, error certification through nested CSL operators is an open research problem: near a probability threshold, small TT truncation errors can flip the satisfaction status of many states, causing error amplification. Our error-contraction theorem (Section: New Mathematics Required) addresses the single-level case; nested composition uses interval-arithmetic three-valued model checking (Katoen, Klink, Leucker, and Wolf, 2007; Hermanns, Wachter, and Zhang, 2008) where states with probability within ε of the threshold are classified as “indeterminate” rather than true/false. This is sound and publishable — three-valued model checking is an established framework — but the indeterminate region may be large for high-entanglement models. We report this honestly as a limitation, not as full nested CSL model checking. Third, non-negativity of probability vectors under TT truncation is a soundness concern — TT-rounding via SVD can produce negative entries, and post-truncation clamping is a nonlinear operation that breaks the contractivity argument (the clamped vector is not the SVD-optimal TT approximation). We must rigorously bound ‖p_clamped − p_SVD‖₁ ≤ f(ε_trunc) for a known function f, or alternatively explore non-negativity-preserving TT rounding (Uschmajew 2015, alternating projections). This is flagged as a risk if the clamping bound is not tight. Fourth, convergence of fixpoint iteration for the unbounded-until operator in TT format is not guaranteed for general CTMCs. Each iteration inflates rank (Hadamard product + MPO-MPS contraction), truncation deflates it, and for slowly-mixing CTMCs with metastability the fixpoint signal may be destroyed by aggressive truncation. If fixpoint iteration fails to converge within a bounded number of iterations, we report “indeterminate” and fall back to bounded-until with a large time bound as a sound over-approximation.

**Pre-build validation gate (HARD REQUIREMENT).** Before any implementation begins, we conduct a 3-week pilot study using tntorch (Python) on **10+ BioModels** at 20–80 species to validate that TT rank remains manageable at ε = 10⁻⁴. The pilot must include adversarial models — bistable systems (genetic toggle switches, *B. subtilis* competence circuit), oscillators (repressilator, p53-Mdm2), and stiff multi-scale networks — not merely modular cascades where low rank is expected. Specifically, we measure bond dimensions for: the MAPK cascade (BIOMD0000000009), the *B. subtilis* competence circuit, the repressilator, p53-Mdm2 oscillator, NF-κB signaling, EGFR pathway, and 4+ additional signaling/regulatory models spanning diverse network topologies. **Go/no-go decision:**
- If **>60% of models** achieve χ ≤ 200 at ε = 10⁻⁴: proceed with full scope.
- If **40–60%** achieve χ ≤ 200: narrow scope to modular CTMC subclass (cascades, feedforward networks) where the complexity separation theorem (Theorem 3) provides guarantees.
- If **<40%** achieve χ ≤ 200: **terminate the project.** The compressibility hypothesis does not hold broadly enough to justify the engineering investment.

This gate is non-negotiable. No implementation work begins until the pilot completes and the go/no-go decision is recorded.

## Value Proposition

**Who needs this:** Computational biologists building stochastic models of gene-regulatory circuits, signaling cascades, and metabolic pathways with 20–200+ molecular species who currently can simulate (Gillespie SSA) but cannot *verify* — cannot prove that their model satisfies a temporal-logic specification with a certified probability bound. Specific unmet needs: synthetic biologists requiring formal safety guarantees for engineered genetic circuits (therapeutic gene circuits) where regulatory standards demand more than simulation confidence intervals; systems biology researchers studying noise-driven phenomena (bistability in *B. subtilis* competence, p53-Mdm2 oscillations, MAPK cascade stochastic misactivation) who need full probability distributions rather than sample averages; and any modeler who needs rigorous answers about rare events — properties with probability below 10⁻³ — where Gillespie SSA requires prohibitively many trajectories.

**Why now:** The TT-CME numerical infrastructure is mature (Kazeev-Schwab, Dolgov-Khoromskij, Gelss-Matera-Schütte, Gingrich group), but no one has built the verification layer. The window is open: the Gingrich group publishes rapidly on TT-CME numerics (Julia-based codebase, not directly reusable) but lacks formal-methods expertise; the STAMINA team (Myers lab, Utah) has verification expertise but not TT expertise. A 6–12 month window exists for establishing priority on TT-native CSL model checking before either group independently closes the gap.

## New Mathematics Required

Four load-bearing results must be established before the system can claim verification soundness. All other mathematics is adapted from existing work (Kazeev-Schwab for CME-MPO, Dolgov-Savostyanov for AMEn, Ceruti-Lubich-Walach for non-Hermitian DLRA, Baier-Katoen for CSL semantics).

**Theorem 1 (Error Contraction for TT-Compressed Markov Semigroups).** If p_exact(t) = e^{Qt} p₀ is the exact CME solution and p_TT(t) is the TT-compressed approximation with per-step rounding tolerance ε_round, then ‖p_exact(t) − p_TT(t)‖₁ ≤ C(t) · ε_round where C(t) is bounded because the Markov semigroup is a contraction in the ℓ¹ norm (column sums of Q are zero, off-diagonals non-negative). *Proof strategy:* exploit the fact that e^{Qt} is a stochastic matrix (‖e^{Qt}‖₁ = 1) so per-step errors do not amplify multiplicatively — unlike the generic Hermitian case where ‖e^{Ht}‖ can grow. The Metzler structure of Q (specific to CME generators) provides the contractivity that generic non-Hermitian operators lack. **Critical caveat on clamping:** TT-SVD truncation produces negative entries, and post-truncation clamping is a nonlinear operation that breaks the contractivity argument. The clamped vector is not the SVD-optimal TT approximation. The proof must rigorously bound ‖p_clamped − p_SVD‖₁ ≤ f(ε_trunc) for a known function f, AND specify C(t)’s explicit dependence on t and the number of truncation steps. Alternatively, the proof may employ non-negativity-preserving TT rounding (Uschmajew 2015 or alternating projections) to avoid clamping entirely. If the clamping bound is not tight, this is a risk to the soundness claim, and the error certificate must conservatively account for the clamping error. **Status: conjectured with strong structural evidence; the clamping gap must be resolved before the error certificate can be trusted.**

**Theorem 2 (MPO Bond-Dimension Preservation Under Absorbing-State Modification).** Constructing the projected rate matrix Q_{Φ₁∧¬Φ₂} for the time-bounded until operator — zeroing rows/columns for states violating the path formula — preserves MPO bond dimension when the proposition Φ is axis-aligned (i.e., each atomic predicate constrains a single species’ copy number). Specifically, the absorbing-state modification is a Hadamard product with a rank-1 TT mask, yielding an MPO with bond dimension at most D_original. For non-axis-aligned predicates (e.g., n_A + n_B ≤ 10), the mask has TT rank > 1, and bond dimension grows — an acknowledged limitation. We assume MPO bond dimension D compresses from D_raw = M+1 to approximately D ≈ 50 for modular networks with shared species; for networks with minimal species sharing, D may remain O(M), and computational cost scales accordingly.

**Theorem 3 (Complexity Separation for Modular CTMCs).** For a reaction network decomposable into k weakly-coupled modules of m species each (N = km), where inter-module coupling strength is bounded by δ, the TT rank of the CME probability distribution at time t satisfies χ(t) ≤ poly(m) · (1 + δ·t)^{O(k)}, yielding verification complexity polynomial in m and exponential only in effective coupling. In contrast, explicit enumeration is Θ(M^N) and BDD compression provides no guaranteed reduction for stochastic systems. *Proof strategy:* formalize the notion of weakly-coupled modules via a block decomposition of the CME generator Q = Q_local + δ·Q_coupling, where Q_local is block-diagonal in the module partition. At δ = 0 the distribution is exactly separable (TT rank 1 per module boundary); the perturbation δ·Q_coupling introduces entanglement that grows polynomially in coupling strength and time. The rank bound follows from a perturbative expansion of the Markov semigroup e^{Qt} combined with TT rank bounds for polynomial approximations of matrix exponentials (Grasedyck 2004; Khoromskij 2011). The BDD comparison follows from the observation that BDD compression exploits repeated Boolean substructure, which stochastic probability vectors generically lack. This would be the first rigorous complexity separation between TT-based and explicit model checking for structured CTMCs, and the key theoretical contribution justifying the TT approach for biological verification.

**Open Problem (CSL Model Checking with Sound Three-Valued Semantics for Nested Operators).** For nested formulas P≥0.9[F≤10 P≥0.5[G φ]], truncation error in the inner probability computation propagates to the outer satisfaction set. Near the probability threshold, small errors flip the satisfaction status of many states. Tight error composition through arbitrary nesting depth is an open research problem. Our approach: implement interval-arithmetic-based three-valued model checking (Katoen, Klink, Leucker, and Wolf, 2007; Hermanns, Wachter, and Zhang, 2008) where states with probability within ε of the threshold are classified as “indeterminate” rather than true/false. Single-level CSL properties (the common case in biological verification) receive tight certified bounds. Nested operators return {true, false, indeterminate} with certified indeterminate regions. This is sound and publishable — three-valued model checking is an established framework — but the indeterminate region may be large for high-entanglement models or when many states have probabilities near the threshold. We frame this contribution as “CSL model checking with sound three-valued semantics for nested operators,” not as full nested CSL model checking, and report the indeterminate fraction as a quality metric.

## Best Paper Argument

This work merits best-paper consideration at CAV or TACAS for three reasons that are independently strong and mutually reinforcing.

**Algorithmic novelty.** No prior work implements CSL model-checking operators on tensor-network compressed state representations. Symbolic model checking on compressed representations is 30 years old (McMillan’s BDD-based methods, 1993), but BDDs exploit repeated substructure while TT/MPS exploits low-rank correlation structure — a fundamentally different compression principle suited to a different problem class. The specific algorithms — satisfaction-set algebra via rank-1 TT masks, projected-rate-matrix construction in MPO form, fixpoint iteration with TT rounding for unbounded-until, three-valued interval-arithmetic semantics for nested probability evaluation — are genuine contributions to both the tensor-network and formal-verification literatures. Theorem 3 (Complexity Separation for Modular CTMCs) provides the first rigorous theoretical justification for TT-based model checking: for reaction networks with modular structure and bounded inter-module coupling δ, TT-based verification complexity is polynomial in module size m and exponential only in effective coupling, whereas explicit enumeration is Θ(M^N) and BDD compression provides no guaranteed reduction for stochastic systems. This is the kind of complexity separation theorem that elevates a systems paper into a theoretical contribution.

**Scale breakthrough.** If the TT-compressibility hypothesis holds for biological CMEs — and preliminary evidence from Kazeev-Schwab (low rank at N=2–5), Gingrich (low rank at N=7), and the structural argument from network modularity support it — TN-Check extends certified probabilistic model checking from ~25 species (STAMINA’s demonstrated frontier) to 50–100+ species. This is not a 2× speedup; it is access to an entirely new class of models: full signaling pathways (MAPK cascade, 22 species), gene-regulatory modules (competence circuit, ~30 species), and potentially genome-scale subsystems. A concrete illustration: the MAPK cascade (BIOMD0000000009, 22 species, 48 reactions) with property P≥0.99[F≤3600 (active_MAPK > threshold)] has state space 30²² ≈ 10³² at bound M=30. PRISM cannot enumerate this. STAMINA’s truncation window remains above 10¹⁰ reachable states. TN-Check with the three-layer modular structure of the cascade at estimated bond dimension χ ≈ 150, local dimension d = 30, stores the full probability vector in 22 × 150² × 30 ≈ 1.5 × 10⁸ parameters (~1.2 GB). Time integration via TDVP with Fox-Glynn truncation of the Poisson sum for stiff uniformization: estimated 2–4 hours on a laptop at 40 GFLOPS sustained (the M2’s theoretical 100 GFLOPS peak is unrealistic for memory-bound SVD workloads). This is dramatically longer than a quick computation but still a qualitative improvement over “impossible” — going from “cannot verify at all” to “overify overnight on a laptop.”

**Cross-disciplinary impact.** The structural observation — CME generator = sum of local operators, structurally identical to a quantum many-body Hamiltonian — has been known since Kazeev-Schwab but never exploited for verification. For formal-methods researchers, this paper provides a concrete new primitive: they can now model-check systems 5–10× larger in species count by using TT compression instead of BDD/MTBDD compression, applicable whenever the CTMC generator has Kronecker-product structure, with Theorem 3 providing theoretical guarantees for the modular subclass. For tensor-network researchers, this paper introduces fixpoint iteration and satisfaction-set algebra on MPS — operations with no analog in the physics literature — opening biological verification as a new application domain with distinct mathematical challenges (non-Hermiticity, non-negativity constraints, temporal-logic semantics over compressed representations).

## Laptop CPU Feasibility

The computational profile is dominated by dense matrix operations on matrices of size χ × χ (bond dimension, typically 50–200) and SVD decompositions at each of the N–1 bonds. All performance estimates use **40 GFLOPS sustained throughput** on Apple M2 via Accelerate — the theoretical peak of 100 GFLOPS is unrealistic for memory-bound SVD workloads that dominate TT computation.

**Storage.** An MPS with N = 100 sites, local dimension d = 50 (max copy number per species), and bond dimension χ = 200 stores N · χ² · d = 100 × 200² × 50 = 200M floats ≈ 1.6 GB in double precision. The MPO (CME generator) adds O(N · d² · D²) where D is the operator bond dimension; for M = 200 reactions, D_raw = M+1 = 201, compressing via SVD to D ≈ 50 for modular networks, yielding ~100 MB. Total resting data: under 2 GB.

**Working memory.** Peak working memory during TDVP sweeps is approximately 4–5× the resting MPS storage size. At χ = 200: SVD workspace for the 2-site local problem (matrices up to 10,000 × 10,000) requires ~2.4 GB via LAPACK’s dgesdd; two working MPS copies for TDVP require ~3.2 GB; zip-up streaming intermediates add ~800 MB per site. **Total working set at χ = 200 is 8–12 GB**, depending on integrator choice. The honest operating regime: **χ ≤ 100 is comfortable on 16 GB** (working set ~2–3 GB); **χ = 200 needs 32 GB**; χ > 300 requires a 64+ GB workstation. When bond dimension exceeds the memory budget, TN-Check detects the approaching wall and can degrade gracefully: either reduce truncation tolerance (reporting wider certified error intervals) or switch to statistical estimation via trajectory sampling from the TT-compressed distribution, reporting “indeterminate” with the current error bound.

**Computation.** A single 2-site TDVP sweep costs O(N · χ³ · d) per site (the χ³ comes from SVD). For χ = 200, d = 50, N = 100: cost per sweep = 100 × 200³ × 50 = 4 × 10¹⁰ FLOPs. At 40 GFLOPS sustained: **~1000 seconds (~17 minutes) per sweep.** A verification requiring 100 sweeps: ~28 hours. At the boundary of laptop feasibility for large models. For χ = 100 (plausible for modular networks): cost drops 8× to ~125 seconds per sweep, ~3.5 hours for 100 sweeps — practical on a laptop.

**Uniformization cost for bounded-until.** CSL bounded-until requires numerical uniformization with Poisson-weighted matrix powers. For stiff models, the uniformization parameter q·t (where q = max exit rate, t = time bound) can reach 10⁵–10⁶, requiring correspondingly many Poisson terms even with Fox-Glynn truncation. We use TDVP-based time integration for bounded-until where possible; when stiffness demands uniformization, Fox-Glynn truncation (Fox and Glynn, 1988) bounds the number of required Poisson terms to O(q·t + √(q·t·log(1/ε))). For the MAPK cascade with χ ≈ 150: estimated **2–4 hours** for full verification. Still a dramatic improvement over “impossible.”

**Honest operating regimes:**

| Regime | Parameters | Expected Time | Hardware |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Laptop-comfortable** | N ≤ 50, χ ≤ 100, q·t ≤ 10⁴ | Minutes to 1 hour | 16 GB RAM |
| **Laptop-feasible** | N ≤ 100, χ ≤ 200, q·t ≤ 10⁵ | 2–8 hours | 32 GB RAM |

**The critical bottleneck: MPO-MPS contraction.** Applying Q (as MPO with bond dimension D) to p (as MPS with bond dimension χ) produces an intermediate MPS with bond dimension χ · D. For χ = 200, D = 50: intermediate bond dimension = 10,000. Without zip-up: the full intermediate tensor at one site is 40 GB — unacceptable. With zip-up streaming: peak per-site memory is O(χ · D · d · χ) = 200 × 50 × 50 × 200 = 10⁸ entries ≈ 800 MB. Tight but feasible when only one site’s intermediate is resident at a time.

**Honest assessment.** For N ≤ 50 species with χ ≤ 100, laptop verification is comfortable (minutes to one hour). For N = 100 species with χ = 200, verification requires a 32 GB machine and runs for hours. For χ > 200 (which may be required for highly correlated models like bistable switches during transient dynamics) or stiff models with q·t > 10⁵, laptop CPU is impractical. We report all wall-clock and memory numbers honestly, including cases where the laptop constraint is binding and failure modes where the system exceeds its resource budget.
