# Synthesis: Definitive Problem Statement

## Title

**GuardPharma: Compositional Temporal Verification of Polypharmacy Safety Across Interacting Clinical Guidelines with Pharmacokinetic State Semantics**

## Slug

`compositional-guideline-polypharmacy-verify`

## Problem Statement

### §1 — The structural failure nobody checks

Clinical practice guidelines are the backbone of evidence-based medicine. Each guideline — the ADA Standards of Care for diabetes, the ACC/AHA guidelines for hypertension, the KDIGO recommendations for chronic kidney disease — is a carefully curated decision algorithm specifying when to initiate drugs, adjust doses, order labs, and escalate treatment. These guidelines are increasingly published as *computable artifacts*: CQL (Clinical Quality Language) libraries, FHIR PlanDefinition resources, eCQM (electronic Clinical Quality Measures), and CDS Hooks services. The HL7 FHIR Clinical Reasoning specification, CDS Connect, and the CQFramework reference implementation collectively form a growing ecosystem of machine-executable clinical logic. But here is the structural failure: **each guideline is authored, validated, and published in isolation**. The ADA does not check its diabetes guideline against ACC/AHA's hypertension guideline. KDIGO does not verify that its renal dosing constraints are consistent with the CHEST anticoagulation protocol. No tooling exists — anywhere in the HL7 ecosystem, anywhere in the CDS vendor stack, anywhere in regulatory oversight — to ask the question: *"If guidelines G₁, G₂, …, Gₖ activate simultaneously for a patient with comorbidities C₁, C₂, …, Cₘ, do they ever produce contradictory, dangerous, or pharmacokinetically unsafe recommendations?"* The average Medicare beneficiary has 6.8 chronic conditions. Most patients are polypharmacy patients. Most patients are governed by multiple interacting guidelines that nobody has verified for mutual consistency. The result is an estimated 125,000 deaths per year in the US from adverse drug events — many caused by multi-drug regimens that individually comply with best-practice guidelines but collectively violate pharmacological safety constraints that no guideline author considered.

### §2 — Why existing approaches don't solve it

The clinical informatics community is not unaware of the problem. Four lines of prior work exist, and all fall short in specific, identifiable ways. (1) **TMR (Transition-based Medical Recommendation model)** by Zamborlini et al. (Semantic Web Journal, 2017; extended 2024) uses first-order Semantic Web rules to detect interactions between guideline recommendations. TMR is *atemporal* — it reasons over recommendation-level abstractions ("recommend drug X," "avoid drug Y") without modeling *when* recommendations fire, *how long* drugs persist in the body, or *what sequence* of guideline decisions produces the conflict. It handles only pairwise interactions, not the combinatorial space of N concurrent guidelines. (2) **GLARE/META-GLARE** (Terenziani et al., MDPI 2023) executes multiple guidelines concurrently and detects conflicts via constraint propagation along a *single execution path*. It does not perform exhaustive verification — it finds conflicts that happen to occur during a particular simulation, not conflicts that *could* occur across all possible patient trajectories. (3) **UPPAAL-based clinical protocol verification** (various, surveyed in MDPI Sensors 2025) applies timed-automata model checking to *individual* clinical protocols. These are one-off, hand-modeled case studies: no system automatically parses standard CIG representations, automatically composes multiple guidelines, or provides clinical-domain abstractions for scalability. (4) **CAREPATH** (Frontiers in Medicine, 2024) transforms CPGs into FHIR-based computable rules with CDS Hooks for execution, but provides no verification capability whatsoever. The gap is precise: **no existing system combines (i) automated parsing of standard computable guideline formats, (ii) automatic composition of N guidelines into a verifiable product model, (iii) temporal reasoning over real-time and pharmacokinetic constraints, and (iv) sound, decidable verification with counterexample generation.** This is the gap GuardPharma fills.

### §3 — What GuardPharma does

GuardPharma is a formal verification engine for multi-guideline clinical decision support under polypharmacy. It operates in four stages. **Stage 1: Guideline Ingestion.** The system parses computable clinical guidelines from three input formats — CQL libraries (via a full CQL 1.5 parser and type-checker), FHIR R4 PlanDefinition resources (the workflow encoding of guidelines with action steps, conditions, and timing constraints), and eCQM HQMF (Health Quality Measure Format) documents — into a unified intermediate representation: *Pharmacological Timed Automata* (PTA). To address the small-corpus problem (the CDS Connect repository has dozens, not hundreds, of rich CQL guidelines), GuardPharma includes an automated CQL compilation pipeline that ingests the HL7 FHIR CDS library, the CQFramework reference CQL libraries, publicly available eCQM specifications from CMS, and guideline-derived CQL from academic medical centers, targeting a verification corpus of 300+ distinct guideline artifacts. **Stage 2: Pharmacokinetic State Modeling.** Each PTA augments classical timed automata with *pharmacokinetic state variables*: continuous variables whose evolution is governed by linear compartmental ODE systems (one-/two-/three-compartment models with first-order absorption and elimination) parameterized from published population PK (PopPK) models. Drug concentrations, AUC (area under the curve), and steady-state trough levels are first-class elements of the automaton's state. Guideline transitions are guarded by predicates over both discrete clinical state (lab categories, diagnosis codes, risk strata) and continuous PK state (concentration thresholds, toxic-level predicates, drug-drug interaction magnitude). **Stage 3: Compositional Verification.** When N guidelines are active for a multimorbid patient, the system constructs the product PTA and verifies safety properties expressed in a medical-domain dialect of metric temporal logic (MTL). The crown-jewel mathematical contribution is a *compositional verification theorem*: under a pharmacokinetic monotonicity condition (which holds for the Metzler-matrix dynamics of linear compartmental PK models), verification of the N-guideline product reduces to verification of individual guidelines plus pairwise drug-interaction properties, collapsing exponential state-space explosion to quadratic cost. Decidability of PTA reachability and MTL model checking is established via a novel zonotopic reachable-set construction exploiting the Metzler property, with a δ-decidability fallback (in the style of Gao et al.'s dReal) for nonlinear PK dynamics. A CEGAR (counterexample-guided abstraction refinement) loop with clinical-domain abstractions (drug-class equivalence, lab-value discretization at clinical thresholds, temporal coarsening) manages the region explosion. **Stage 4: Clinical Significance Filtering.** Not every formal conflict is clinically dangerous. GuardPharma stratifies discovered conflicts by severity using a multi-signal scoring system: (a) drug interaction severity from DrugBank, (b) Beers Criteria classification for potentially inappropriate medications in older adults, (c) FAERS (FDA Adverse Event Reporting System) disproportionality signal for the conflicting drug combination, and (d) prevalence of the triggering comorbidity profile in Medicare claims data (via publicly available CMS statistics). Conflicts are ranked as critical (immediate patient harm potential), significant (requires clinical judgment), or informational (formally inconsistent but clinically manageable). Only critical and significant conflicts are reported as primary findings.

### §4 — The evaluation is fully automated and devastating

Evaluation requires zero human annotation. **(1) Parsing fidelity:** Round-trip parse-unparse-reparse of all CQL libraries in CDS Connect, CQFramework, and CMS eCQM repositories; measure syntactic and semantic round-trip fidelity. **(2) Known-conflict recall:** Inject known polypharmacy conflicts from the Beers Criteria (2023 update: 30+ explicit drug-disease and drug-drug interactions), the STOPP/START criteria (v3: 80+ explicit contraindication rules), and FDA safety communications into guideline sets; measure detection rate. **(3) Scalability:** Measure solve time vs. number of simultaneously active guidelines, targeting 20+ guidelines verified in <60 seconds on a laptop CPU. **(4) Novel conflict discovery:** Verify all pairwise and selected higher-order combinations of the 20 most prevalent Medicare comorbidities (mapped to their computable guidelines), cross-reference discovered conflicts against DrugBank's interaction database and FAERS adverse event reports, and report the fraction that are (a) confirmed by existing databases (true positives), (b) plausible but undocumented (novel findings), or (c) false positives (formal conflicts with no pharmacological basis). **(5) Compositionality speedup:** Empirically measure the wall-clock improvement from compositional verification (Theorem 3) vs. monolithic product-automaton verification, demonstrating the practical value of the mathematical contribution.

### §5 — What an honest novelty assessment reveals

Let us be brutally honest about what is genuinely novel versus repackaged. **Genuinely novel:** (a) The PTA formalism itself — timed automata with linear compartmental ODE state variables — does not exist in the literature; the closest work (Alur-Dill timed automata, Henzinger hybrid automata, o-minimal automata) does not cover this class. (b) The decidability result for PTA reachability via Metzler-matrix zonotopic stabilization is a new theorem. (c) The compositional verification theorem reducing N-guideline checking to O(N²) pairwise checks under pharmacokinetic monotonicity is a new theorem with direct practical impact. (d) The δ-decidability extension for nonlinear PK via validated ODE integration is a novel application of dReal-style techniques to pharmacokinetics. (e) The automated CQL-to-PTA compilation pipeline, producing verifiable formal models from standard clinical artifacts, has no precedent. **Repackaged but necessary:** (f) CEGAR is a known technique (Clarke et al., 2000); our clinical-domain abstractions are novel but the CEGAR framework is not. (g) SMT solving (Z3) is used as a backend; we contribute the encoding, not the solver. (h) DrugBank/FAERS integration for evaluation is standard pharmacoinformatics engineering. (i) The clinical significance filter uses existing severity databases (Beers, DrugBank severity, FAERS PRR) — we contribute the integration and stratification, not the data. The paper's mathematical contribution is concentrated in the PTA decidability proof and the compositionality theorem. Everything else is systems building, domain modeling, and careful engineering — all necessary, none independently publishable. The mathematical core is what elevates this from "useful tool" to "best paper."

## New Mathematics Required

### Theorem 1: Decidability of PTA Reachability (the foundation)

**Statement.** Let P = (L, l₀, X, C, Φ, E, I) be a Pharmacological Timed Automaton where X evolves according to ẋ = Mx + Bu with M a Metzler matrix (all off-diagonal entries ≥ 0, as holds for linear compartmental PK systems) and Φ ∈ [φ_lo, φ_hi] ⊂ ℝᵖ are patient parameters ranging over bounded intervals. The reachability problem — "does there exist φ ∈ Φ and a dosing schedule u(t) consistent with the guideline such that a designated location l_bad is reached?" — is decidable.

**Proof strategy.** (a) Show that the set of reachable PK states at any discrete transition forms a *zonotope*, because the solution of ẋ = Mx + Bu with interval initial conditions and interval inputs under Metzler dynamics yields a zonotopic reachable set (exploiting the fact that Metzler systems preserve the componentwise partial order on ℝⁿ). (b) Prove that the succession of zonotopes across discrete transitions *stabilizes after finitely many steps* via a novel monotonicity argument: the Metzler property ensures that reachable sets grow monotonically with respect to set inclusion, and boundedness of PK concentrations (physiological upper bounds) forces convergence. (c) Once stabilized, reduce to a finite-state system and apply standard reachability checking.

**Fallback (δ-decidability).** If the full decidability proof requires conditions stronger than Metzler (e.g., for PK models with enterohepatic recirculation), fall back to δ-decidability in the style of Gao et al. (2013, dReal): the verifier answers either "unsafe" (exact) or "δ-safe" (safe up to perturbation δ in state variables), where δ is chosen to be below the minimum pharmacologically meaningful concentration difference. This is a weaker but still clinically actionable guarantee.

**Why this is genuinely new.** Alur-Dill timed automata handle clocks that reset to zero — not ODE-governed continuous variables. Henzinger's hybrid automata are undecidable in general. The only decidable hybrid subclasses (initialized rectangular automata, o-minimal automata) do not cover Metzler linear ODEs with interval parameters. The δ-decidability of general nonlinear hybrid systems (dReal) has been established, but our *exact* decidability for the Metzler linear case is new and tighter.

### Theorem 2: MTL Model Checking for PTA (temporal properties)

**Statement.** Given a PTA P and an MTL formula φ expressing a temporal safety property (e.g., "the predicted steady-state concentration of drug X is always below the toxic threshold while the patient is concurrently on drug Y within the first 90 days"), decide whether all runs of P satisfy φ. The problem is PSPACE-complete for bounded-horizon MTL.

**Proof strategy.** Construct a novel *pharmacokinetic region graph* that partitions the continuous PK state space into finitely many equivalence classes — cells defined by clinical threshold crossings rather than clock-value equalities. The region count is bounded by O(∏ᵢ (Kᵢ/δᵢ)) where Kᵢ is the maximum clinically relevant concentration for drug i and δᵢ is the minimum pharmacologically meaningful difference. For typical parameters (5 drugs, K/δ ~ 10³), this yields ~10¹⁵ regions — large but finite, and aggressively prunable by CEGAR. PSPACE-completeness follows by reduction to and from timed-automata model checking.

### Theorem 3: Compositionality for Polypharmacy (the crown jewel)

**Statement.** Let P₁, …, Pₙ be PTAs for N concurrent guidelines sharing a common PK state space. Let φ be a safety property that is *pharmacokinetically monotone* (formally: if a PK state s satisfies φ and s' ≥ s componentwise, then s' satisfies φ — capturing the intuition that "higher drug concentrations are more dangerous"). Then φ holds on the product PTA P₁ ⊗ … ⊗ Pₙ if and only if:
1. φ holds on each Pᵢ individually, AND
2. For every pair (Pᵢ, Pⱼ) sharing at least one PK variable (drug metabolized by the same enzyme), φ holds on the pairwise product Pᵢ ⊗ Pⱼ.

**Corollary.** Verification of N guidelines reduces from exponential (product of all N) to O(N²) pairwise checks. For 20 guidelines, this is the difference between 2²⁰ ≈ 10⁶ states and 190 pairwise checks.

**Why this is the crown jewel.** This theorem is what makes polypharmacy verification *practical*. Without it, verifying a patient on 5+ guidelines is intractable. With it, the problem becomes routine. The Metzler monotonicity of PK dynamics is the key enabler — it ensures that the "worst case" for drug concentrations is compositional (the highest concentration of drug A when taking drugs B and C together is at most the sum of the increases caused by B and C individually, because Metzler systems propagate bounds monotonically).

**Honesty about limitations.** Pharmacokinetic monotonicity does *not* hold for enzyme induction (which decreases the concentration of co-administered drugs) or for pharmacodynamic antagonism. The theorem covers CYP inhibition interactions (the most common and dangerous class) but not all interaction types. For non-monotone interactions, the system falls back to monolithic product verification with CEGAR — slower but still sound.

### Theorem 4: Counterexample Minimality

**Statement.** When MTL model checking fails, a minimum-length counterexample (shortest violating run in terms of discrete transitions) can be computed in time polynomial in the PTA size times the model-checking complexity. The proof uses a shortest-path algorithm on the pharmacokinetic region graph, modified to account for continuous PK dynamics between transitions.

### Theorem 5: Conservative Abstraction for Nonlinear PK

**Statement.** For drugs with nonlinear PK (Michaelis-Menten elimination: phenytoin, theophylline, warfarin at high doses), define a linearization-based conservative abstraction that over-approximates the nonlinear dynamics by a Metzler linear system. If the abstracted PTA satisfies a safety property, the concrete PTA also satisfies it. The conservatism gap (the set of patient parameters spuriously flagged as unsafe) has measure bounded by O(ε · vol(Φ)) where ε depends on the Lipschitz constant of the nonlinear terms relative to the linear approximation.

## Best Paper Argument

A best-paper committee selects this paper for five reasons:

1. **The problem is undeniable.** 125,000 annual deaths from adverse drug events. Guidelines published in isolation with no cross-checking. Computable guidelines becoming the decision substrate via FHIR/CQL adoption mandated by 21st Century Cures Act. Every clinician, informatician, and EHR vendor recognizes this problem. The paper doesn't argue that the problem exists — it solves it.

2. **The result is dramatic and automated.** "We formally verified 190 pairwise guideline interactions covering the 20 most prevalent Medicare comorbidities and found N previously uncharacterized conflict states, M of which involve commonly co-prescribed medications with confirmed severity in DrugBank and statistically significant FAERS signals." No reviewer can dismiss a paper that finds dozens of real, dangerous, previously unknown clinical guideline conflicts using automated formal methods and validates them against independent pharmacovigilance data.

3. **The mathematical contribution is genuine and load-bearing.** The PTA formalism, its decidability, and the compositionality theorem are new results in the hybrid systems/model checking intersection. They are not "call Z3" — they require novel proofs about Metzler-matrix dynamics, zonotopic reachable sets, and pharmacokinetic monotonicity. The compositionality theorem is the practical enabler that makes polypharmacy verification tractable. Without the math, the tool doesn't scale. A committee that values formal contributions will recognize the theorems; a committee that values practical impact will recognize the system. Both are satisfied.

4. **It explicitly surpasses the prior art.** The paper positions against TMR (atemporal, pairwise), GLARE (single-path, not exhaustive), UPPAAL clinical studies (hand-modeled, single-guideline), and CAREPATH (execution only) with a clear capability matrix showing what GuardPharma does that none of them can: automated parsing + N-guideline composition + temporal PK reasoning + exhaustive verification + clinical severity ranking.

5. **It bridges two communities.** Formal methods and clinical informatics have operated in parallel for decades. This paper is the bridge — it takes the most powerful technique from formal verification (compositional model checking) and applies it to the most urgent problem in clinical informatics (polypharmacy safety). Neither community alone could have produced it. A JAMIA or AMIA best-paper committee that sees formal methods saving lives will not pass it over.

## Laptop CPU Feasibility

**Why this runs on a laptop.** Every computational component is CPU-native:

- **CQL parsing:** Compiler-style parsing is CPU-bound; ANTLR generates efficient parsers that process the entire CDS Connect corpus in seconds.
- **PK ODE solving:** Linear compartmental models (Metzler systems) have closed-form matrix-exponential solutions. For 1–3 compartment models (the vast majority), this is a 3×3 matrix exponential — microseconds per evaluation. Even interval ODE solving (for δ-decidability) is fast for small-dimensional systems.
- **Zonotopic reachability:** Zonotope operations (Minkowski sum, linear map) are polynomial in the zonotope order and state dimension. For m = 5 drugs, the zonotope is 5-dimensional — trivially manageable.
- **Model checking with CEGAR:** BDD-based model checking is CPU-native (BuDDy, CUDD). SAT-based bounded model checking uses MiniSat/CaDiCaL, which are pure CPU. CEGAR dramatically reduces the explored state space.
- **SMT solving:** Z3 runs on CPU with no GPU support even available.
- **FAERS processing:** 20M+ adverse event reports are large but structured; disproportionality analysis (PRR, ROR) is SQL-level computation, easily streaming on a laptop with modest memory.

**Memory budget:** The largest in-memory structure is the product PTA region graph. With compositionality (Theorem 3), we never build the full N-guideline product — only pairwise products, each with at most ~10⁶ reachable regions (after CEGAR pruning). At ~100 bytes/region, this is ~100MB per pairwise check, well within 16GB laptop RAM.

## Key Risks and Mitigations

| # | Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|---|------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | **Decidability proof fails for full Metzler class.** The zonotopic stabilization argument may require additional conditions beyond Metzler (e.g., asymptotic stability of M, which means all drugs are eventually eliminated — physiologically true but mathematically a constraint). | High | Fall back to δ-decidability (dReal-style) which is *already proven* for general nonlinear hybrid systems. The δ parameter is set to the minimum pharmacologically meaningful concentration, so δ-decidability is clinically equivalent to exact decidability. The paper presents both: exact decidability for the Metzler subclass, δ-decidability for the general case. |
| 2 | **Compositionality theorem's monotonicity condition is too restrictive.** Enzyme induction and pharmacodynamic antagonism violate monotonicity, excluding some important drug interactions from the compositional speedup. | Medium | Characterize precisely which drug interaction classes satisfy monotonicity (CYP inhibition, transporter competition, additive QT prolongation) and which don't (enzyme induction, receptor antagonism). Report coverage: we expect ≥ 70% of clinically significant drug interactions involve CYP inhibition and are thus monotone. For non-monotone interactions, fall back to monolithic verification with CEGAR (slower but sound). |
| 3 | **Small computable guideline corpus.** The publicly available CQL guideline ecosystem is growing but modest. If only 50 guidelines are parseable, the evaluation is limited. | Medium | The automated CQL compilation pipeline (Subsystem 3) aggressively harvests from CDS Connect, CQFramework, CMS eCQM specifications (which number in the hundreds), and academic CQL repositories. Additionally, we encode well-known clinical decision rules (Wells score, CHADS₂-VASc, MELD, etc.) directly as PTA from their published algorithms. Target: 300+ guideline artifacts covering the 20 most prevalent Medicare comorbidities. |
| 4 | **Formal conflicts ≠ clinical significance.** Many formal conflicts may be trivially resolvable by a clinician (e.g., dose adjustment resolves the contradiction). If the "novel findings" are mostly trivial, the paper's dramatic claim collapses. | High | The clinical significance filter (Subsystem 10) explicitly addresses this. Conflicts are ranked by severity using DrugBank interaction severity, Beers Criteria classification, FAERS disproportionality signal, and comorbidity prevalence. The paper reports results *stratified by severity tier*, not as a single undifferentiated count. The headline number is "N critical conflicts" (those involving known-dangerous drug combinations or significant FAERS signals), not "N total formal conflicts." Experiment E8 quantifies the filter's value by showing that a substantial fraction of formal conflicts are correctly classified as informational. |
| 5 | **FAERS confounding.** FAERS is a spontaneous reporting system with massive confounding (sicker patients take more drugs and report more events). A "validated" conflict may be a confounding artifact. | Medium | Use established pharmacovigilance methodology: (a) disproportionality analysis with multiple methods (PRR, ROR, BCPNN) requiring concordance, (b) stratification by age, sex, and indication, (c) negative controls (drug pairs with no pharmacological interaction mechanism that should show no signal). The paper explicitly characterizes the FAERS validation as *signal detection* (hypothesis-generating), not *causal confirmation*, and reports effect sizes alongside statistical significance. |
| 6 | **CQL language coverage.** CQL is a rich language; some constructs (aggregate expressions, complex retrieve queries with date filters, multi-source queries) may be difficult to encode precisely in PTA. | Medium | Implement the CQL 1.5 specification completely for the *expression* language (arithmetic, comparison, Boolean, temporal, interval operators), which covers >90% of CQL guideline logic. For constructs that cannot be precisely encoded (e.g., aggregate functions over unbounded collections), use sound over-approximation (any recommendation that *might* fire in the original CQL is guaranteed to fire in the PTA). Document the exact CQL fragment supported and the approximation boundaries. |
| 7 | **Region count explosion.** Even with CEGAR, the pharmacokinetic region graph for 5+ concurrent drugs may be too large for exhaustive exploration on a laptop. | Medium | Compositionality (Theorem 3) is the primary mitigation: we never explore the 5-drug product, only 2-drug products. Each 2-drug product has K/δ ~ 10³ per drug, giving ~10⁶ regions before CEGAR. After CEGAR prunes clinically irrelevant regions (concentrations in the therapeutic range for both drugs are safe and need not be explored), the effective region count drops by 2–3 orders of magnitude. If this is still too large, use bounded model checking (SAT-based) with a horizon of T = 365 days (one year of treatment), which avoids enumerating all regions. |
