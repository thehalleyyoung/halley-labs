# Review: MARACE — Interaction Race Detection via Happens-Before–Aware Abstract Interpretation for Multi-Agent RL Safety Verification

**Reviewer:** Joseph S. Chang (Automated Reasoning and Logic Expert)
**Score: 7/10**

---

MARACE's proof-theoretic foundations are ambitious and largely well-constructed, but several critical soundness gaps deserve attention. The separation theorem comprises three results: non-projectability (Theorem 1), statistical intractability (Theorem 2), and PSPACE-hardness (Theorem 3). Theorem 1's proof by explicit construction over piecewise-linear policies in 2D continuous state spaces is clean — demonstrating that per-agent safe regions can intersect in a joint-predicate-violating region is a standard but necessary geometric argument. Theorem 2's bound on sampling failure probability is a straightforward application of concentration inequalities to schedule-dependent events, though the O((Δt/T)^k) scaling for k agents provides a useful quantitative characterization of the "curse of asynchrony." Theorem 3, the PSPACE-hardness result, is the most significant theoretical contribution: the reduction from timed automata reachability with N clocks to interaction race detection encodes clock valuations as schedule timing offsets and automaton configurations as joint policy states. The claim that single-agent verification remains in P while the interaction problem is PSPACE-hard establishes a genuine complexity-theoretic separation — but the reduction's correctness depends on faithful encoding of clock resets and guard conditions, and the paper provides only a high-level sketch rather than a complete proof. For a best-paper submission to CAV, this proof must be fully formalized.

The logical foundations of the verification pipeline have a more concerning gap: the trusted computing base (TCB) is implicitly large and nowhere explicitly analyzed. The soundness chain runs from the specification language through the temporal logic parser, the HB construction, the abstract interpretation framework, the compositional decomposition, and finally the proof certificate generator — any bug in any of these 47,445 LoC invalidates the certificates. Compare this to tools like CBMC or SPIN, which have decades of testing and well-understood TCBs, or to certified verifiers like CompCert/CakeML where the TCB is reduced to the proof assistant's kernel. MARACE's proof certificates (`reporting/proof_certificates.py`, 2,008 LoC) are "machine-checkable" but no external checker is specified, no formal proof format is adopted (Alethe, LFSC, CPC), and the certificates are generated by the same codebase that performs the verification — this is self-certification, not independent verification. The assume-guarantee composition's soundness (Theorem: any global race projects onto some interaction group) relies on the causal closure property of HB graph connected components, which is standard for message-passing systems but requires careful argument for environment-mediated causality where the causal chain traverses continuous physical dynamics. The SMT-based contract discharge for linear arithmetic contracts is decidable, but the contracts themselves are generated by the abstract interpreter — are the generated contracts always in the linear fragment, or can nonlinear policy dynamics produce contracts outside the decidable fragment? The denotational semantics of the specification language should be formalized to ensure that the temporal logic operators compose with the HB-aware abstract interpretation in a semantically consistent way — currently, the BNF grammar and the abstract interpretation framework appear to be developed independently.
